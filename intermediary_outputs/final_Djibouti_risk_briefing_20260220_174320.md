Escalating communal tensions and violence—particularly in the capital—raise a direct risk to social cohesion and can quickly disrupt service delivery, beneficiary outreach, and public confidence in state-supported programs. Several operations are structurally exposed because they rely on transparent and accepted community/household selection for transfers and community investments; the PAD explicitly warns that cash/nutrition support, productive inclusion, and registry strengthening can trigger elite capture, exclusion, and “social conflict” if targeting is contested or seen as unfair [P506969 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf). Other operations are similarly exposed to unrest dynamics in Djibouti City: the food security response is explicitly framed around preventing demonstrations and economic disruption if food availability tightens [P178988 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf), and major agri-food coordination and investments include a peri-urban Djibouti City growth pole, making implementation vulnerable to any mobility/communications disruption in the capital [P178836 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf). No ISR/aide-memoire extracts were provided to confirm whether this risk has already disrupted activities; however, the PADs indicate that both realized shocks (food price spikes and perceived inequities) and forward-looking pressures (further polarization and contested benefit allocation) could rapidly translate into implementation delays and reputational risk.

Heightened political volatility around the April 2026 electoral cycle—and associated spillover concerns—could disrupt reform momentum, inter-agency coordination, and cross-border operational collaboration at exactly the time several projects require sustained senior-level attention. The agri-food project makes this exposure explicit through “critical assumptions” that include no major political crisis during implementation, implying schedule and delivery vulnerability if instability rises [P178836 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf). Similarly, the housing finance operation’s results depend on tight coordination among multiple public and quasi-public actors and governance adjustments to key institutions—conditions that can weaken in periods of political contestation [P176772 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-38023395-68a8-4eb6-8dd3-05bbe5a8c9ed.pdf). Trade facilitation is also exposed because Djibouti–Ethiopia-facing activities are conditioned on a satisfactory mutual understanding between customs authorities, creating a direct channel through which political instability could pause implementation and disbursements [P174300 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes). No ISR/aide-memoire extracts were provided to evidence whether election-related instability is already delaying decisions; nonetheless, the PADs point to a credible forward-looking pathway where reform bandwidth narrows, coordination weakens, and bilateral processes stall as the political calendar tightens.

A constrained governance environment—characterized by limited civic participation, centralized decision-making, and weak accountability—creates material operational risk for projects that depend on transparent rules, predictable approvals, and credible institutions. This susceptibility is visible across operations: fiduciary and implementation arrangements are explicitly vulnerable to “line ministry interference,” which can undermine autonomy, controls, and timely execution [P506969 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf); governance reforms also face a weak oversight ecosystem and “limited participation of, or contributions from, non-state actors,” reducing the feedback loops needed to sustain reform traction [P180696 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf). Where project outcomes rely on independent data production, PADs also highlight risks to institutional autonomy and credibility if agencies cannot remain insulated from political/external influence [P171777 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087c0f9e4&stream=Yes). This risk is not only forward-looking: implementation evidence indicates that delayed adoption of a key digital/telecom legal framework (the Code du Numérique) is already blocking regulatory and operational activities, demonstrating how governance bottlenecks can concretely stall enabling reforms needed for delivery [P174461 – Aide](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-a6aeb6a6-887c-41f9-8eb7-fb31c9e7d652.pdf).

Large-scale displacement and refugee-hosting pressures—compounded by recurrent drought and limited fiscal/service capacity—risk overloading local systems and reducing the effectiveness of refugee-inclusive delivery models that the portfolio increasingly relies on. Multiple PADs describe how inflows place “substantial demands on public services and local communities,” creating a systemic constraint that can depress coverage, quality, and response speed during crises [P509082 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf), including in underserved and refugee-hosting regions where services are already strained [P506969 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf). Operationally, refugee inclusion is also vulnerable to practical access barriers: the TVET project’s PAD notes that lack of physical connectivity and internet access in remote refugee-hosting locations is already impeding ICT-related training delivery [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf). Beyond service delivery, displacement and mobility also complicate monitoring and results verification in data-dependent operations, with the statistics project highlighting the difficulty of tracking and sampling mobile/refugee and transit populations [P171777 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087c0f9e4&stream=Yes). No ISR/aide-memoire extracts were provided to confirm the extent to which these pressures are currently reducing refugee uptake or slowing results; however, the PAD evidence points to both a present constraint (connectivity and service strain) and a forward-looking risk that further inflows and drought shocks will widen last-mile delivery gaps and weaken outcome measurement.
