# Guatemala FCV Portfolio Briefing

---

**1. Political-Criminal Capture of Judicial Institutions and Its Threat to Project Governance**

Guatemala faces a systemic risk of elite capture of its judicial and prosecutorial institutions, with the 2026 cycle of appointments to the Attorney General's Office, Constitutional Court, Supreme Electoral Tribunal, and Comptroller General representing a concentrated moment of institutional vulnerability. Corrupt political and economic actors have historically exploited opaque nominating commission processes to install loyalists, and the UN Special Rapporteur on the Independence of Judges and Lawyers has explicitly warned that these processes are vulnerable to interference. The IACHR has found that some state institutions are already operating as instruments of selective persecution rather than impartial governance. Both projects in the portfolio carry meaningful exposure to this risk. The Guatemala Smart Public Financial Management Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> depends on sustained political commitment within MINFIN and a functional legal framework to protect procurement, financial accountability, and reform momentum; if judicial institutions are captured, the legal protections underpinning these mechanisms could be dismantled. The Rural Roads Connectivity Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a> relies on institutional coordination with DGC, CIV, municipalities, and community councils, and its resettlement, environmental compliance, and contractor accountability frameworks all assume functional judicial oversight—assumptions that become precarious if the attorney general and Constitutional Court are co-opted. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization is yet available for either project, but the forward-looking risk is acute given the February–August 2026 appointment timeline.

**2. Political-Criminal Networks Instrumentalizing Gang Violence to Destabilize Reform**

A distinct and particularly dangerous risk in Guatemala is the documented entanglement between organized crime and political elites who have a vested interest in preventing governance reform. Former anti-corruption prosecutor Juan Francisco Sandoval and President Arévalo himself have characterized the January 2026 gang offensive—which triggered a 30-day state of siege—not merely as a criminal security crisis but as a deliberate operation by political-criminal networks seeking to destabilize the government ahead of critical judicial appointments. The IACHR has concluded that the Attorney General's Office has been coopted to operate as an instrument of selective persecution, and the US, EU, and UK have all imposed sanctions against Attorney General Porras. This environment poses a direct threat to both portfolio projects. The Smart PFM Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> is explicitly designed to enhance fiscal transparency and accountability—objectives that directly threaten the opacity enabling political-criminal networks to extract rents from public finances, making the project and its implementing officials potential targets of orchestrated disruption. The Rural Roads Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a> depends on stable governance and institutional cooperation across a large territory; if political-criminal networks continue to instrumentalize violence to create emergency conditions, government resources and attention may be diverted away from project implementation priorities. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded to date, but the structural incentive for these networks to obstruct reform-oriented programming is well-established.

**3. Gang-Driven Violence, Extortion, and Security Fragility in Project Implementation Areas**

Guatemala's security environment has deteriorated sharply, with extortion complaints reaching 25,151 in 2024—a 39% increase from 2023—and homicide rates rising 21% in the first half of 2025. MS-13 and Barrio 18 operate large-scale extortion networks primarily from within prisons, and Mexico-based cartels have expanded their presence along the Guatemala–Mexico border, with over 21 documented blind spots enabling trafficking. The January 2026 state of siege resulted in roughly 3,300 detentions but analysts warn that structural vulnerabilities remain unaddressed. The Rural Roads Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a> carries the most direct exposure: its intervention areas in Huehuetenango and San Marcos fall within the "Poppy Triangle" and key trafficking corridors where the Sinaloa Cartel and CJNG are competing for control, and the PAD explicitly acknowledges weak state presence in these zones—road construction activities, equipment, and labor mobilization are typical extortion targets in gang-controlled areas. The same project's reliance on community participation through COMUDES and COCODES is vulnerable to gang intimidation of community leaders, and its plans to hire local labor could be disrupted by gang recruitment pressure or extortion demands on contractors <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The Smart PFM Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> faces a secondary but real exposure through its ICT infrastructure modernization component: the PAD documents over 8,000 cyberattacks on the Tax Administration in 2021, ransomware on the Foreign Ministry in 2022, and attacks on Guatecompras and SICOIN in 2023, and ongoing emergency security measures could disrupt the movement of technical staff and procurement timelines required for complex systems implementation. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded, but the security trajectory is worsening.

**4. Criminalization of Indigenous Leaders, Human Rights Defenders, and Community Advocates**

Guatemala's Attorney General's Office has pursued a systematic campaign of politically motivated prosecutions against journalists, Indigenous leaders, and human rights defenders, with UDEFEGUA documenting 4,520 attacks against defenders between January and September 2025 alone and Global Witness recording a sharp increase in murders of land and environmental defenders from 4 in 2023 to 20 in 2024. The IACHR has found that lack of legal certainty, combined with coordinated actions of private firms, the judiciary, and the public prosecutor's office, has led to misappropriation of ancestral lands and forced evictions of Indigenous communities. The Inter-American Court ruled in December 2024 that Guatemala violated the rights of the Maya Q'eqchi' people in El Estor by failing to title their lands and conduct adequate prior consultation regarding mining. The Rural Roads Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a> carries substantial exposure through multiple channels: its involuntary resettlement activities—affecting dwellings and commercial stands in rural rights-of-way—could expose affected persons and community advocates to threats if they resist displacement or assert their rights, and the PAD's culturally appropriate grievance redress mechanisms assume that Indigenous community leaders can safely advocate for their interests without facing criminal prosecution. The project's special focus on Indigenous peoples, women, and vulnerable populations, combined with its community mobilization approach, could inadvertently expose beneficiaries to retaliation in an environment where Indigenous activists face terrorism charges for peaceful protest <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The Smart PFM Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> faces a related risk: if reform-oriented officials and technical staff within MINFIN and the PIU face legal intimidation, recruitment and retention of qualified personnel becomes significantly more difficult, undermining the institutional capacity that the project's implementation model depends upon. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded for either project.

**5. USAID Dismantlement and Humanitarian Funding Collapse Weakening the Development Ecosystem**

The Trump administration's termination of USAID has created a significant gap in Guatemala's development and governance ecosystem, disrupting protection services, justice programming, climate-smart agriculture supply chains, and civil society oversight capacity. The EU has become the sole donor funding protection programs in areas like Huehuetenango, and the Norwegian Refugee Council has closed its Guatemala operations entirely due to funding cuts. The 2025 Humanitarian Response Plan received less than 20% of its required USD 100.6 million. This collapse in complementary financing and civil society capacity creates direct risks for both portfolio projects. The Smart PFM Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> relies on stakeholder engagement and civil society oversight mechanisms for accountability and sustainability; the loss of USAID-funded governance and justice programming has weakened the external accountability environment that these mechanisms depend upon, and the project's moderate stakeholder risk rating may underestimate the difficulty of sustaining meaningful civil society engagement in a depleted institutional landscape. The Rural Roads Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a> operates in Huehuetenango—one of the departments most affected by USAID's withdrawal—and its community participation framework and grievance redress mechanisms could leave vulnerable beneficiaries, particularly women and girls facing gender-based violence, without the protection and psychosocial support services that previously complemented project activities. The Smart PFM Project also faces a macroeconomic dimension of this risk: remittances account for 19.5% of GDP and drive 47.8% of government net income through VAT, and the combination of mass deportations and a 1% remittance tax under US legislation is projected to reduce remittance flows precisely when the project requires sustained government investment in PFM modernization <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded, but the funding environment is deteriorating rapidly.
