Lebanon's portfolio of World Bank projects faces five distinct and compounding country-level risks that are simultaneously shaping project design vulnerabilities and materializing as concrete implementation failures across the active portfolio.

**Active Full-Scale Military Conflict and Mass Displacement**

The most acute risk confronting the Lebanon portfolio is the full-scale Israeli military offensive that resumed on March 2, 2026, involving ground operations along multiple axes in southern Lebanon, systematic destruction of bridges and villages, and airstrikes reaching central Beirut. Over 1,497 people have been killed and more than 1.2 million displaced—one in five Lebanese residents—with displacement orders covering approximately 14 percent of Lebanon's territory, including southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and Beirut's southern suburbs. Every project in the portfolio carries explicit PAD-level exposure to this risk. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> was designed with geopolitical risk mitigation including exclusion of disputed territories and Lebanese Army clearance requirements for explosive remnants of war, but the March 2026 escalation has expanded Israeli operations far beyond previously anticipated boundaries, rendering these mitigations insufficient. The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a> explicitly acknowledged that works remain suspended and implementing agency operations severely disrupted, with a PAD-level commitment that no works would commence until conflict had ended and sites were declared safe. The Solid Waste Management and Circular Economy Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a> has project locations in the Union of Municipalities of Tyre, Abbasiyeh, Sahel el Zahrani, and Nabatieh—all now within or adjacent to active conflict zones—making field activities for waste characterization, dumpsite rehabilitation, and facility upgrades operationally impossible. Implementation evidence confirms these risks are materializing: the Beqaa Valley sewage network project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a> saw contractors suspend all works due to escalating military activities, and the project team was forced to conduct supervision missions virtually while prohibiting any work near areas of active shelling. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a> shows persistent high political and governance risk ratings, with stakeholder engagement severely constrained by security conditions. The Social Protection and Social Services Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-bde1794c-f97c-48b9-a56e-1949644a584e.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – Aide Memoire</a> documented sporadic armed strikes on South Lebanon and Beirut's southern suburbs as a direct operational constraint on service delivery to GBV survivors, mental health beneficiaries, and displaced populations.

**Destruction of Critical Infrastructure and Collapse of Public Services**

A second distinct risk is the systematic destruction of civilian infrastructure—bridges, water systems, healthcare facilities, and housing—compounded by the pre-existing collapse of public service delivery. Israeli forces have destroyed most bridges south of the Litani River, isolating over 150,000 people; UNICEF recorded 17 incidents affecting water systems across four governorates; and 53 healthcare workers have been killed with 82 attacks on emergency medical services documented since March 2. This risk intersects with Lebanon's pre-existing infrastructure fragility, where the electricity sector has cost $40 billion since the civil war while providing minimal service, and municipalities lack resources to maintain basic utilities. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> targets rapid road access restoration and multi-sectoral restoration of critical infrastructure including water, energy, health, education, and transport, but the PAD's Build Back Better approach assumes a stable enough environment to prevent reconstruction from being immediately destroyed—an assumption now invalidated. The Solid Waste Management Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a> requires construction of 200 kilometers of sewerage networks across Beqaa Valley districts, with the destruction of bridges south of the Litani directly impeding access to construction sites and material transport. The Beirut Urban Resilience Project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088d3d75f&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P176577 – PAD</a> depends on functioning water, sanitation, and electricity for housing rehabilitation works, while the PAD's environmental risk assessment identified traffic and logistics risks during waste transport that are now compounded by destroyed road networks. The Social Recovery and Protection Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-6dadd285-c9a6-442f-8faa-3a5cdb52d0ab.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – PAD</a> operates safe shelters and delivers services through primary healthcare centers and mobile medical units, with 51 percent of surveyed refugees accessing healthcare via PHCCs—facilities now closed across conflict zones. Implementation evidence shows these risks are actively materializing: the Beqaa Valley sewage project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a> documented that most bridges south of the Litani have been destroyed, directly preventing access to project sites; the Beirut Urban Resilience Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176577-6c3b4a27-9cf9-4050-9a10-ed310209e452.pdf" target="_blank">P176577 – Aide Memoire</a> recorded permit approval delays due to reduced public sector working hours and capacity constraints; and the Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a> confirmed that NGO partners face severe operational constraints as public service infrastructure collapses around them.

**Mass Displacement, Refugee Secondary Displacement, and Protection Crises**

Lebanon's displacement crisis—now exceeding 1.2 million newly displaced persons since March 2, 2026, on top of pre-existing displacement from 2023–2024 hostilities—constitutes a third distinct risk with profound implications for project targeting, beneficiary identification, and protection safeguards. Over 562,000 persons have crossed into Syria, including approximately 180,000 Syrians and 28,000 Lebanese, while more than 90,000 refugees inside Lebanon have been displaced a second time. The UN has tracked spikes in hate speech and sectarian rhetoric targeting internally displaced persons, and UNICEF reports that 20 percent of casualties in the first week of the current conflict were children. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> was designed around 900,000 displaced persons from the 2023–2024 conflict, with Component 1 including decommissioning of IDP centers—an activity now premature given that 1.2 million additional persons have been displaced and collective shelters are at or beyond capacity. The Vaccination and Health System Resilience Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9c1bfedd-5761-415c-9838-dc89cfe988b8.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – PAD</a> targets approximately 4.7 million beneficiaries including 1.5 million Syrian and Palestinian refugees, with mobile vaccination units and targeted awareness campaigns designed for refugee-dense areas in Akkar and Arsal; the secondary displacement of 90,000 refugees and outflow of 180,000 Syrians directly undermines beneficiary registries and vaccination continuity. The Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-6dadd285-c9a6-442f-8faa-3a5cdb52d0ab.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – PAD</a> explicitly targets Syrian refugees as cross-cutting beneficiaries across all workstreams, noting that 86 percent of recorded GBV incidents in the first half of 2023 were displaced Syrians and 35 percent of mental health hotline callers are refugees—populations now experiencing compounded trauma and secondary displacement. Implementation evidence confirms materialization: the Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a> documented zero enrollment of displaced Syrian beneficiaries against a target of 180,000, reflecting the operational impossibility of maintaining beneficiary identification amid mass population movements; the Vaccination Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178587-8133ed2c-dff3-4ee7-a615-063ca255128b.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – ISR</a> shows persistent gaps in refugee hospital utilization tracking as populations become increasingly mobile and inaccessible; and the Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-bde1794c-f97c-48b9-a56e-1949644a584e.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – Aide Memoire</a> recorded that NGO core funding losses are reducing the capacity of civil society organizations to respond to the acute humanitarian crisis affecting displaced populations.

**State Sovereignty Deficit, Hezbollah Disarmament Impasse, and Institutional Paralysis**

A fourth risk—distinct from the military conflict itself—is the Lebanese state's demonstrated inability to enforce its own authority, exemplified by the government's unprecedented March 2, 2026 ban on Hezbollah military activities that the Lebanese Armed Forces have refused to enforce. The LAF commander reportedly objected to direct confrontation with Hezbollah, fearing desertions by Shia soldiers, while Hezbollah has continued daily rocket salvos and maintained armed resistance in southern Lebanon. This sovereignty deficit compounds pre-existing institutional fragility, including the Council for Development and Reconstruction's documented procurement approval timelines of nine months and bid evaluation periods of two to eight months. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> depends on CDR institutional capacity and multi-ministerial coordination, with the PAD identifying institutional capacity as a high risk and noting CDR's limited capacity for a project of this scale; the state's inability to enforce disarmament decrees signals a governance collapse that directly undermines CDR's authority and operational coherence. The Public Financial Management and Fiscal Transparency Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-5067fa60-78e4-4f5e-b6c1-5a428d852b01.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – PAD</a> aims to restore basic functions of domestic revenue mobilization and accountable allocation of public resources, explicitly seeking to renew the social contract between state and citizens—objectives that are fundamentally undermined when the state cannot enforce its own monopoly on the use of force. The Renewable Energy Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-c260299b-4517-48c7-a0c1-b95eb42d17a7.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – PAD</a> depends on EDL and LRA as implementing entities operating under state authority, with the PAD acknowledging that the state's inability to enforce monopoly on force creates risks for project implementation in areas where non-state actors exercise influence. Implementation evidence confirms materialization: the Public Financial Management Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a> shows persistent high political and governance risk ratings with parliamentary ratification of the IBRD financing agreement still pending, reflecting the same institutional paralysis that prevents enforcement of disarmament decrees; the Renewable Energy Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180501-1bd1cf0b-8078-42e6-a4bc-c43a782224ca.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – ISR</a> documents high institutional capacity risk for EDL and LRA, with implementing agencies forced to allocate limited human and financial resources to emergency response rather than project execution; and the Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a> confirms that government partnerships with the Ministry of Public Health, Ministry of Justice, and Ministry of Social Affairs are compromised by the state's demonstrated inability to enforce policy decisions.

**Parliamentary Election Postponement, Democratic Backsliding, and Economic Fragility**

The fifth distinct risk combines Lebanon's parliamentary election postponement—parliament voted on March 9, 2026 to extend its own mandate by two years, cancelling elections scheduled for May 2026—with the pre-existing economic fragility that the renewed conflict has now reversed. Lebanon's economy had registered a cautious 3.5 percent real GDP growth in 2025, with the World Bank projecting 4 percent growth in 2026 contingent on reform momentum and political stability; both conditions have been fundamentally undermined. The parliamentary extension eliminates a key accountability mechanism and risks entrenching the political class that has historically prevented structural reform, while the economic reversal compounds a pre-existing crisis of 38 percent cumulative GDP decline since 2019, a 357 percent debt-to-GDP ratio, and $72 billion in accumulated banking sector losses. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> identified political and governance risk as high, noting that past challenges with loan agreement signing and parliamentary ratification have caused delays, and that parliamentary ratification of the loan agreement is a prerequisite for project effectiveness—a risk now compounded by the unconstitutional term extension. The Beqaa Valley Wastewater Management Project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08452b660&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P147854 – PAD</a> requires continuous engagement with the Council of Ministers and Parliament to maintain political support, with the PAD noting that political instability and absence of presidential elections since May 2014 had already impacted government priorities; the March 2026 term extension deepens this pattern. The Agricultural and Rural Development Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-c14c98c6-d426-4177-9a31-f1acaae2fb97.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – PAD</a> was designed based on World Bank January 2026 projections of 4 percent growth in 2026, with all project disbursements denominated in USD and beneficiary payments in USD as mitigation for currency risk—mitigations that assume continued access to USD liquidity and stable exchange rates now threatened by economic collapse. Implementation evidence confirms materialization: the Beirut Urban Resilience Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176577-4f8e4069-1a4c-4b78-b5bb-344e2c6a59af.pdf" target="_blank">P176577 – ISR</a> shows macroeconomic risk escalating from moderate to high, with the project's macroeconomic operating environment deteriorating as the January 2026 growth projections have been invalidated; the Agricultural Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a> documents zero disbursement (0.25 percent) as of August 2025 and zero farmers reached, zero SME loans approved, and zero infrastructure completed as of January 2025, reflecting the combined effect of political paralysis and economic collapse on project execution; and the Public Financial Management Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a> confirms that parliamentary ratification of the IBRD financing agreement remains pending, with the same institutional gridlock that enabled the March 2026 term extension now blocking the foundational fiscal governance project from achieving effectiveness.
