Lebanon's portfolio of World Bank projects faces five distinct and compounding country-level risks that are simultaneously shaping project design vulnerabilities and materializing as concrete implementation failures across the active portfolio.

Active Full-Scale Military Conflict and Physical Access Denial

The most immediate and severe risk confronting the portfolio is the full-scale Israeli military offensive that resumed on March 2, 2026, involving ground operations along multiple axes in southern Lebanon, systematic destruction of bridges over the Litani River, airstrikes on Beirut's southern suburbs and the Bekaa Valley, and displacement orders covering approximately 14 percent of Lebanese territory. This security environment directly threatens every project with field operations in conflict-affected areas. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project P509428 – PAD explicitly acknowledges that geopolitical risks from Middle East conflict may impede implementation in border areas, with project locations spanning southern Lebanon and the Bekaa Valley; the PAD requires Lebanese Army clearance for explosive remnants of war before any investment, and the Bank's own supervision capacity is constrained by security restrictions on physical verification. The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project P504170 – PAD notes that works remain suspended and implementing agency operations severely disrupted, with a mitigation strategy requiring that no works commence until conflict has ended and sites are safe. The Beqaa Water and Wastewater Project P147854 – PAD identifies that ongoing conflict creates security concerns in the Beqaa region, potentially limiting contractor mobility and Bank supervision missions. These risks are fully materializing: contractors suspended all sewage network works in the Beqaa due to escalating military activities P147854 – Aide Memoire; the LEAP reconstruction program has come to a complete halt, with the UN Special Coordinator confirming that progress collapsed following the March 2026 escalation P509428 – ISR; project teams across the portfolio have been forced to shift to virtual supervision modalities and cannot conduct physical site verification P147854 – Aide Memoire P172770 – Aide Memoire; and unexploded ordnance clearance requirements are now blocking resumption of works at multiple sites P147854 – Aide Memoire. The Solid Waste Management and UPOPs Reduction Project P172770 – PAD has seen its South Lebanon interventions entirely suspended, with environmental and social impact assessments for the Nabatieh sanitary landfill delayed by security conditions that prevent field access P172770 – Aide Memoire.

Mass Displacement Overwhelming Beneficiary Targeting and Service Delivery

More than 1.2 million people—one in five Lebanese residents—have been forcibly displaced since March 2, 2026, with displacement orders covering southern Lebanon, Beirut's southern suburbs, and the Bekaa Valley. This displacement crisis is not merely a humanitarian emergency but a structural implementation failure for projects whose design assumptions rest on stable beneficiary populations. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project P509428 – PAD was designed around an area-based approach targeting 1.2 million affected people in priority conflict-affected districts, with Component 1 including decommissioning of IDP centers; the March 2026 escalation has created a new wave of 1.2 million displaced persons, rendering baseline targeting data obsolete and making decommissioning of IDP centers premature or impossible. The Social Protection Enhancement Project P173367 – PAD relies on home verification visits to confirm all applicants for cash transfers to 147,000 Lebanese families, a methodology that is structurally incompatible with mass displacement; the project also explicitly targets Syrian displaced persons alongside Lebanese nationals, but over 200,000 people—predominantly Syrians—have crossed into Syria since March 2026, removing a core beneficiary population from reach. The Lebanon Health Resilience Project P178587 – PAD targets approximately 4.7 million people including 1.5 million Syrian and Palestinian refugees, with mobile vaccination units and targeted outreach designed for populations in areas with high refugee concentration; the secondary displacement of over 90,000 refugees within Lebanon and the cross-border flight of 180,000 Syrians directly undermines beneficiary registries and vaccination continuity. Implementation evidence confirms these vulnerabilities are materializing: the Social Protection Enhancement Project has been unable to maintain cash transfer delivery to beneficiaries in displacement-affected areas, with the Bekaa Valley—one of the project's priority poor regions—now under displacement orders P173367 – ISR; the Lebanon Integrated Finance for Infrastructure Project P163476 – ISR has enrolled zero of its 180,000 target displaced Syrian beneficiaries; and the Lebanon Social Protection Enhancement Project reports that its quarterly review mechanism for adjusting transfer values cannot keep pace with hyperinflation in active conflict conditions P173367 – ISR.

Destruction of Critical Infrastructure Creating Reconstruction-Destruction Cycles

Israeli forces have systematically destroyed bridges over the Litani River, isolating over 150,000 people, while 17 incidents affecting water systems have been recorded across Baalbek-Hermel, Bekaa, South, and Nabatieh governorates, and 64 attacks on healthcare infrastructure have been documented since March 2, 2026. For projects whose core mandate is infrastructure restoration, this creates a cycle in which reconstruction efforts are destroyed by renewed hostilities before they can deliver benefits. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project P509428 – PAD includes rapid road access restoration and multi-sectoral restoration of critical infrastructure including water, energy, health, education, transport, and municipal services; the PAD acknowledges that geopolitical risks may impede implementation in border areas, but the scale of bridge destruction and village demolition in southern Lebanon far exceeds the risk scenarios contemplated at design. The Beqaa Water and Wastewater Project P147854 – PAD requires construction of approximately 200 kilometers of new sewerage network across Beqaa Valley districts, with the PAD noting that security concerns may limit contractor mobility; the destruction of bridges south of the Litani directly impedes logistics for this construction program. The Lebanon Renewable Energy and Electricity Sector Reform Project P180501 – PAD includes rehabilitation of three Litani River Authority hydropower plants and Qaraoun Dam safety improvements in the Litani River basin, as well as transmission line rehabilitation in areas now under active military operations; the PAD acknowledges that the security situation may impede implementation. Implementation evidence confirms the cycle: the Beirut Port Explosion Recovery Project P176577 – Aide Memoire faces a tight implementation timeline with zero slippage tolerance, but the March 2026 escalation has created material shortages, security restrictions on movement, and contractor unavailability that make October deadlines unachievable; the Solid Waste Management Project P172770 – Aide Memoire has seen Component 2 activities put on hold due to security escalation; and the Beqaa Water Project has experienced complete suspension of sewage network works by contractors P147854 – Aide Memoire.

Institutional Fragility and State Sovereignty Deficits Undermining Implementation Governance

Lebanon's government took the unprecedented step of banning Hezbollah's military activities on March 2, 2026, but the Lebanese Armed Forces commander objected to direct confrontation, and the decrees remain unenforced—exposing a critical gap between political declarations and state capacity to enforce authority. This sovereignty deficit compounds pre-existing institutional fragility, including the Council for Development and Reconstruction's documented procurement approval timelines of nine months and bid evaluation periods of two to eight months, the Ministry of Environment's 30 percent staff loss since 2019, and the electricity utility's lack of experience implementing Bank-financed projects. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project P509428 – PAD identifies institutional capacity as a high risk, noting CDR's limited capacity for a project of this scale and inefficient decision-making processes; the project depends on CDR with overall leadership from the Ministry of Public Works and Transport, and mitigation relies on an autonomous PMU reporting directly to the CDR President and CDR reform measures. The Lebanon Fiscal Management Reform Project P181155 – PAD is designed to restore basic functions of domestic revenue mobilization and accountable allocation of public resources, but the PAD acknowledges that the project was designed to operate despite ongoing political stalemate; the state's demonstrated inability to enforce its own disarmament decrees directly contradicts the project's core objective of restoring state fiscal authority. The Lebanon Renewable Energy Project P180501 – PAD depends on EDL and LRA as implementing entities requiring strategic oversight from the Ministry of Energy and Water, but the PAD identifies institutional capacity as high risk due to EDL and LRA's lack of experience with Bank-financed projects and depletion of qualified human resources. Implementation evidence confirms these constraints are binding: the Lebanon Fiscal Management Reform Project has not achieved effectiveness due to pending parliamentary ratification, with the loan signed in May 2024 but ratified only in July 2025 P181155 – ISR; the Beirut Port Explosion Recovery Project faces persistent inability to obtain necessary approvals from Beirut Governorate and Municipality for waste management infrastructure P176635 – ISR; the Lebanon Renewable Energy Project shows high institutional capacity risk with EDL and LRA unable to absorb project demands while simultaneously managing emergency response P180501 – ISR; and governance delays in fund transfers between the Ministry of Finance and implementing ministries are creating procurement bottlenecks across multiple projects P176635 – Aide Memoire P147854 – Aide Memoire.

Pre-Existing Economic Fragility Compounded by Conflict Shock Reversing Recovery

Lebanon's economy had registered a cautious 3.5 percent real GDP growth in 2025 following years of severe contraction, with the World Bank's January 2026 Economic Monitor projecting 4 percent growth in 2026 contingent on reform momentum, political stability, and reconstruction inflows—all of which have been fundamentally undermined by the March 2026 conflict. The Lebanese pound has lost 98 percent of its value since 2019, the debt-to-GDP ratio exceeds 357 percent, and the banking sector carries $72 billion in accumulated losses. These conditions directly threaten project financial sustainability, beneficiary purchasing power, and government counterpart funding capacity. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project P509428 – PAD identifies macroeconomic risk as high, noting ongoing economic crisis with triple-digit inflation now easing to double digits, and implements mitigation through US dollar denomination of contracts and contingent financing for price increases; the PAD's assumption that reconstruction activity will restore economic activity is now undermined by the conflict's reversal of nascent recovery. The Beqaa Water and Wastewater Project P147854 – PAD requires $5 million in government counterpart funding and depends on tariff increases and cost recovery improvements; the economic collapse directly threatens the government's ability to provide counterpart funds and sustain tariff policies. The Lebanon Social Protection Enhancement Project P173367 – PAD implements quarterly reviews of transfer values to account for inflation using adjusted exchange rates, but the PAD acknowledges sharp exchange rate fluctuations and very high inflation rates; the quarterly review mechanism cannot keep pace with hyperinflation in active conflict conditions. Implementation evidence confirms economic deterioration is materializing across the portfolio: the Beirut Port Explosion Recovery Project shows persistent high macroeconomic risk with the January 2026 growth projections now obsolete P176635 – ISR; landowner compensation for the Zahle sewer network has become effectively uncollectable because Lebanese lira devaluation has reduced compensation amounts to between $27 and $728 per plot in real terms, with 52 percent of landowners unable or unwilling to collect P172770 – Aide Memoire; the Lebanon Renewable Energy Project faces a structural currency mismatch between revenues in Lebanese pounds and costs in US dollars, with the Cash Waterfall Mechanism operationalization now threatened by the collapse of macroeconomic stabilization conditions P180501 – ISR; and NGO partners implementing social protection activities are experiencing core funding losses as international development financing contracts in response to the conflict, forcing operational scale-backs precisely when humanitarian demand has surged P506127 – Aide Memoire.
