# Djibouti FCV Portfolio Briefing

## Paragraph 1: Authoritarian Entrenchment and Collapse of Independent Civil Society

Djibouti faces a severe and worsening governance crisis rooted in the systematic dismantling of institutional checks and the elimination of independent civic space. In October–November 2025, the National Assembly unanimously removed the presidential age limit, clearing the path for President Guelleh's sixth term in April 2026 without public debate or dissenting votes. This constitutional manipulation operates alongside a near-total suppression of independent civil society, free media, and judicial independence, leaving virtually no autonomous actors capable of contesting executive decisions or monitoring public institutions. These twin dynamics—authoritarian entrenchment and civic collapse—create a structurally hostile operating environment for the entire World Bank portfolio. PAD evidence across projects confirms that this risk is embedded in project design: governance structures relying on high-level steering committees under executive bodies (P175483, P509082), civil society engagement components (P180696), and grievance redress mechanisms (P506969, P178988) all assume institutional autonomy and civic participation that do not exist in practice. Aide Memoire evidence confirms these risks are actively materializing: P174461 documents that the ARMD regulatory board has had no appointments since 2022, that the Digital Code's telecommunications regulation remains unadopted despite repeated mission recommendations, and that project management units exhibit slow procurement and weak accountability without external corrective pressure. P175483 records an inactive steering committee, a compromised internal audit function, and delayed stakeholder mobilization plans. P178332 documents missing accounting systems at project launch, delayed financial reporting, and stalled NGO contract negotiations for GBV services—all consistent with an institutional environment where independent oversight has been systematically dismantled. ISR evidence from P180696 and P174300 confirms that political and governance risks have remained rated "High" and "Substantial" respectively, with the constitutional revision cited as a direct driver of institutional fragility affecting project implementation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-9e33bd55-6107-428e-bde8-2dc02b8b55cf.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178332-285d7898-9c2d-4514-b7ba-c25e1c82a9dd.pdf" target="_blank">P178332 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>.

## Paragraph 2: Acute Food Insecurity, Structural Poverty, and Humanitarian Strain from Refugee Hosting

Djibouti's structural economic fragility—characterized by over 90% food import dependency, 79% poverty rates, 75% youth unemployment, and a Human Development Index ranking of 175 out of 193—creates a chronic humanitarian vulnerability that is compounded by the country's hosting of approximately 33,000 refugees and asylum seekers, predominantly from Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen. As of mid-2025, approximately 194,000 people (17% of the analyzed population) were in IPC Phase 3+ acute food insecurity, with projections of deterioration to 230,000 by end-2025, while the Regional Migrant Response Plan remained only 7% funded. These intersecting pressures directly threaten project delivery across the portfolio. PAD evidence shows that multiple projects explicitly target refugee populations in resource-constrained settings—P175483 targets 3,165 refugees for skills training in Ali Sabieh and Obock camps, P506969 targets 12,860 refugees alongside 35,900 host community residents, P178988 includes emergency food stocks for displaced persons, and P162901 targets 115,000 slum inhabitants including non-Djiboutian populations—without adequately accounting for how resource scarcity, humanitarian funding gaps, or competing demands on fragile public services could undermine delivery <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf" target="_blank">P506969 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – PAD</a>, <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing extensively. ISR evidence from P178033 documents refugee immunization rates of only 12% against an 80% target, with vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5% among refugee children. P175483 ISR records zero refugee enrollment in TVET programs against a target of 220, zero employment outcomes for refugees (stuck at 5%), and failure to track disability enrollment data. P178988 ISR documents a June 2025 drought declaration triggering project suspension, only 50% of emergency food stock targets achieved, and zero completion of 26 water infrastructure rehabilitation facilities. P162901 ISR records declining refugee student enrollment (from 596 to 384 between 2021–2025), zero implementation by refugee-led CSOs, and stalled property rights documentation in Ali Addeh and Holl Holl villages. Aide Memoire evidence from P508560 and P180999 confirms that poor rural road access, weak livelihood activity identification, and infrastructure maintenance failures are compounding service delivery gaps in refugee-hosting areas <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf" target="_blank">P178033 – ISR</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – ISR</a>, <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P180999 – Aide Memoire</a>.

## Paragraph 3: Structural Youth Unemployment, Economic Inequality, and Elite Capture of Project Benefits

Youth unemployment exceeding 75%, extreme poverty affecting approximately 40% of the population, and rapid urbanization concentrated in Djibouti City's Balbala slum create a volatile socioeconomic environment in which development projects face both demand-side absorption constraints and supply-side institutional fragility. Despite approximately 6% annual GDP growth driven by port and logistics revenues, trickle-down benefits remain negligible, and the absence of independent civil society removes the accountability mechanisms that would normally pressure equitable benefit distribution. PAD evidence demonstrates that this risk is embedded across the portfolio: P175483 acknowledges "macroeconomic" risk as "substantial" but proposes no labor market demand assessment or contingency for limited job creation despite targeting 23,775 trained graduates; P506969 identifies macroeconomic risk from external shocks without addressing structural unemployment barriers; P178836 targets youth and rural households for agri-food investments without analyzing how 75% youth unemployment constrains market absorption; and P180696 notes that "extreme poverty increased to 23–30% due to multiple crises" while relying on government institutional capacity that is itself constrained by fiscal stress <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf" target="_blank">P506969 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. Aide Memoire and ISR evidence confirm that elite capture and inequitable access are actively materializing. P508560 Aide Memoire documents that youth training programs lack pre-defined eligibility criteria and transparent selection procedures, creating direct vulnerability to ethnic favoritism and benefit concentration among already-privileged youth. P180999 Aide Memoire records the same deficiency for 400 training slots, with weak grievance mechanisms and limited beneficiary participation compounding exclusion risks. P174300 Aide Memoire documents 150 daily workers employed under non-compliant contracts without social security registration—a direct manifestation of how 75% youth unemployment eliminates worker bargaining power—alongside persistent construction site safety violations and inadequate HSE oversight. ISR evidence from P175483 confirms that the online gig work component has stalled entirely, labor mobility agreements with Kuwait and Qatar remain unfinalized, and refugee employment outcomes are stuck at 5% against targets, reflecting the structural inability of Djibouti's constrained economy to absorb trained cohorts <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P180999 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>.

## Paragraph 4: Regional Security Spillovers, Geopolitical Rivalry, and Cross-Border Instability

Djibouti's position at the Bab el-Mandeb strait—hosting U.S., Chinese, French, Japanese, and Italian military facilities within few miles of each other—creates a geopolitical over-militarization risk in which great-power competition, al-Shabaab spillover from Somalia, and escalating regional diplomatic crises generate compounding security and political instability. UN investigators confirmed in February 2025 that al-Shabaab and Houthi forces had progressed to physical meetings and materiel transfers including weaponized drones, creating distributed maritime threat zones around the corridor. Simultaneously, Israel's December 2025 recognition of Somaliland triggered Djibouti's condemnation and reports of Issa clan militiamen allegedly backed by Djibouti moving into Somaliland's Awdal region, deepening regional polarization and inserting Djibouti into proxy dynamics. PAD evidence shows that these risks are embedded in project design across the portfolio: P174300 acknowledges "security/fragility (substantial)" risk from regional instability but relies only on "regular security risk assessments" without specific protocols for insurgent threats to road rehabilitation sites or border operations at Guelileh; P175483 identifies security risk as "moderate" focused narrowly on refugee protection without addressing al-Shabaab proximity to Ali Sabieh camps; P509082 notes the project is "located in restive Horn of Africa region" but does not detail contingency plans for FRUD or terrorist disruption to supply chains; and P174300 acknowledges coordination dependency with Ethiopia without assessing how Djibouti's military alignment with Mogadishu could affect the Guelileh One-Stop Border Post <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. ISR evidence confirms that these risks are materializing at the project level. P174300 ISR records persistent "Substantial" political and governance risk ratings, with the Djibouti-Somaliland diplomatic crisis explicitly cited as threatening cross-border coordination capacity essential for corridor implementation. The ISR also documents persistent "Substantial" stakeholder engagement risks, with ethnic tensions and political exclusion dynamics identified as contributing to coordination failures between ADR, contractors, and ONEAD. Aide Memoire evidence from P174300 documents systemic labor non-compliance affecting 150 daily workers, inadequate government oversight of safety standards, and weak environmental and social compliance reporting—all consistent with an institutional environment where regional security preoccupations and geopolitical positioning divert government attention from development project governance <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>, <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>.
