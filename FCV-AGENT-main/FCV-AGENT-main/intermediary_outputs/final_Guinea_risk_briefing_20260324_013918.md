# Guinea FCV Portfolio Briefing

## 1. Authoritarian Consolidation and Institutional Fragility

Guinea's junta has systematically dismantled democratic institutions since the September 2021 coup, culminating in the March 2026 dissolution of 40 political parties—including the three main opposition groups—by late-night decree, stripping them of legal status and ordering asset confiscation. This follows a December 2025 presidential election in which all major opposition leaders were barred, yielding an 86.72% reported victory for President Doumbouya, and a September 2025 constitutional referendum extending presidential terms and permitting junta members to stand. Legislative elections scheduled for May 24, 2026 will proceed with meaningful competition structurally absent. Across the portfolio, PADs anticipated this trajectory: the energy access project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-e7b1cb57-9078-490e-8988-30bc9fe608c0.pdf" target="_blank">P511453 – PAD</a>, the emergency response project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509503-9e19c665-633a-4b5d-9057-1897309bb41a.pdf" target="_blank">P509503 – PAD</a>, the water project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-2e1acf99-ec4d-4ffc-b49b-0c9ec31f74c2.pdf" target="_blank">P179017 – PAD</a>, the fisheries project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ff8aaae6-4241-49f6-b294-04cfff550aaf.pdf" target="_blank">P180906 – PAD</a>, the local governance project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-dbb6f3f7-06bb-4d73-a6df-8c71eb7f7711.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – PAD</a>, the mining governance project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08859ec01&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P168613 – PAD</a>, the social protection project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08852e437&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P168777 – PAD</a>, and the agribusiness project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087d59368&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P164184 – PAD</a> all rate political and governance risk as high or substantial, with mitigation strategies relying on "continued high-level policy dialogue" and "transitional government assurances"—mechanisms that presuppose good-faith engagement with a government now consolidating party-state control. These risks are materializing concretely: the local governance project documents that municipal councils were dissolved and replaced with appointed special delegations, causing cascading implementation delays <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-0d85cb88-e8fe-4bb3-b9b1-81c8c0178a90.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – ISR</a>; treasury approval processes are blocking 45% of local fund transfers <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-0d85cb88-e8fe-4bb3-b9b1-81c8c0178a90.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – ISR</a>; and the mining governance project records inter-ministerial coordination failures and a 25% disbursement rate attributable to institutional fragility <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-8608b6ee-d8be-4d24-a9b0-3883596421e7.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – ISR</a>. Aide Memoire evidence confirms that local government accounts have been frozen since 2022, local public service reform has been suspended, and the recruitment of Local Development Planning Service Chiefs has been halted since April 2024 <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-594cf5df-5372-4b2c-b80e-d1702874247b.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – Aide Memoire</a>. The forward-looking risk is that the May 2026 legislative elections—occurring with opposition structurally excluded—will further entrench the party-state model, reducing government commitment to the governance reforms on which performance-based conditions across multiple projects depend.

## 2. Security Force Violence, Protest Suppression, and Civic Space Closure

Guinea's junta has maintained a ban on public protests since May 2022, and security forces have responded to violations with lethal force, killing at least 59 protesters and bystanders since 2022—including at least 20 in 2024 and at least eight people including three children during Conakry protests documented by Human Rights Watch. Simultaneously, enforced disappearances have been used as a systematic tool of repression: prominent FNDC activists Oumar Sylla and Mamadou Billo Bah have been missing since July 2024, civil society leader Abdoul Sacko was abducted and tortured in February 2025, and investigative journalist Habib Marouane Camara disappeared in December 2024. Six major private media outlets—including Djoma TV, Espace FM, and Sweet FM—had their licenses revoked in May 2024 and remain banned, creating what RSF describes as a "profound media vacuum." These interlocking risks directly threaten portfolio operations. PADs for the energy access project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-e7b1cb57-9078-490e-8988-30bc9fe608c0.pdf" target="_blank">P511453 – PAD</a>, water project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-2e1acf99-ec4d-4ffc-b49b-0c9ec31f74c2.pdf" target="_blank">P179017 – PAD</a>, and emergency response project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509503-9e19c665-633a-4b5d-9057-1897309bb41a.pdf" target="_blank">P509503 – PAD</a> identify Conakry as a primary implementation location and acknowledge that community acceptance of tariff increases, resettlement compensation, and connection regularization could generate grievances—but their mitigation strategies assume a civic space where communities can safely voice concerns through grievance mechanisms and stakeholder consultations. The climate of terror documented by Amnesty International and 25 human rights organizations means that community members, civil society partners, and project staff face personal security risks for engaging in visible civic activities. The fisheries project's ISR confirms a high political and governance fragility rating, noting that the protest ban and security force violence directly constrain community engagement and participatory management <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180906-af1ea935-d30a-4433-ab02-ba43568d4457.pdf" target="_blank">P180906 – ISR</a>. The water project's ISR similarly records that environmental and social risks are materializing in Greater Conakry, where resettlement-affected communities lack safe channels to contest compensation processes <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P179017-70d0e4c6-d109-4724-94b5-d9be1036fa78.pdf" target="_blank">P179017 – ISR</a>. The mining governance project's Aide Memoire documents that communities displaced by mining projects without clear resettlement frameworks may attempt to protest but face the existing ban and risk of lethal security force response <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-5b69ef5d-0ac1-4652-9e38-a491f1563ed9.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – Aide Memoire</a>. With the junta entrenching itself through the May 2026 legislative elections, the risk of renewed protest and violent crackdown is elevated precisely during the critical early implementation phases of several projects.

## 3. Simandou Mining Boom: Revenue Management Failure and Social Disruption

Guinea shipped its first iron ore from the $20 billion Simandou mega-project in December 2025, with the IMF projecting a 26% GDP lift by 2030. However, employment peaked at over 60,000 construction jobs in 2024–2025 and will fall to under 15,000 operational workers, creating a mass layoff dynamic that threatens significant social disruption in mining communities. The World Bank's July 2025 Guinea Economic Update warned of a "narrow window to ensure benefits of growth are widely shared," noting that more than half the population lives in poverty despite Guinea being the world's top bauxite exporter. Guinea holds only a 15% stake in Simandou, with the arrangement dominated by Chinese interests, and the IMF has called for windfall revenues to be channeled into education and infrastructure. The mining governance project's PAD explicitly identifies this risk, noting that the project targets communities in mining regions and includes alternative income-generating activities for 500 beneficiaries—a capacity far exceeded by the anticipated 45,000+ layoffs <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08859ec01&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P168613 – PAD</a>. The agribusiness project similarly targets Boké and Kindia prefectures—the same mining regions—with the explicit aim of alleviating social tensions from mining-driven unemployment <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087d59368&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P164184 – PAD</a>. These risks are actively materializing: the mining governance project's Aide Memoire documents that the Environmental Management Agency (AGEE) lacks sustainable financing for independent monitoring of Simandou operations, that resettlement and compensation frameworks remain non-operationalized, that inter-ministerial divergence between the Mines and Infrastructure ministries is preventing effective rail and port oversight, and that institutional capacity for social impact management is insufficient <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-5b69ef5d-0ac1-4652-9e38-a491f1563ed9.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – Aide Memoire</a>. The ISR for the mining governance project records that environmental and social governance implementation stands at less than 20%, that the E&S assessment database has not been modernized, and that citizen involvement in oversight committees remains limited <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-8608b6ee-d8be-4d24-a9b0-3883596421e7.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – ISR</a>. The local governance project's Aide Memoire confirms that suspended recruitment of local planning officers and weakened local institutions will constrain the government's ability to manage social tensions from mass layoffs <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-594cf5df-5372-4b2c-b80e-d1702874247b.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – Aide Memoire</a>. The convergence of mass unemployment, weak institutional safeguards, and inadequate revenue management creates acute forward-looking risk of social disruption in mining communities during 2026.

## 4. Intercommunal and Ethnic Tensions Exacerbated by Political Exclusion

Guinea's political landscape has historically been deeply polarized along ethnic lines, with the UFDG drawing its base from the Fulani (Peulh) community and the RPG from the Malinke community. The March 2026 dissolution of both parties—along with 38 others—eliminates the institutional channels through which these ethnic cleavages have historically been managed. The exclusion of exiled UFDG leader Cellou Dalein Diallo (Fulani) and Sidya Toure from elections, combined with the junta's ethnic Malinke military leadership, risks translating political grievances into intercommunal friction. Civil society analysts have noted that "governance reforms perceived as exclusionary erode legitimacy and fuel popular discontent," a pattern observed by UNOWAS across the region. Multiple PADs identify this risk as a structural sensitivity: the local governance project explicitly identifies "political instrumentalization of ethnicity" as a high-level FCV driver and designs an Early Warning and Response System (EWRS) to address inter-community tensions <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-dbb6f3f7-06bb-4d73-a6df-8c71eb7f7711.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – PAD</a>; the social protection project acknowledges "ethnic and socio-economic tensions" and notes that its 18% coverage rate (150,000 of 840,000 eligible households) creates "risk of social tensions due to limited coverage relative to need" and "potential for perception of bias in beneficiary selection" <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08852e437&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P168777 – PAD</a>; and the energy access project targets Fulani-majority regions including Labé, Mamou, Pita, and Dalaba without explicitly addressing ethnic dimensions of stakeholder engagement <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-e7b1cb57-9078-490e-8988-30bc9fe608c0.pdf" target="_blank">P511453 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing: the local governance project's Aide Memoire documents that the EWRS covers only 169 of 337 communes, leaving significant blind spots in areas where intercommunal tensions are most likely to escalate <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-594cf5df-5372-4b2c-b80e-d1702874247b.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – Aide Memoire</a>. The mining governance project's Aide Memoire confirms that weak resettlement frameworks disproportionately affect specific geographic and ethnic communities in mining regions, and that inadequate environmental monitoring of Simandou operations risks amplifying grievances along ethnic lines <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-5b69ef5d-0ac1-4652-9e38-a491f1563ed9.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – Aide Memoire</a>. The ISR for the fisheries project records that the high governance fragility rating reflects, in part, the risk that project implementation in ethnically diverse coastal and rural areas could be affected if benefits are perceived as unevenly distributed <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180906-af1ea935-d30a-4433-ab02-ba43568d4457.pdf" target="_blank">P180906 – ISR</a>. The forward-looking risk is that the May 2026 legislative elections—proceeding without the institutional channels that previously managed ethnic representation—will intensify these dynamics across all project areas.

## 5. Development Partner Tensions: Governance Conditionalities Versus Mining Engagement

The governance environment—characterized by party dissolution, protest bans, enforced disappearances, and media suppression—creates structural friction between multilateral development partners committed to governance conditionalities and the Doumbouya government, which is increasingly aligned with Chinese and Russian investors who operate without political conditions. Russia maintains significant bauxite mining interests in Guinea, the Doumbouya government has facilitated Russian arms shipments to Sahelian juntas, and the Simandou arrangement is dominated by Chinese interests. The World Bank's July 2025 Guinea Economic Update explicitly called for reforms to make mining-driven growth "more inclusive and resilient," warning of a narrow window for action. This tension is embedded in project design across the portfolio: the energy access project's performance-based conditions totaling $60 million are linked to governance reforms and tariff restructuring that depend on government commitment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-e7b1cb57-9078-490e-8988-30bc9fe608c0.pdf" target="_blank">P511453 – PAD</a>; the emergency response project explicitly contemplates use of OP 7.30 if circumstances require and notes the possibility of a further Country Engagement Note if medium-term programming becomes impossible <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509503-9e19c665-633a-4b5d-9057-1897309bb41a.pdf" target="_blank">P509503 – PAD</a>; and the mining governance project's PAD acknowledges that "more efforts needed to enable wider citizen benefits from mining" while depending on "continued engagement with Government at various levels" <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08859ec01&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P168613 – PAD</a>. These tensions are actively materializing across the portfolio. The local governance project's Aide Memoire documents that the government's suspension of local public service reform, frozen commune accounts, and delayed municipal treasurer deployments signal resistance to the institutional strengthening that development partners have conditioned on <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177095-594cf5df-5372-4b2c-b80e-d1702874247b.pdf" target="_blank">P177095 – Aide Memoire</a>. The mining governance project's ISR records persistent delays in critical regulatory reforms—including the Protected Area Code, Water Code, and mining permit exclusions from classified forests—alongside weak inter-ministerial coordination and a 25% disbursement rate <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P168613-8608b6ee-d8be-4d24-a9b0-3883596421e7.pdf" target="_blank">P168613 – ISR</a>. The agribusiness project's ISR documents that UN agency implementation delays in agricultural value chains reflect broader development partner friction with the government <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08872333c&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P164184 – ISR</a>. The health project's PAD notes that the government's "strong political commitment evidenced by health sector budget increase" predates the March 2026 political consolidation and may not survive if World Bank governance messaging is perceived as interference <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-aa26d346-cc66-4708-a538-60e0b30cdda4.pdf" target="_blank">P506072 – PAD</a>. The forward-looking risk is that as the government deepens its alignment with non-conditional partners and consolidates authoritarian control, the political incentive to maintain World Bank fiduciary standards and governance reform commitments will diminish, threatening the sustainability of performance-based conditions and institutional reform objectives across the portfolio.
