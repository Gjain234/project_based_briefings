# Djibouti FCV Portfolio Briefing

**Paragraph 1: Authoritarian Consolidation and the Erosion of Institutional Checks**

Djibouti's political landscape is defined by the progressive concentration of executive power under President Guelleh, who has governed since 1999 and, in October–November 2025, secured a constitutional amendment removing the presidential age limit of 75—clearing the path for a sixth term in April 2026. This revision was adopted unanimously by a legislature with no recorded dissent, reflecting near-total alignment between the executive and formal institutions. The removal of term limits signals not merely an electoral maneuver but a structural weakening of the institutional checks that development projects depend upon for governance predictability, policy continuity, and fiduciary integrity. Across the portfolio, PAD-stage risk assessments flagged this vulnerability explicitly: the TVET and skills development project identified that its multi-ministry steering committee, housed under the executive Economic Development Council, lacked safeguards against political capture <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>; the emergency resilience project noted that governance arrangements were "spread across various ministries with unclear mandates," making the system vulnerable to political interference as power consolidates <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>; the public financial management reform project identified a "high" political risk that vested interests could delay reforms, with mitigation relying on "government commitment demonstrated through past successes"—an assumption increasingly fragile under indefinite rule <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>; and the corridor development project warned that the ability to mobilize private sector finance depended on political will that could weaken under executive concentration <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. These risks are now materializing. The PFM project's ISR documents a persistent "High" political and governance risk rating, with administrative delays in publishing governance decrees blocking project operationalization, and the PFM Reform Steering Committee's continuity threatened by political preoccupation with the 2026 election cycle <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>. The TVET project's ISR records an inactive steering committee, stalled labor mobility agreement negotiations, and a governance gap in EFTP institutional reform where regulatory texts were formally adopted but not implemented—a pattern consistent with a political system that prioritizes form over institutional substance <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The digital economy project's ISR similarly documents a "High" political and governance risk, with ARMD board appointments vacant since 2022 and the Digital Code's telecommunications regulation (Book Two) unadopted despite repeated mission recommendations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-c15be353-62d2-4f55-928e-a77385dc1393.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – ISR</a>. Across the portfolio, the constitutional revision is not an abstract political event but a realized governance deterioration with direct operational consequences.

**Paragraph 2: Collapsed Civil Society and the Accountability Vacuum**

Djibouti's civic space has been systematically dismantled. Freedom House classifies the country as "Not Free," the judiciary has been weaponized against opponents, and the U.S. State Department has noted that formal democratic structures have "little relevance to the real distribution and exercise of power." Journalists and activists are regularly harassed or arrested, and virtually no independent civil society remains to monitor government performance, advocate for beneficiaries, or provide the external accountability that development projects require. This structural condition creates a pervasive accountability vacuum that cuts across every project in the portfolio. At the PAD stage, multiple projects identified this risk but underestimated its operational depth: the PFM project's Component 3 explicitly targeted civil society engagement for public finance oversight, yet the PAD did not acknowledge that independent civil society organizations capable of meaningful participation do not exist in practice <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>; the agri-food project noted "substantial" stakeholder risk from weak value chain organization but assumed functional grievance mechanisms without acknowledging the restricted political environment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>; and the education project established a grievance mechanism "for all stakeholders" without addressing how refugees, rural students, and girls could safely report concerns in a context of political repression <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the consequences are concrete and pervasive. The corridor project's Aide Memoire documents systemic non-compliance with labor and social protection standards—150 daily workers employed without compliant contracts or social security registration—enabled directly by the absence of independent labor monitors, unions, or media scrutiny <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The same project's Aide Memoire records that government oversight reports omit critical safety violations and non-conformities, with no independent mechanism to detect or correct these gaps <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The TVET project's ISR documents that its grievance redress mechanism and stakeholder mobilization plans remain delayed or non-functional, with the GBV action plan not yet implemented <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The GBV prevention project's Aide Memoire records missing accounting systems at project launch, delayed financial reporting, stalled NGO contract negotiations, and capacity gaps in psychosocial counseling—all reflecting an institutional ecosystem where independent service providers have atrophied under governance pressure <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178332-285d7898-9c2d-4514-b7ba-c25e1c82a9dd.pdf" target="_blank">P178332 – Aide Memoire</a>. The statistics project's ISR documents that its mitigation strategy of "systematic citizen engagement" depends on civil society that does not exist, creating a structural gap between design assumptions and country reality <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0884c1bc2&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P171777 – ISR</a>. The accountability vacuum is not a background condition—it is an active implementation constraint degrading results across the portfolio.

**Paragraph 3: Acute Food Insecurity, Structural Poverty, and the Limits of Humanitarian Absorption**

Djibouti imports over 90% of its food, approximately 17% of the analyzed population experienced IPC Phase 3 or above acute food insecurity as of mid-2025, and structural poverty rates stand at approximately 79%—with 42% in extreme poverty. A 2024 multisectoral survey recorded a 14.7% global acute malnutrition rate, up from 13% the prior year, driven by structural poverty, water access constraints, and rising food prices linked to regional geopolitical tensions. These conditions are not cyclical but structural, and they directly constrain the absorptive capacity of projects targeting livelihoods, food security, and economic resilience. The food security and drought response project identified this risk at appraisal, noting "substantial" macroeconomic risk from the combined effects of the Ukraine war, COVID-19, the Ethiopia conflict, and drought on fiscal and external accounts, and acknowledging high volatility in food commodity markets <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – PAD</a>. The emergency resilience project similarly flagged that its institutional implementing agencies—SEGRC and MEFI—had "limited capacity due to lack of funding and staff," creating a structural mismatch between the project's ambitions and the government's baseline capacity <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. In implementation, these risks have materialized severely. The food security project's ISR records that Djibouti declared a drought emergency in June 2025, triggering a project restructuring that shifted resources from preparedness to emergency response; only 50% of the emergency food stock target was acquired, and zero water infrastructure rehabilitation facilities were completed out of 26 planned <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – ISR</a>. The same project's Aide Memoire documents procurement bottlenecks requiring ministerial-level escalation, delayed transmission of environmental and social safeguard documents, non-compliance with planned water distribution routes across six regions, and a monitoring and evaluation system that had not yet been established <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-79f711cb-4676-4798-95e4-844f5162676d.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – Aide Memoire</a>. The community resilience project's Aide Memoire records that remote rural areas lack viable income-generating activities due to weak market access and limited economic diversification, that rural savings groups face persistent governance and financial management capacity constraints, and that poor road infrastructure causes logistical delays in cash and in-kind transfer distributions <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a> <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P180999 – Aide Memoire</a>. Youth unemployment exceeding 75% compounds these dynamics: even where training programs exist, the labor market lacks absorptive capacity, and the TVET project's ISR records that refugee employment outcomes remain stuck at 5% against an 80% target <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The structural economic fragility of Djibouti is not a risk that project design can fully mitigate—it is the operating environment.

**Paragraph 4: Refugee and Displacement Pressures Straining Service Delivery**

Djibouti hosts approximately 33,360 refugees and asylum seekers—over 3% of its population—primarily from Ethiopia, Somalia, and Yemen, with 85% concentrated in refugee villages such as Ali Addeh, Holl-Holl, and Markazi. An additional 150,000 non-registered migrants transit or reside in the country, and the Regional Migrant Response Plan was only 7% funded as of mid-2025. This hosting burden, sustained by a progressive 2017 Refugee Law, is increasingly strained by resource constraints, recurrent droughts, and the institutional fragility of the agencies responsible for refugee management. Across the portfolio, projects targeting refugee populations identified this risk at appraisal but underestimated the depth of institutional strain. The TVET project noted that 3,165 refugee beneficiaries concentrated in Ali-Addeh, Holl-Holl, and Obock might face competing priorities—food security, shelter—that undermine training participation, but did not detail contingency plans for humanitarian deterioration <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The community resilience project acknowledged that SEGRC had "limited capacity due to lack of funding and staff" while simultaneously targeting 32,000 displaced persons for emergency response <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. The education project identified a "moderate" refugee protection risk and proposed mitigation through "regular dialogue with UNHCR and partners," without assessing how recurrent droughts or resource scarcity would affect refugee education programming <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. The slum upgrading project acknowledged host-refugee tensions but did not address ethnic dimensions or resource competition dynamics <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the refugee service delivery gap is the most consistently documented failure across the portfolio. The health project's ISR records refugee immunization rates at 50% against an 80% target, vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5%, and a 12% immunization rate against an 80% target for a specific refugee health indicator—concrete manifestations of the resource constraints and institutional fragility flagged in the country risk <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf" target="_blank">P178033 – ISR</a>. The TVET project's ISR records zero refugee enrollment in training programs against a target of 220, zero refugee employment outcomes, and zero tracking of refugee completion data <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The education project's ISR documents declining refugee student enrollment from 596 to 384 between 2021 and 2025, failure to integrate refugee schools into the national EMIS system, and zero implementation of refugee law recommendations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf" target="_blank">P166059 – ISR</a>. The slum upgrading project's ISR records zero implementation by refugee-led CSOs, stalled property rights documentation in refugee villages, and a delayed refugee integration and social cohesion assessment <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>. The statistics project's ISR and Aide Memoire document that boosted sampling of migrant and refugee households for the national census and household budget survey has created institutional overload at INSTAD, compromising data quality across concurrent surveys <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0884c1bc2&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P171777 – ISR</a> <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171777-8dfe1d10-65dd-4d7a-96a0-701ceb6e77ed.pdf" target="_blank">P171777 – Aide Memoire</a>. The displacement risk is not a future scenario—it is the primary driver of realized implementation gaps across the portfolio.

**Paragraph 5: Ethnic Tensions, Political Exclusion, and the Risk of Inequitable Project Delivery**

Djibouti's political order rests on a delicate and historically contested balance between the Issa-Somali majority (approximately 60% of the population) and the Afar minority (approximately 35%), who fought an insurgency against the government in the early 1990s before a 1994 power-sharing agreement co-opted the former armed movement FRUD into the ruling coalition. Governing party officials are predominantly Issa, and the Afar population remains structurally marginalized in resource allocation, institutional representation, and political participation. Recent reports of alleged Djibouti-backed Issa clan militiamen moving into Somaliland's Awdal region in December 2025 risk inflaming trans-border ethnic dynamics, and a contested or poorly managed presidential succession could fracture the delicate ethnic balance underpinning the current political order. This structural fault line creates a persistent risk that project benefits, beneficiary selection, and institutional governance will be captured by or perceived as favoring dominant ethnic constituencies—undermining social cohesion objectives and creating community resistance. At the PAD stage, this risk was identified across the portfolio but without adequate mitigation specificity. The TVET project noted that its multi-ministry steering committee lacked explicit ethnic representation safeguards, creating potential for political interference in beneficiary selection among a population that includes 3,165 refugees of primarily Somali origin <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The community resilience project acknowledged that ethnic tensions could undermine impartial distribution of water, food, and emergency supplies across diverse populations, but relied on general grievance mechanisms without ethnic-specific safeguards <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. The agri-food project specified three geographically dispersed implementation zones without addressing the ethnic composition of target areas or mechanisms to ensure equitable benefit distribution across Issa and Afar populations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>. The education project noted that resource allocation to refugee education—predominantly Somali and Ethiopian beneficiaries—could become politically contested if ethnic tensions intensified <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the ethnic exclusion risk is materializing through opaque beneficiary selection and weak grievance mechanisms. The community resilience project's Aide Memoire documents that youth training programs lack pre-defined eligibility criteria and transparent selection procedures, creating a direct mechanism through which ethnic favoritism could operate in a context where Issa dominance in resource allocation is structurally documented <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a> <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P180999 – Aide Memoire</a>. The corridor project's ISR records persistent "Substantial" stakeholder engagement risk, with coordination failures that may reflect underlying ethnic tensions if project benefits are perceived as concentrated in Issa-dominated areas along the corridor <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>. The slum upgrading project's ISR documents that refugee-led CSO participation has achieved zero implementation, with refugees—predominantly Somali and Ethiopian—facing compounded barriers to organizing that mirror the structural exclusion of non-dominant groups from political participation <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>. The statistics project's ISR notes that private Arabic schools—often associated with minority communities—have not adopted the national EMIS system, suggesting institutional fragmentation along ethnic and community lines that could compromise the representativeness of national data <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf" target="_blank">P166059 – ISR</a>. Without explicit ethnic equity safeguards in beneficiary selection, monitoring, and grievance mechanisms, the portfolio risks reproducing and entrenching the structural inequalities it is designed to address.
