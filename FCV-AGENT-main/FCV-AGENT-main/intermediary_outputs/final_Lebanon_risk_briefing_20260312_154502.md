# Lebanon FCV Portfolio Briefing

## Paragraph 1: Renewed Armed Conflict and Direct Project Disruption

The most acute risk facing the portfolio is the renewed Israel-Hezbollah armed conflict that erupted on 2 March 2026, when Hezbollah fired rockets and drones into northern Israel following the killing of Iranian Supreme Leader Khamenei, triggering large-scale Israeli military operations across southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and Beirut's southern suburbs—killing at least 294 people and injuring over 1,000 in the first eight days, with Israeli forces announcing further ground incursions and no active diplomatic mediation in sight. Projects are acutely susceptible because their implementation geographies map directly onto active conflict zones: the Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project explicitly targets southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and Beirut's southern suburbs for reconstruction of energy, water, transport, schools, and hospitals <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a>; the Greater Beirut Water Supply Project requires civil works in areas including Beirut's southern suburbs and the Litani basin, with the PAD stating that "no works will commence until conflict has ended and sites are safe" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a>; the Lake Qaraoun Pollution Prevention Project operates in Hermel, Ras Baalbek, and the Litani River basin—Hezbollah strongholds now under active military pressure <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-c260299b-4517-48c7-a0c1-b95eb42d17a7.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – PAD</a>; and the Solid Waste and Pollution Management Project has project zones in Tyre and Nabatieh in southern Lebanon <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a>. These risks are fully materializing: contractors suspended all sewage network works in the Beqaa region following the September 2024 escalation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a>; the LEAP project is conducting virtual-only missions and prohibiting work near active shelling, with UXO clearance required before any resumption <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a>; Component 2 activities across multiple projects have been placed on hold [P172770 | Aide Memoire; P176635 | Aide Memoire]; and the UN Special Coordinator confirmed in March 2026 that "a major World Bank loan was set to jump-start reconstruction" but "progress has now collapsed due to the renewed hostilities" [P509428 | ISR; P180501 | ISR]. The conflict's asymmetric and unpredictable character, combined with the absence of diplomatic containment, means the entire portfolio faces an indefinite suspension of field operations across its most critical implementation zones.

## Paragraph 2: Mass Internal Displacement and Humanitarian Crisis Overwhelming Project Baselines

A second, compounding risk is the acute humanitarian crisis driven by mass internal displacement: as of 10 March 2026, more than 667,000 people had registered on Lebanon's government displacement platform—an increase of over 100,000 in a single day—with around 120,000 sheltering in collective sites, many displaced for the second time since 2024, and over 4.1 million people (more than 70% of Lebanon's population) already in need of humanitarian assistance before the March 2026 attacks. Projects are susceptible because their beneficiary targeting, service delivery, and institutional capacity assumptions were designed for a stabilizing post-conflict environment, not a second displacement wave of this magnitude: the LEAP project identified 1.2 million affected people as beneficiaries and included decommissioning of IDP centers as a project component, but the March 2026 escalation has created new displacements exceeding the original baseline and rendered geographic targeting mechanisms obsolete <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a>; the Greater Beirut Water Supply Project explicitly noted that displacement from southern Lebanon to Beirut would "further strain BMLWE's capacity to meet growing water demand" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a>; the Social Recovery Project targets 15,475 beneficiaries across GBV survivors, mental health cases, and persons with disabilities—populations now scattered across collective shelters and host communities <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-6dadd285-c9a6-442f-8faa-3a5cdb52d0ab.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – PAD</a>; and the Lebanon Social Safety Net Project relies on the DAEM registration platform and WFP beneficiary verification, both of which assume stable beneficiary residence <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-07e5fa4b-e782-4ffd-ab74-21191c4b8c78.pdf" target="_blank">P178075 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing concretely: the Social Recovery Project's ISR documents vulnerability to "service delivery disruption in conflict-affected areas" with challenges accessing GBV survivors and mental health beneficiaries <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a>; the Solid Waste Project reports that mass displacement has disrupted community engagement and vulnerable group identification in southern Lebanon and the Bekaa <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a>; and the Beirut Urban Resilience Project faces owner access denial for structural rehabilitation works, plausibly connected to displacement dynamics that have left property owners absent or unreachable <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176577-6c3b4a27-9cf9-4050-9a10-ed310209e452.pdf" target="_blank">P176577 – Aide Memoire</a>. The pivot from development to humanitarian aid response—with the EU mobilizing emergency aid on 10 March 2026—risks entrenching Lebanon in crisis-response mode and permanently displacing the structural recovery objectives the portfolio was designed to advance.

## Paragraph 3: Sectarian Political Fragmentation, Institutional Paralysis, and Democratic Backsliding

A third systemic risk is Lebanon's deepening sectarian political fragmentation and institutional paralysis, now compounded by democratic backsliding: on 9 March 2026, parliament voted 76 to 41 to extend its term by two years—the fourth such extension in a decade—while tensions between the reform-oriented government of President Aoun and Prime Minister Salam on one side, and the Hezbollah-Amal bloc and Parliament Speaker Berri on the other, have persisted over disarmament, electoral law, and fiscal policy, with senior Hezbollah officials walking out of cabinet sessions. Projects are susceptible because they depend on inter-ministerial coordination, parliamentary ratification of loan agreements, and sustained government commitment to reform: the Fiscal Management Project requires a Project Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Finance coordinating across multiple agencies, and the PAD explicitly identifies "past challenges with loan agreement signing and parliamentary ratification" as a key risk <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-5067fa60-78e4-4f5e-b6c1-5a428d852b01.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – PAD</a>; the LEAP project requires coordination across the Ministries of Energy, Water, Education, Social Affairs, Public Health, and Environment, with the PAD noting that "inefficient decision-making processes (procurement approval takes 9 months, bid evaluation 2-8 months)" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a>; and the Energy Sector Project requires ERA commissioner appointments and implementation of the Letter of Electricity Sector Policy as disbursement conditions, with the PAD noting that "government lacks track record of consistent implementation of electricity sector policies" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-c260299b-4517-48c7-a0c1-b95eb42d17a7.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing across the portfolio: the Fiscal Management Project's IBRD loan component had not achieved parliamentary ratification as of June 2025, with the ISR documenting that the project is "critical for the post-war recovery phase" yet blocked by the same parliament that subsequently extended its own term <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a>; the Agricultural and Rural Development Project (GATE) achieved effectiveness only in August 2025 after a 14-month delay due to parliamentary ratification gridlock, with disbursement at 0.25% as of the latest ISR <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a>; the Beirut Critical Environmental Recovery Project faces inability to obtain approvals from Beirut Governorate and Municipality, with the ISR documenting delays in Parliament ratification of follow-on financing <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-f112aa75-2e9c-4c03-ac0e-46311eb3089b.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – ISR</a>; and the Solid Waste Project's ISR documents governance delays in fund transfers between the Ministry of Finance and Ministry of Environment, attributed to "external coordination challenges among key government stakeholders" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a>. The structural nature of this risk—rooted in Lebanon's confessional power-sharing system rather than any single political episode—means it will persist regardless of conflict trajectory and represents the most durable constraint on portfolio implementation.

## Paragraph 4: Economic Collapse, IMF Reform Stalling, and Financial Sector Fragility

A fourth cross-cutting risk is Lebanon's severe and worsening economic crisis: real GDP has declined more than 38% since 2019 (contracting 6.6% in 2024 alone), public debt exceeds 357% of GDP, the banking sector carries over $72 billion in accumulated losses, depositors remain locked out of savings above monthly withdrawal limits of $400–500, and the government committed to eight IMF reform prior actions to unlock a $3 billion program but has completed only one (a bank secrecy amendment in April 2025), with the March 2026 conflict escalation halting nascent recovery momentum entirely. Projects are susceptible because their financial sustainability, procurement viability, and government counterpart funding all depend on macroeconomic conditions that have fundamentally deteriorated: the LEAP project's PAD identifies "macroeconomic (high)" risk including "triple-digit inflation, Lebanese pound lost 98% of value, reconstruction activity may drive up construction material and labor prices" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a>; the Greater Beirut Water Supply Project requires a Cash Waterfall Mechanism operationalized by June 2025 to ensure EDL's access to forex, with the PAD noting that "approximately US$1 billion per year needed for 8 hours of services" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a>; the Social Safety Net Project relies on USD-denominated cash transfers through money transfer agents, but the PAD acknowledges that "money transfer agents may face liquidity constraints" given banking sector dysfunction <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-07e5fa4b-e782-4ffd-ab74-21191c4b8c78.pdf" target="_blank">P178075 – PAD</a>; and the Solid Waste Project requires approximately $62 million in government counterpart contributions across components, with the PAD noting "Lebanon facing most severe financial and economic crisis in modern history" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing with documented severity: the Zahle sewer network project reports that Lebanese lira depreciation has reduced land compensation amounts to USD 27–728 per plot, making compensation "negligible in real terms" and leaving 52% of landowners unable or unwilling to collect <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a>; the GATE project shows zero disbursement and zero implementation across all indicators (farmers reached, SME loans, infrastructure) as of January 2025, with the ISR attributing the stall to macroeconomic fragility and political instability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a>; the Health System Resilience Project ISR documents a persistent "High" macroeconomic risk rating with currency instability impacting disbursements and operational costs <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178587-8133ed2c-dff3-4ee7-a615-063ca255128b.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – ISR</a>; and the Social Recovery Project ISR documents that local NGO partners face sustainability challenges rooted in Lebanon's economic collapse, with the macroeconomic risk rated "Substantial" throughout implementation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a>. The March 2026 conflict has eliminated any near-term prospect of IMF program completion, making macroeconomic stabilization a distant horizon and threatening the financial viability of the entire portfolio.

## Paragraph 5: World Bank and Donor Reconstruction Program Collapse Under Renewed Conflict

The fifth and most portfolio-specific risk is the direct threat to the World Bank's own reconstruction program: in June 2025, the World Bank Board approved the $250 million Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project (LEAP) as part of a $1 billion scalable reconstruction framework addressing critical public infrastructure in conflict-affected areas, following a Rapid Damage and Needs Assessment that estimated $7.2 billion in direct damages and $11 billion in reconstruction needs from the 2023–2024 conflict—but the March 2026 escalation has directly halted this program, with the UN Special Coordinator stating that "a major World Bank loan was set to jump-start reconstruction" but "progress has now collapsed due to the renewed hostilities," and the EU pivoting to emergency humanitarian aid as over 680,000 people were displaced. The entire portfolio is susceptible because multiple projects are either components of or dependent on the LEAP framework and the broader reconstruction agenda: the LEAP project itself (P509428) is the $250 million reconstruction vehicle now suspended; the Energy Sector Project (P180501) is explicitly identified in the FCV risk as the project whose reconstruction objectives have collapsed; the Greater Beirut Water Supply Project (P504170) was designed to address water security as part of the recovery agenda; and the Fiscal Management Project (P181155) was designed to "restore core fiscal management functions" as the institutional foundation for reconstruction. These risks are materializing across the portfolio in a cascading pattern: the LEAP ISR documents "High" political and governance risk, "High" macroeconomic risk, and "High" institutional capacity risk simultaneously, with stakeholder engagement severely disrupted <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a>; the Beirut Urban Resilience Project's ISR documents that contaminated rubble management activities are pending transfer to the LEAP, which itself is pending parliamentary ratification <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-f112aa75-2e9c-4c03-ac0e-46311eb3089b.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – ISR</a>; the Solid Waste Project's ISR documents a deterioration in fiduciary risk from "Substantial" to "High" as the implementation environment has worsened <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-4727dec5-5d09-4ce2-8055-7dcb271aae12.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – ISR</a>; and the Social Recovery Project's ISR documents that NGO partners are losing core funding as international development financing contracts in response to the conflict, with the broader financing environment shifting from development to humanitarian response <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a>. The development-to-humanitarian aid pivot documented across the portfolio represents a structural threat: if Lebanon remains in crisis-response mode, the $1 billion reconstruction framework cannot be executed, the structural reforms needed for long-term recovery cannot be implemented, and the portfolio's development objectives will remain unrealized indefinitely.
