**Social unrest and protests:** Pakistan's political environment in early 2026 is defined by the continued detention of former Prime Minister Imran Khan on 14-year corruption charges, mass arrests of PTI supporters under terrorism and sedition statutes, and a national strike called by the opposition alliance TTAP on February 8 marking two years since the disputed 2024 elections. Analysts describe a consolidating hybrid civil-military system in which the 26th constitutional amendment has strengthened executive influence over the judiciary, social media platform X remains intermittently banned to suppress PTI organizing, and Baloch and Pashtun civil society leaders including Mahrang Baloch and Ali Wazir have been charged under anti-terrorism laws. These dynamics directly threaten portfolio projects that depend on federal-provincial coordination and civil society engagement. **Violence between refugees, host communities, and the state:** Pakistan deported over 146,000 Afghans in the first two months of 2026 alone, part of a broader campaign approaching 1.4 million Proof of Registration card holders, with UNHCR documenting arrests, harassment, and evictions of Afghan nationals and only 15 percent of the required $454.2 million humanitarian response funded as of end-February. The Torkham border crossing was closed as a result of the Pakistan-Afghanistan conflict, disrupting civilian and humanitarian movement, while tens of thousands were internally displaced from KPK's Tirah Valley amid military operations and cross-border shelling. These dynamics directly threaten the Refugees and Host Communities Project, which is funded through IDA's Regional Sub-Window for Refugees and Host Communities and explicitly targets 519,303 refugees in KP districts; the PAD identifies the risk that Pakistan's protection framework could be changed due to the evolving regional situation, and the current deportation acceleration directly contradicts the voluntary repatriation principle the project depends on <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0879fa728&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166309 – PAD</a>. Separately, protests in Gilgit-Baltistan on March 1 linked to Shia mourning events resulted in 14–20 civilian deaths and the arrest of Awami Action Committee leaders under anti-terrorism charges, signaling a widening of sectarian targeting beyond traditional frontline provinces. **Political instability:** Pakistan's governance is characterized by a consolidating hybrid system in which civilian authority is subordinate to military power, the 26th constitutional amendment has weakened judicial independence, and the federal government is in direct political conflict with the PTI-governed KP province over resource allocation and security coordination. The IMF revised Pakistan's growth forecast for FY2026–27 down to 3.5 percent and raised inflation to 8.4 percent, while the World Bank cut the FY2025–26 forecast to 3 percent; public debt stands at approximately 72 percent of GDP with an interest-revenue ratio of approximately 60 percent, and FDI collapsed to $808 million in the first half of FY2026 from $1.425 billion a year earlier. These fiscal and governance dynamics threaten the entire portfolio. **External risks and intra-state conflict:** Pakistan launched airstrikes on TTP and ISKP camps in Afghanistan on February 22, 2026, following a deadly Islamabad mosque bombing and military convoy attack in Bannu; Pakistan's defense minister declared "open war" by February 27, and subsequent strikes hit Kabul, Kandahar, and Paktia province. A fragile Eid ceasefire brokered by Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Turkey held from March 18–23 but left core grievances unresolved. Simultaneously, the BLA launched Operation Herof II on January 31, executing over 76 coordinated attacks across 14 locations in Balochistan, while TTP recorded 146 attacks nationwide in March 2026 alone—a 76 percent increase from February—with growing BLA-TTP tactical convergence including joint radicalization networks and quadcopter drone deployment. India has continued to hold the Indus Waters Treaty in abeyance, threatening food security for approximately 80 percent of Pakistan's arable land. These dynamics create acute exposure across the portfolio. The Merged Areas Development Project operates in eight districts directly bordering Afghanistan—Bajaur, Khyber, Kurram, Mohmand, North Waziristan, and South Waziristan—where cross-border airstrikes, ground operations, and drone strikes were documented in March 2026, far exceeding the PAD's characterization of "low-grade conflict with occasional skirmishes" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-107c8a45-eb40-4cbd-85f2-4e94744ec5dd.pdf" target="_blank">P176780 – PAD</a>.
