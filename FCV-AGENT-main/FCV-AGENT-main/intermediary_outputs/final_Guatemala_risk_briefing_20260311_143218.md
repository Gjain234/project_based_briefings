# Guatemala FCV Portfolio Briefing

## 1. Political-Criminal Capture of Governance Institutions and the Reform Environment

Guatemala faces a deeply entrenched political-criminal nexus in which organized crime networks and corrupt elites have systematically co-opted state institutions—most notably the Attorney General's Office—to protect their interests and undermine reform. The IACHR has concluded that the Attorney General's Office operates as "an instrument for selective persecution," with its leadership sanctioned by the US, EU, and UK for corruption. This environment directly threatens the portfolio's governance-dependent operations. The Smart Public Financial Management (PFM) project is acutely exposed: its core objective of improving fiscal transparency and accountability directly threatens the opacity that enables political-criminal networks to extract rents from public finances, and the PAD explicitly flags substantial political and governance risk, noting that "potential changes in government leadership could disrupt implementation momentum" <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. The project's reliance on sustained commitment from senior MINFIN leadership is vulnerable if reform-oriented officials face legal intimidation or politically motivated prosecution—a documented pattern affecting journalists, Indigenous leaders, and government reformers alike <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. The rural roads project faces analogous exposure: its dependence on interinstitutional coordination with DGC, CIV, and municipalities assumes a functional legal environment, but if judicial appointments are captured by corrupt actors, enforcement of project contracts, resolution of land disputes, and accountability for environmental and social compliance could all be compromised <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The annulment of President Arévalo's Movimiento Semilla party by the Constitutional Court has further isolated the executive, reducing the government's policy levers and compounding the risk that reform commitments cannot be sustained through the 2027 electoral cycle. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization is yet available for the portfolio, but the structural conditions for disruption are firmly in place.

## 2. Gang-Driven Violence, Extortion, and Security Fragility in Project Areas

Surging gang violence—marked by a 21% increase in homicides in the first half of 2025 and a 39% rise in extortion complaints to over 25,000 in 2024—poses direct operational risks to both projects in the portfolio. The January 2026 state of siege, triggered by coordinated Barrio 18 attacks that killed 11 police officers, exposed the depth of prison-based command structures and the state's limited control over gang-controlled territories. The rural roads project carries the most direct exposure: the PAD explicitly identifies intervention areas in Huehuetenango and San Marcos as characterized by "high crime levels and weak state presence," and flags substantial labor influx and SEA/SH risks during construction <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. Road construction projects involving labor mobilization and equipment are typical extortion targets in gang-controlled areas, and the project's reliance on community participation through COMUDES and COCODES could be undermined if community leaders face threats or coercion <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The project's planned local labor hiring and employment opportunities for local populations are similarly vulnerable to gang recruitment pressure or extortion demands on contractors <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The Smart PFM project faces a different but related exposure: the PAD documents that Guatemala has experienced over 8,000 cyberattacks on the Tax Administration in 2021, ransomware on the Foreign Ministry in 2022, and attacks on Guatecompras and SICOIN in 2023, and Component 2's modernization of IFMIS and peripheral systems creates new attack surfaces during a period of heightened instability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. Ongoing emergency security measures and the transition to a nationwide state of prevention could also disrupt the movement of technical staff and procurement timelines. No ISR-stage evidence of materialization is yet recorded, but the operational environment remains highly adverse.

## 3. Criminalization of Indigenous Leaders, Human Rights Defenders, and Community Advocates

The systematic use of criminal law as a tool of harassment against Indigenous leaders, human rights defenders, and community advocates represents a structural risk to projects that depend on community participation, grievance redress, and land rights processes. UDEFEGUA documented 4,520 attacks against human rights defenders between January and September 2025 alone, while Global Witness recorded a sharp increase in murders of land and environmental defenders from 4 in 2023 to 20 in 2024. The rural roads project is particularly exposed given its substantial involuntary resettlement risk—physical displacement of dwellings and commercial stands is prevalent within road rights-of-way—and its special focus on Indigenous peoples, women, and vulnerable populations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The PAD's culturally appropriate grievance redress mechanisms and community participation frameworks assume a neutral justice system; if the Attorney General's Office continues to weaponize criminal law against Indigenous activists, project beneficiaries could face retaliation for asserting land rights or participating in environmental grievances <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The documented arrest of Indigenous leaders on terrorism charges for participation in peaceful protests illustrates the concrete form this risk takes. The Smart PFM project faces a related exposure: if reform-oriented technical staff and PIU personnel face legal intimidation, recruitment and retention of qualified personnel becomes significantly more difficult, directly undermining the project's institutional capacity mitigation strategy <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. The absence of an implementation body for the government's November 2025 public policy to protect defenders means that the formal protection framework remains non-operational. No implementation-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded for the portfolio to date.

## 4. Internal Displacement, Mass Deportations, and Humanitarian Funding Collapse

Guatemala faces converging displacement pressures—an estimated 242,000 internally displaced persons driven by gang violence, food insecurity, and climate shocks, combined with a surge in deportations from the United States that reached nearly 66,000 Guatemalans in fiscal year 2024 and is expected to accelerate. Simultaneously, the 2025 Humanitarian Response Plan received less than 20% of its required USD 100.6 million, NRC has closed its Guatemala operations, and USAID's dismantlement has disrupted protection and psychosocial services across high-risk regions. The rural roads project's beneficiary targeting and community engagement mechanisms are directly exposed to these dynamics: the PAD identifies 450,000 primary beneficiaries and 5.1 million indirect beneficiaries among inhabitants of selected rural areas, with special focus on Indigenous peoples and vulnerable populations, but this assumes stable communities <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. If displacement accelerates in Huehuetenango, San Marcos, and other intervention departments—precisely the areas where USAID-funded protection services have been withdrawn—the project's beneficiary base could become unstable and community participation mechanisms compromised by population flux <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The Smart PFM project faces a macroeconomic transmission channel: the PAD explicitly identifies remittances as accounting for 19.5% of GDP and driving 47.8% of government net income through VAT, and notes that a decline in remittance flows could reduce VAT collections and create fiscal vulnerabilities that constrain the government's capacity to sustain PFM modernization investments <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. The passage of a 1% US remittance tax and ongoing deportation surges compound this fiscal risk. The loss of USAID's complementary governance and stakeholder engagement programming also weakens the civil society oversight environment that both projects depend on for accountability and sustainability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a> <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>.

## 5. Food Insecurity, Chronic Malnutrition, and Climate-Induced Agricultural Shocks in Intervention Zones

Guatemala's chronic food security crisis—with nearly 47% of children under five chronically malnourished, rising to 58% among Indigenous children, and 2.2 million people requiring humanitarian assistance in 2025—is concentrated in the same highland departments where the rural roads project operates. El Niño-induced droughts devastated maize and bean crops across 54,000 hectares in 160 municipalities, and by October 2024, acute malnutrition in children under five reached 25,000 cases with 50 associated deaths, particularly concentrated in Alta Verapaz, Huehuetenango, and San Marcos. The rural roads project's intervention areas in the Highlands priority zone—explicitly including Huehuetenango and San Marcos—are therefore among the most food-insecure and nutritionally vulnerable in the country <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The PAD acknowledges outbound migration and loss of human capital as fragility factors in intervention areas; food insecurity and climate shocks are primary drivers of this migration, which can destabilize the community participation and local labor recruitment mechanisms the project depends on <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9fd7d239-f540-447f-ae10-ba1d29631b37.pdf" target="_blank">P178573 – PAD</a>. The project's focus on improving rural connectivity has the potential to reduce food insecurity by improving market access, but implementation timelines could be disrupted if communities are in acute crisis or if population displacement accelerates. The Smart PFM project faces an indirect exposure through the fiscal channel: climate-induced agricultural shocks reduce VAT-generating economic activity and increase demands on public expenditure for emergency response, potentially crowding out the sustained government investment in PFM modernization that the project requires <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-6a4472a2-a645-45dd-bc0d-6e635f83b0e3.pdf" target="_blank">P510861 – PAD</a>. Prolonged drought across Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador identified for 2026 suggests these pressures will intensify rather than abate during the project implementation period. No ISR-stage evidence of materialization has been recorded for the portfolio, but the geographic overlap between project intervention zones and the epicenters of food insecurity is a forward-looking concern of significant magnitude.
