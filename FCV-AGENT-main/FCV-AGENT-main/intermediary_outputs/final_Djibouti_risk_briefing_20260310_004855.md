# Djibouti FCV Portfolio Briefing

## 1. Governance Consolidation and Democratic Backsliding

Djibouti's removal of presidential age limits in October 2025—enabling President Guelleh to seek a sixth term after more than two decades in power—represents a fundamental deterioration in institutional checks and democratic norms. This concentration of executive authority creates an environment of political uncertainty and reduced rule-of-law predictability that permeates the entire portfolio. The Public Finance Management Modernization Project is directly exposed: its governance structure relies on a PFM Reform Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Finance, and the PAD explicitly identifies "vested interests within public administration" as a high political risk, with mitigation depending on "government commitment demonstrated through past successes"—an assumption that weakens as constitutional manipulation signals declining institutional accountability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The TVET and Skills Development Project similarly depends on multi-ministry coordination under the Economic Development Council, but the PAD's reliance on a government-led steering committee without independent oversight safeguards leaves it vulnerable to political capture as executive power consolidates <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The Djibouti-Addis Corridor Project faces analogous exposure, as its PAD explicitly warns that "ability to influence private sector finance mobilization depends on political will" and that "line ministries may lack incentives or resist change"—vulnerabilities that intensify under indefinite presidential rule <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing across the portfolio. The PFM project is experiencing a persistent "High" political and governance risk rating, with administrative delays in publishing governance decrees blocking project operationalization despite ministerial approval <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>. The Digital Economy Project has seen the ARMD regulatory board remain unstaffed since 2022, with no board appointments made despite repeated mission recommendations—a pattern consistent with deliberate institutional subordination to executive authority <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-c15be353-62d2-4f55-928e-a77385dc1393.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – ISR</a>. The TVET project's steering committee has been inactive, with governance coordination between government and development partners stalled and inter-ministerial training plans for refugees unexecuted <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>. Across the portfolio, the pattern is consistent: formal institutional structures exist on paper but lack the operational independence and political priority to function effectively, a direct consequence of governance consolidation at the apex.

## 2. Collapse of Independent Civil Society and Accountability Mechanisms

Djibouti's classification as "Not Free" by Freedom House—with journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested, and virtually no independent civil society remaining—creates a structural accountability deficit that undermines project monitoring, grievance mechanisms, stakeholder engagement, and fiduciary controls across the entire portfolio. The TVET Project's M&E system relies on "independent academic evaluation" and a GRM with "confidential reporting," but in an environment where independent verification is impossible and self-censorship is pervasive, these mechanisms are structurally compromised <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The PFM Modernization Project's Component 3 explicitly targets civil society engagement for public finance oversight, yet the PAD does not acknowledge that "only marginal voices present on social media" and that the civil society organizations it assumes can meaningfully participate in oversight do not functionally exist <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The Education Quality and Inclusion Project establishes a grievance mechanism "for all stakeholders," but the PAD does not address how refugee students, rural populations, or girls can safely report concerns in an environment of political repression <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. These risks are extensively realized. The Digital Economy Project has experienced repeated delays in adopting the Digital Code's telecommunications regulation, with the ARMD unable to operationalize essential governance tools—a pattern the evidence links directly to the absence of independent institutional pressure and civil society accountability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-9e33bd55-6107-428e-bde8-2dc02b8b55cf.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Djibouti-Addis Corridor Project has documented systemic non-compliance with labor and social protection standards affecting 150 daily workers, with no independent actors—unions, labor advocates, or media—to expose or correct violations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The GBV Prevention Project launched without accounting software in place, with delayed financial reporting and stalled NGO contract negotiations for service delivery—institutional failures that persist precisely because independent oversight mechanisms to flag and pressure remediation are absent <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178332-285d7898-9c2d-4514-b7ba-c25e1c82a9dd.pdf" target="_blank">P178332 – Aide Memoire</a>. The TVET Project's internal audit function has been structurally compromised, with the auditor lacking organizational independence, mirroring the broader erosion of institutional checks <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>. Across projects, the collapse of civil society is not a background condition but an active driver of implementation failure.

## 3. Refugee and Asylum-Seeker Hosting Under Severe Resource Constraints

Djibouti hosts approximately 32,000 displaced persons—23,000 refugees and 8,000 asylum-seekers, with 85% concentrated in refugee villages—alongside 150,000 non-registered migrants, representing roughly 5% of the total population. This hosting burden, sustained amid recurrent droughts and severely limited public resources, creates structural strain on service delivery, institutional capacity, and project implementation across the portfolio. The Integrated Health Service Delivery Project targets refugee populations directly, but the PAD acknowledges that 85% of displaced persons reside in villages with limited infrastructure and public service capacity, creating inherent service delivery gaps [P178033 | PAD]. The Emergency Food Security Project explicitly targets refugees for food stock distribution but does not specify allocation protocols between refugee villages and host communities, nor does it address contingency planning for competing demands under resource scarcity <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – PAD</a>. The TVET Project targets 3,165 refugees as direct beneficiaries but does not assess how recurrent droughts or resource scarcity would affect training delivery or beneficiary retention <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing with severity. The Health Project has documented refugee immunization rates of only 12% against an 80% target, with vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5%—concrete manifestations of the resource constraints and fragile public health infrastructure that characterize the refugee hosting environment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf" target="_blank">P178033 – ISR</a>. The TVET Project has recorded zero refugee enrollment in training programs and zero refugee employment outcomes (stuck at 5% against target), with the evidence linking these failures directly to the structural inability of the host country system to absorb refugee labor even with targeted interventions <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The Urban Poverty Reduction Project has seen refugee village restructuring plans for Ali Addeh and Holl Holl remain pending validation, with refugee-led CSO sub-projects recording zero implementation—reflecting both resource constraints and the institutional fragility of refugee management systems <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>. The Statistics Project has required boosted sampling of migrant and refugee households in both the national census and household budget survey, with field enumeration facing security concerns in settlements and population displacement complicating data collection <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171777-8dfe1d10-65dd-4d7a-96a0-701ceb6e77ed.pdf" target="_blank">P171777 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Emergency Food Security Project has achieved only 50% of food stock acquisition targets and zero water infrastructure rehabilitation, directly reducing drought response capacity for the 32,000 displaced persons the country hosts <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-79f711cb-4676-4798-95e4-844f5162676d.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – Aide Memoire</a>.

## 4. Ethnic Tensions and Structural Political Exclusion

Djibouti's political landscape is structurally defined by Issa Somali dominance—approximately 60% of the population—over governance institutions, while the Afar minority (approximately 35%) remains systematically marginalized from political participation and resource allocation. This structural imbalance creates persistent risks of elite capture, inequitable benefit distribution, and community resistance across projects operating in ethnically mixed or Afar-populated regions. The TVET Project's multi-ministry coordination does not address how ethnic balance will be maintained in steering committees or implementation units, creating potential for political interference if beneficiary selection is perceived as favoring particular groups <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The Djibouti-Addis Corridor Project lists "populations living in corridor area" as beneficiaries but provides no disaggregation by ethnicity or targeted inclusion mechanisms for the Afar minority despite their structural political exclusion <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. The Agri-Food Value Chain Project specifies three geographically dispersed implementation zones but does not address ethnic composition of target areas or mechanisms to ensure equitable benefit distribution across Issa and Afar populations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing in ways that compound implementation challenges. The Corridor Project has documented persistent "Substantial" stakeholder engagement risk, with evidence linking coordination failures to ethnic tensions and political exclusion dynamics that create trust deficits and resistance from marginalized communities <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>. The same project has seen resettlement of informal traders at Arta proceed without adequate community consultation, with the evidence noting that resettlement decisions carry heightened political sensitivity in a context of Issa-Afar political imbalance <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Urban Poverty Reduction Project has recorded a delayed refugee and social cohesion assessment, preventing early identification of whether refugee settlement patterns are exacerbating existing ethnic fault lines in target slum areas <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>. The Social Safety Net Project has documented opaque youth training program selection without pre-defined eligibility criteria or accessibility safeguards, creating a direct vulnerability to ethnic favoritism in a context where Issa Somali dominance in resource allocation is structurally entrenched <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Education Project has seen private Arabic schools—often associated with minority communities—refuse to adopt the national EMIS system, suggesting institutional fragmentation along ethnic lines and community distrust of state data collection mechanisms <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf" target="_blank">P166059 – ISR</a>.

## 5. Severe Youth Unemployment, Economic Inequality, and Structural Labor Market Failure

Youth unemployment exceeding 75%, endemic corruption, high public debt, and limited economic diversification create a structural economic fragility that undermines project sustainability, beneficiary engagement, and the viability of employment-oriented interventions across the portfolio. The TVET Project's employment outcomes depend on private sector absorption of nearly 24,000 trained graduates, but the PAD acknowledges "macroeconomic uncertainties" without conducting a labor market demand assessment or documenting employer absorption capacity—a critical gap given that the economy structurally cannot generate sufficient formal employment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The Agri-Food Value Chain Project targets youth as key beneficiaries but relies on technical assistance and business plan development without addressing the macro-level youth unemployment context, creating risk that training cannot be converted into actual employment or income <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>. The Social Safety Net Project's economic opportunity component faces implementation challenges because structural economic conditions—weak market access, limited diversification, and geographic isolation in remote localities—are beyond project scope to address [P508560 | PAD]. These risks are extensively realized. The TVET Project's online gig work component has stalled entirely, with the evidence indicating that supply-side training interventions cannot overcome demand-side constraints including limited digital infrastructure, low internet penetration, and lack of formal employment pathways <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. Labor mobility agreements with Kuwait and Qatar—a core project mechanism for addressing domestic unemployment—have recorded zero progress, with bilateral negotiations stalled and no agreements signed <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Social Safety Net Project has been unable to identify viable income-generating activities in remote localities due to weak market access and limited economic diversification, with the evidence characterizing this as a localized manifestation of Djibouti's economy-wide inability to create sustainable employment pathways <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Corridor Project has documented exploitation of 150 daily workers through non-compliant contracts and lack of social security registration, with the evidence directly linking this to the severe youth unemployment context: workers facing 75%+ unemployment have minimal bargaining power and are forced to accept exploitative informal arrangements <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Emergency Food Security Project has achieved only 50% of food stock acquisition targets and zero water infrastructure rehabilitation, with the drought declaration in June 2025 triggering resource reallocation away from preparedness toward emergency response—a cascading failure that disproportionately affects rural youth populations already facing the highest unemployment rates <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – ISR</a>.
