**Social unrest and protests:** Lebanon's political landscape is defined by compounding governance crises rather than discrete protest episodes. Parliament voted 76 to 41 on 9 March 2026 to extend its mandate by two years, postponing elections constitutionally required by May 2026 and drawing condemnation from opposition blocs and civil society as a continuation of crisis-enabled democratic backsliding since 2013 (LBN-2026-05). The Aoun-Salam government's inability to enforce its own March 2 ban on Hezbollah military activities, combined with the LAF commander's refusal to confront Hezbollah directly, has exposed a critical gap between political declarations and state authority, fuelling public distrust (LBN-2026-04, LBN-2026-09). The Lebanon Public Financial Management and Procurement Reform Project is susceptible to this dynamic: its core objective of restoring fiscal functions and renewing the social contract depends on parliamentary ratification of the IBRD Financing Agreement, which remains pending as of mid-2025 <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-5067fa60-78e4-4f5e-b6c1-5a428d852b01.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the loan's effectiveness deadline was extended to August 2025 to allow parliamentary ratification, and the project is rated High across political and governance, macroeconomic, institutional capacity, and fiduciary risk categories, with stakeholder tensions also rated High <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a>. The Beirut Urban Solid Waste Management Project similarly cannot obtain necessary approvals from Beirut Governorate and Municipality for planned waste drop-off centres, and contaminated rubble management activities cannot proceed within the project timeline pending Parliament ratification of a follow-on project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-f112aa75-2e9c-4c03-ac0e-46311eb3089b.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – ISR</a>. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project (LEAP) carries persistent High ratings for political and governance, stakeholder, and macroeconomic risks, with all PDO indicators at zero as of project effectiveness in August 2025 and only 0.25% of funds disbursed <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a>.

**Violence between refugees, host communities, and the state:** Lebanon hosts the highest number of refugees per capita globally, and the March 2026 escalation has dramatically worsened an already acute displacement crisis. Over 1.2 million people have been newly displaced since 2 March 2026, with approximately 140,000 sheltering in 684 collective sites at or beyond capacity; over 562,000 people have crossed into Syria, 63 percent of them Syrians (LBN-2026-02, LBN-2026-08). Social stigma against refugees is rising, with displaced Shia civilians encountering hostility from non-Shia communities and local authorities vetting arrivals for Hezbollah links (LBN-2026-04). Secondary displacement of over 90,000 refugees within Lebanon—99 percent Syrian—compounds protection risks including gender-based violence, family separation, and trafficking in overcrowded shelters (LBN-2026-08). The Lebanon Social Protection Enhancement Project targets approximately 147,000 Lebanese families and explicitly includes Syrian refugees, but mass displacement has rendered household-based verification visits impossible in affected zones and the DAEM beneficiary registry obsolete <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-07e5fa4b-e782-4ffd-ab74-21191c4b8c78.pdf" target="_blank">P178075 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the project's cash transfer operations in the Bekaa Valley—one of the poorest target regions—face access denial due to destroyed bridges and active hostilities, and the independent monitoring agency cannot conduct monthly data collection in conflict zones [P178075 | ISR]. The Lebanon Social Safety Net Reform Project similarly targets displaced Syrians alongside Lebanese nationals, but zero displaced Syrian beneficiaries have been enrolled against a target of 180,000, and zero conflict-related hospitalization costs have been financed against a target of 6,000 <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0867b7011&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P163476 – ISR</a>. The Lebanon Integrated Social Protection and Jobs Project, implemented by IRC, documents sporadic armed strikes on South Lebanon and Beirut's southern suburbs continuing to threaten beneficiary access, while several supported NGOs have lost core financing previously sourced from US resources, creating near-term capacity and sustainability challenges <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-bde1794c-f97c-48b9-a56e-1949644a584e.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – Aide Memoire</a>.

**Organized violence between political and sectarian groups:** The Hezbollah disarmament impasse represents the most acute organised violence risk in the portfolio. The Lebanese government took the unprecedented step of banning Hezbollah's military activities on 2 March 2026 and directing the LAF to disarm the group, but the LAF commander objected to direct confrontation, and Hezbollah has remained defiant, warning that enforcement could trigger sectarian defections within the LAF and risk civil war (LBN-2026-04). Analysts note that displaced Shia civilians have encountered hostility from non-Shia communities, signalling rising sectarian fracture lines (LBN-2026-04). The Lebanon Electricity Transmission and Distribution Rehabilitation Project depends on EDL and LRA as implementing entities operating under state authority, but the sovereignty deficit and non-enforcement of government decrees undermine the state's ability to protect project assets and maintain operational control over critical infrastructure in areas where non-state actors exercise influence <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-c260299b-4517-48c7-a0c1-b95eb42d17a7.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – PAD</a>. In implementation, the project carries persistent High ratings for political and governance, macroeconomic, institutional capacity, and fiduciary risk, with an additional unspecified High "Other" risk category <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180501-1bd1cf0b-8078-42e6-a4bc-c43a782224ca.pdf" target="_blank">P180501 – ISR</a>. The Lebanon Beqaa Wastewater Project has experienced direct materialisation of this risk: contractors suspended all works on the Zahle networks and Anjar Lot 2 contracts from 23 September 2024 due to escalating military activities in the Beqaa region, a High-Level Meeting planned for 30 September 2024 could not be held due to the security situation, and the World Bank mission was conducted virtually with explicit guidance to avoid work near active shelling and to clear unexploded ordnances before resuming <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Lebanon Solid Waste Management and Circular Economy Project documents security escalation causing slippage in Component 2 implementation in Nabatieh and Tyre, with data collection shifted to virtual tools; a stakeholder at the Srar Sanitary Landfill site has threatened to damage the facility if not included in the project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a>.

**Political instability:** Lebanon's political environment is characterised by compounding institutional failures. The parliamentary term extension of March 2026 is the fourth in a decade, and the Constitutional Council faces challenges to its legality (LBN-2026-05). The new government under President Aoun and PM Salam has made tangible institutional progress—resuming the Beirut port investigation, initiating electricity sector regulation—but is structurally fragile and unable to enforce its own decrees (LBN-2026-09). The World Bank projected 3.5 percent real GDP growth in 2025 and 4 percent for 2026 contingent on sustained reforms and stability; the March 2026 escalation has fundamentally undermined these conditions, with IMF program negotiations, banking sector restructuring, and sovereign debt resolution all stalled (LBN-2026-07). The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project (GATE) for agriculture and rural development achieved effectiveness only in August 2025 after a 14-month delay due to parliamentary ratification requirements, and as of January 2025 all PDO and intermediate result indicators remain at zero with only 0.25 percent of funds disbursed <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a>. The Lebanon Recovery and Reconstruction Project (LEAP) carries High ratings for political and governance, macroeconomic, institutional capacity, and fiduciary risk, with stakeholder risks also rated High <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a>. The Lebanon Health Resilience Project carries persistent High ratings for political and governance and macroeconomic risk across all assessment periods, with only 56 percent of public hospitals equipped with priority pharmaceuticals against a 70 percent target <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178587-8133ed2c-dff3-4ee7-a615-063ca255128b.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – ISR</a>. The Beirut Port Explosion Recovery Project documents persistent High ratings for political, macroeconomic, and stakeholder risks, with Component 1 housing rehabilitation delays posing risk to PDO achievement for three high-risk buildings with tight October 2025 completion deadlines <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176577-4f8e4069-1a4c-4b78-b5bb-344e2c6a59af.pdf" target="_blank">P176577 – ISR</a>.

**External risks and intra-state conflict:** Since 2 March 2026, Lebanon has been engulfed in renewed large-scale fighting after Hezbollah fired on Israel following US-Israeli strikes on Iran, triggering a sweeping Israeli air and ground campaign; a single wave of strikes on 8 April killed over 300 people and wounded 1,150 (LBN-2026-01). A fragile 10-day ceasefire announced on 16 April 2026 is of doubtful durability given Hezbollah's ambiguous stance and Israel's stated intention to retain a demilitarised security zone up to the Litani River and raze border villages (LBN-2026-11). UNIFIL's mandate is set to expire in December 2026, three Ghanaian peacekeepers were injured on 6 March, and the US has zeroed out its $125 million annual peacekeeping contribution, creating a systemic funding gap (LBN-2026-06). Seventeen incidents affecting water systems in Baalbek-Hermel, Bekaa, South, and Nabatieh have been documented, 82 attacks on emergency medical services have occurred, and the Qasmieh Bridge strike on 8 April severed the last operational crossing of the Awali River (LBN-2026-03). The Lebanon Recovery and Reconstruction Project explicitly acknowledges that geopolitical risks from Middle East conflict may impede implementation in border areas, requires Lebanese Army clearance for explosive remnants of war before investment, and relies on a Third-Party Monitoring Agency for site verification—all mechanisms now severely constrained by active hostilities <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a>. The Lebanon Solid Waste Management and Circular Economy Project has had South Lebanon interventions detained due to security escalation, the ESIA for the Nabatieh sanitary landfill was delayed by security issues, and management of asbestos-contaminated rubble at the Bakalian site cannot be completed within the project timeline <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-4727dec5-5d09-4ce2-8055-7dcb271aae12.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – ISR</a> <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Lebanon Beqaa Wastewater Project documents a worker seriously injured at Al Marj in June 2024, project closure deadlines at risk due to security-driven work suspensions, and a UXO clearance requirement before works can resume <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a>. The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project PAD explicitly states that works remain suspended and BMLWE operations severely disrupted, and that no works will commence until conflict has ended and sites are safe <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a>, with persistent High ratings across all risk categories confirmed in implementation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P504170-acef5b66-7711-4d27-aa64-52a05ecd1751.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – ISR</a>.
