# FCV Portfolio Briefing: Djibouti

## 1. Constitutional Revision Enabling Indefinite Presidential Rule

In October 2025, Djibouti removed the presidential age limit through constitutional revision, enabling President Ismail Omar Guelleh—in power since 1999—to seek a sixth term in April 2026. This fundamental governance deterioration directly undermines institutional checks and democratic norms, creating an environment of political uncertainty and reduced rule-of-law predictability.

Project susceptibility is acute across the portfolio. The Public Finance Management Reform project [P180696 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf) depends on sustained government commitment and institutional continuity through a multi-year implementation period, but constitutional changes signal weakening institutional checks and potential shifts in government priorities. The Education project [P166059 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes) identifies ministerial reshuffles as a substantial governance risk, with project coordination relying on sustained political priority that may be compromised by political consolidation. The Corridor Development project [P174300 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes) requires cross-border coordination and multi-stakeholder engagement that becomes fragile when executive power concentrates and institutional autonomy weakens.

Risks are materializing across implementation. The PFM project [P180696 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf) shows persistent High political and governance risk directly reflecting institutional fragility from constitutional manipulation. The Education project [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) demonstrates how political stagnation and tightened governance affect institutional capacity and project governance structures. The Corridor project [P174300 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf) exhibits persistent Substantial stakeholder engagement risk as political consolidation reduces institutional predictability and stakeholder confidence in government commitments.

## 2. Collapse of Independent Civil Society and Media Freedom

Djibouti has virtually no independent civil society remaining, with only marginal voices present on social media. Freedom House classifies the country as Not Free, citing severe restrictions on political competition and media freedom, with journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested for criticizing the government. This institutional hollowing eliminates accountability mechanisms and transparency structures essential for development project implementation.

Project vulnerability is structural and pervasive. The Skills Development project [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf) relies on transparent beneficiary selection, grievance mechanisms, and community participation mechanisms that assume functioning civil society oversight—mechanisms that do not exist. The Education project [P166059 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes) establishes stakeholder engagement and grievance redress mechanisms, but effectiveness is undermined by the absence of independent civil society to validate or amplify beneficiary concerns. The Slum Upgrading project [P162901 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes) depends on participatory processes and community facilitators, but restricted civic space constrains authentic community voice in project design and implementation.

Risks are actively materializing. The Skills Development project [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf) shows zero refugee enrollment and stalled online gig work components, reflecting institutional fragility where independent oversight mechanisms are absent. The Education project [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) demonstrates declining refugee student enrollment and non-functional School Management Committees (62% functionality), symptomatic of weak civic participation mechanisms. The Health project [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) exhibits service delivery gaps for refugee immunization (50% vs. 80% target) and vitamin A supplementation collapse (5% coverage), reflecting institutional accountability deficits where independent monitoring is absent. The Corridor project [P174300 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf) documents systematic labor non-compliance (150 daily workers without social security registration) and critical safety violations, enabled by the absence of independent labor oversight and media scrutiny.

## 3. Ethnic Tensions and Political Exclusion

Ethnic tensions are a primary driver of Djibouti's politics, with the Issa Somali ethnic group (approximately 60% of population) dominating governance while the Afar minority (approximately 35%) remains politically marginalized. This structural imbalance fuels grievances and limits inclusive political participation, creating institutional fragmentation that undermines project implementation across diverse constituencies.

Project susceptibility manifests in targeting and benefit distribution mechanisms. The Skills Development project [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf) targets 3,165 refugees (primarily Somali origin) through multi-ministry coordination, but lacks explicit ethnic representation safeguards in steering committees, creating potential for political interference or community resistance. The Slum Upgrading project [P162901 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes) targets both refugees and host communities (12,860 refugees, 35,900 host residents) with conflict-sensitive approaches, but does not detail how ethnic sensitivities will be navigated in resource allocation decisions. The Corridor project [P174300 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes) lists populations living in corridor areas as beneficiaries but provides no ethnic-disaggregated targeting or inclusion mechanisms for marginalized groups.

Risks are materializing through implementation tensions. The Slum Upgrading project [P162901 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes) shows persistent social conflict risk and unresolved tensions between refugee and host communities, reflecting underlying ethnic fault lines. The Corridor project [P174300 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf) documents informal commercial structures at Arta facing resettlement without viable alternative income sources, with potential for ethnic grievances if allocation is perceived as inequitable. The Health project [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) exhibits service delivery gaps that disproportionately affect vulnerable populations, suggesting unequal institutional reach across ethnic lines.

## 4. Severe Youth Unemployment and Economic Inequality

Youth unemployment in Djibouti exceeds 75%, compounded by high rates of inequality, poverty, and endemic corruption. Rising debt and limited economic opportunities create significant domestic pressure, particularly among a young population with few prospects. This structural economic crisis directly constrains project absorptive capacity and undermines the viability of employment-focused interventions.

Project susceptibility is fundamental. The Skills Development project [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf) targets 23,775 trained graduates for private sector absorption, but acknowledges macroeconomic uncertainties and 75% youth unemployment without documenting labor market demand assessment or contingency for limited job creation. The Corridor project [P174300 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes) identifies road transport service providers and local traders as beneficiaries but provides no youth employment targets or analysis of how corridor improvements translate to jobs for the 75%+ unemployed youth population. The Slum Upgrading project [P162901 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes) targets youth as vulnerable groups but does not detail how economic desperation may affect beneficiary cooperation or project sustainability.

Risks are actively materializing. The Skills Development project [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf) shows zero refugee employment outcomes (stuck at 5%), directly reflecting the broader structural economic crisis where labor market lacks sufficient formal sector jobs. The Slum Upgrading project [P162901 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes) demonstrates declining refugee student enrollment and stalled livelihood activities, reflecting economic barriers to participation. The Food Security project [P178988 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-79f711cb-4676-4798-95e4-844f5162676d.pdf) shows difficulty identifying viable income-generating activities in remote localities, a localized manifestation of Djibouti's economy-wide inability to create sustainable employment pathways. The Corridor project [P174300 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf) documents systematic labor exploitation (150 daily workers without social security, non-compliant contracts) enabled by youth desperation and minimal bargaining power.

## 5. Refugee and Asylum-Seeker Hosting Amid Resource Constraints

Djibouti hosts approximately 32,000 displaced persons (23,000 refugees and 8,000 asylum-seekers), primarily from Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen, with 85% residing in refugee villages. Despite recurrent droughts and limited resources, the country maintains an open-door policy, creating potential strain on already fragile public services and infrastructure. This humanitarian burden directly undermines project implementation capacity and service delivery sustainability.

Project susceptibility is acute and direct. The Skills Development project [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf) targets 3,165 refugees directly while operating through government ministries with limited capacity, creating implementation friction if refugee service delivery strains already-fragile institutional systems. The Health project [P178033 | PAD] acknowledges refugee protection framework risk but does not address sustainability of training delivery if humanitarian conditions deteriorate or if host government reduces support to refugee services. The Education project [P166059 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes) targets 29,214 refugees across five regions and refugee villages, but does not assess how resource constraints or humanitarian access risks would affect training delivery or beneficiary retention. The Food Security project [P178988 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf) explicitly targets 32,000 refugees and asylum-seekers for emergency assistance, but does not detail allocation mechanisms between refugee villages and host populations amid resource scarcity.

Risks are materializing across implementation. The Health project [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) shows service delivery collapse for refugee populations: immunization coverage at 50% versus 80% target, vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5%, demonstrating how resource constraints translate into deteriorating health outcomes for vulnerable populations. The Skills Development project [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf) shows zero refugee enrollment and completion data, reflecting institutional capacity limitations in reaching displaced populations. The Education project [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) demonstrates declining refugee student enrollment (596 to 384 between 2021-2025), directly reflecting resource constraints and service delivery challenges. The Food Security project [P178988 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf) shows critical shortfall in food stock acquisition (50% of target) and zero achievement on water infrastructure, directly affecting the 32,000 displaced persons the country hosts. The Statistics project [P171777 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0884c1bc2&stream=Yes) documents data collection challenges on refugees and asylum-seekers, requiring boosted sampling to navigate the displacement pressures and resource constraints that characterize Djibouti's refugee hosting environment.
