# Zimbabwe FCV Portfolio Briefing

**Risk 1: Democratic Backsliding and Constitutional Crisis**

Zimbabwe is experiencing a serious deterioration in democratic governance, driven by the government's push to extend President Mnangagwa's term through Constitutional Amendment Bill No. 3, which would eliminate direct presidential elections and extend parliamentary terms. Cabinet backed the bill in February 2026, with the Parliamentary Speaker gazetzing it for a 90-day consultation before a parliamentary vote widely expected to endorse it—despite constitutional experts, church leaders, and five opposition parties in Matabeleland condemning the measure as a constitutional coup. The African Union has been petitioned to intervene, and the move has drawn international scrutiny that risks complicating Zimbabwe's Structured Dialogue Platform for arrears clearance, a prerequisite for accessing normal IDA and international financing. The World Bank's own Country Engagement Note stresses that sustained policy consistency and reform credibility are essential for unlocking investment, and the constitutional amendments directly undermine that narrative. With no active World Bank projects currently mapped to this risk in the portfolio evidence base, the primary concern for the Bank is the enabling environment for its ZISET-financed operations and the broader donor confidence underpinning Zimbabwe's fragile macroeconomic stabilization path.

**Risk 2: State-Sponsored Repression and Shrinking Civic Space**

The Zimbabwean government has systematically narrowed the space for civil society, independent media, and political opposition through a combination of legal instruments and extrajudicial coercion. The Private Voluntary Organisations Act and amendments to the Broadcasting Services Act have expanded state powers to deregister civil society organizations and extend presidential control over broadcast regulation, while politically motivated arrests of senior journalists and activists—documented by Zimbabwe's own Human Rights Commission—have created a pervasive climate of fear. Human Rights Watch documented a coordinated campaign of violence and intimidation surrounding the gazetting of the constitutional amendment bill, including a suspected arson attack on SAPES Trust offices in Harare and the alleged assault of civil society leader Lovemore Madhuku by men accompanied by police vehicles. OCHA has specifically noted that humanitarian actors in Zimbabwe face bureaucratic impediments as partners navigate provisions of the PVO Act, directly constraining NGO operations on the ground. The termination of US$18.9 million in USAID-funded citizen participation programmes and US$12 million in youth governance initiatives has compounded this contraction, removing critical counterweights to state repression at precisely the moment they are most needed. With no active portfolio projects currently mapped to this risk, the concern is primarily one of operational environment and the sustainability of development gains.

**Risk 3: Collapse of US Development Assistance and Health System Fragility**

The Trump administration's cancellation of USAID programmes in early 2025 terminated approximately US$388 million in active projects in Zimbabwe, creating acute gaps across health, humanitarian, and governance programming. The cuts included over US$110 million in HIV/AIDS programmes, US$58 million in tuberculosis prevention, US$25.7 million for malaria, and US$16.5 million for maternal and child health across five provinces—losses that UNAIDS has directly linked to disruptions in HIV treatment services, medicine distribution, and patient monitoring. Global Fund support currently covers only 21 of Zimbabwe's 64 districts, leaving a substantial share of the population without a credible alternative financing backstop. The abrupt funding freeze has also damaged community-level trust built over decades, with analysts warning that sudden withdrawal risks reversing hard-won gains and undermining future development efforts. Compounding this, the termination of agricultural resilience programmes worth US$19.8 million intersects with persistent food insecurity affecting nearly half the population, particularly in rural areas dependent on rain-fed agriculture. The World Bank's Zimbabwe portfolio will need to assess whether its own operations in health and social protection face increased demand pressure or implementation gaps as a result of these funding withdrawals.

**Risk 4: Macroeconomic Fragility, Debt Distress, and Fiscal Instability**

Zimbabwe's macroeconomic environment remains deeply fragile despite a projected GDP rebound of approximately 6 percent in 2025, driven by improved agricultural output and elevated gold prices. The IMF projects sizeable and persistent fiscal financing gaps of approximately 1.25 percent of GDP in both 2025 and 2026, with no access to external financing and a material risk of renewed domestic arrears accumulation and monetary financing that could reignite inflationary expectations. Total debt stands at approximately US$23.6 billion, with outstanding external arrears constraining access to affordable financing and crowding out priority social spending. The withdrawal of USAID support—which provided approximately one-fifth of Zimbabwe's total aid—adds further downside risk to an already precarious fiscal position, while medium-term growth is expected to slow to around 3.5 percent as confidence in stabilization remains low. The World Bank's engagement is explicitly conditioned on reform progress, with the Structured Dialogue Platform for arrears clearance serving as the gateway to normal IDA access; democratic backsliding and governance deterioration risk stalling this process indefinitely. Rising mid-2026 US dollar inflation and the absence of a credible monetary anchor further threaten to erode household purchasing power and undermine the poverty reduction gains that Bank-financed operations are designed to support.

**Risk 5: Organized Crime, Illicit Mining Networks, and Community Security**

Zimbabwe's extractive sector is deeply penetrated by organized criminal networks, with violent machete gangs—known as maShurugwi—continuing to operate in gold-mining areas across Midlands Province, engaging in armed conflict over mine access, extortion, forced labour, rape, and murder. These gangs are documented to have links to politically connected gold cartels, and the ENACT Organized Crime Index notes that state-embedded actors facilitate illicit gold, lithium, and diamond flows to Dubai and other transit hubs through bribery of underpaid officials. The US Treasury sanctioned President Mnangagwa and ten other officials in 2024 for involvement in gold and diamond smuggling networks, underscoring the degree to which illicit extraction is embedded at the highest levels of the state. Separately, Chinese-owned companies—which control an estimated 90 percent of Zimbabwe's mining industry—have been associated with widespread environmental damage, labour violations, forced evictions, and water source contamination, generating sharp community grievances with limited legal recourse. The governance deficit around both artisanal and large-scale foreign mining investment creates social tensions that intersect with broader anti-government sentiment and risk undermining community acceptance of development programmes operating in affected areas. Weak rule of law, the absence of legal standing for artisanal mining cooperatives, and police susceptibility to bribery perpetuate a cycle of violence and criminality that poses a structural threat to stability in resource-rich regions.
