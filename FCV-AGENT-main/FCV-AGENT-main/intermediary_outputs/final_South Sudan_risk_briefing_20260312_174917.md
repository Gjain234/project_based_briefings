# South Sudan FCV Portfolio Briefing

## 1. Collapse of the R-ARCSS Peace Agreement and Return to Full-Scale War

South Sudan's 2018 Revitalized Agreement on the Resolution of the Conflict in South Sudan (R-ARCSS) stands at a critical juncture. The Reconstituted Joint Monitoring and Evaluation Commission warned in January 2026 that the deal is at a "critical crossroads," and the International Crisis Group assessed that the country had effectively "returned to war" following the SSPDF's major offensive in late January 2026. President Kiir's unilateral amendments to the agreement and cabinet reshuffles without consulting other signatories have further eroded its foundations. Every project in the portfolio is structurally exposed to this risk. Projects relying on government-led implementation—including the Ministry of Livestock and Fisheries for the livestock project, the Ministry of Agriculture and Food Security for the rural livelihoods project, and the Ministry of Energy and Dams for the energy access project—face the prospect of institutional collapse if full-scale conflict resumes, as government coordination mechanisms, project steering committees, and field operations would all be disrupted [P500553 | PAD; P169120 | PAD; P178891 | PAD]. The social safety net project explicitly acknowledges that the "2018 peace agreement [is] largely holding" as a baseline assumption, and the financial sector project's governance structure depends on a functioning Bank of South Sudan operating within a stable political framework [P177663 | PAD; P179278 | PAD]. Materialization is already evident: the energy access project has recorded zero implementation progress since February 2024, with governance and coordination gaps between government entities compounding the political instability <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178891-008ca349-4c37-4697-9301-9c6dbd23e9ef.pdf" target="_blank">P178891 – Aide Memoire</a>. The PFM project documents 9–10 months of civil servant salary arrears, frequent ministerial turnover in the Ministry of Finance, and failure to meet statutory budget submission deadlines—all direct consequences of the governance fragmentation accompanying the peace agreement's deterioration [P176761 | Aide Memoire; P176761 | Implementation Status and Results Report]. The education project has escalated its macroeconomic and institutional capacity risks from Substantial to High, explicitly linking this to the political instability stemming from the collapsing peace framework <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178654-1d2a0a33-5408-4d44-962f-daf3b0692ffa.pdf" target="_blank">P178654 – ISR</a>.

## 2. Intensified Military Offensive in Jonglei State, Mass Atrocity Risk, and Humanitarian Access Crisis

The SSPDF launched a major military operation in Jonglei State in late January 2026, involving repeated aerial bombardments of civilian areas, "no quarter" rhetoric from senior commanders, and ethnic militia mobilization that the UN Commission on Human Rights warned significantly heightens mass atrocity risk. Simultaneously, humanitarian access has deteriorated sharply: the WFP suspended activities in Baliet County (Upper Nile), an MSF-supported hospital in Lankien (Jonglei) was bombed on 3 February 2026, and the government ordered civilians, UN personnel, and humanitarian workers to relocate from several Jonglei counties ahead of military operations. Nearly 10 million people require life-saving assistance, but both sides are reportedly blocking aid. Multiple projects hold direct geographic exposure in Jonglei. The community resilience project targets Pibor, Fangak, and Twic East counties in Jonglei, with the PAD explicitly anticipating suspension of activities if IOM cannot access areas and providing for county replacement if security deteriorates <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-e8d67f4f-bc76-49af-9b6c-6099c748075b.pdf" target="_blank">P177093 – PAD</a>. The livestock project operates in Bor County and Twic East, where a six-month dry-season implementation window is already narrow and is now further compressed by active military operations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-f81886be-210a-4bc0-9a92-5cf656fbbc43.pdf" target="_blank">P500553 – PAD</a>. The health project operates in Jonglei (Jamjang, Pochalla) and has documented aerial bombardment in New Fangak causing facility closures and population displacement, with over 20 HSTP-supported health facilities closed due to ongoing security challenges <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181385-31c255fc-69d1-4cc6-88c2-e37e28acd22f.pdf" target="_blank">P181385 – Aide Memoire</a>. Pharmaceutical deliveries to Nasir and Ulang counties in Jonglei remain unresolved due to security force interference, roadblocks, and looting attempts <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181385-31c255fc-69d1-4cc6-88c2-e37e28acd22f.pdf" target="_blank">P181385 – Aide Memoire</a>. The WASH and hydromet projects have been unable to complete security risk assessments required before infrastructure construction can proceed, with site assessments delayed precisely because the January 2026 construction timeline coincided with the peak of the offensive [P500441 | Aide Memoire; P180611 | Aide Memoire]. The community resilience project's Third Party Monitoring Agent cannot be deployed to remote and conflict-affected zones, and civil works implementation is stalled due to the volatile security situation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177093-fdc25708-770e-40de-aa94-2679135bcf13.pdf" target="_blank">P177093 – ISR</a>.

## 3. Massive Internal Displacement, Refugee Pressure, and Sudan Conflict Spillover

As of late February 2026, approximately 280,000 people had been displaced by fighting and airstrikes since late December 2025, with over 235,000 displaced in Jonglei State alone. Pre-existing displacement was already at scale, with over 1.9 million IDPs and 2.3 million South Sudanese refugees in neighboring countries. South Sudan simultaneously hosts nearly 600,000 refugees—mostly from Sudan—and over 800,000 South Sudanese returnees who fled Sudan's conflict since April 2023, concentrated in Upper Nile and Unity states. RSF forces have increased their presence along the porous Sudan–South Sudan border, and RSF attacks on Sudanese oil infrastructure in 2025 briefly threatened to shut South Sudan's oil export capacity entirely, directly linking Sudan's war to South Sudan's fiscal stability. Projects across the portfolio are designed to serve displaced populations but are now overwhelmed by the scale and pace of new displacement. The community resilience project targets over 920,000 beneficiaries including IDPs, refugees, and host communities across 12 counties, with US$20 million from the IDA Window for Host Communities and Refugees; the recent displacement wave directly undermines beneficiary identification and targeting accuracy <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-e8d67f4f-bc76-49af-9b6c-6099c748075b.pdf" target="_blank">P177093 – PAD</a>. The health project targets approximately 330,000 refugees and host communities in Upper Nile and Jonglei—the states with the most severe displacement needs—and has documented that Boma Health Initiative coverage in refugee areas stands at only 16% against a 26% target, with TPM agencies unable to verify data in refugee zones due to access constraints <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181385-5b09fa0c-9926-43db-adf4-616d047b7fcb.pdf" target="_blank">P181385 – ISR</a>. The education project has modified its development objective to explicitly include refugee and host community children, but school rehabilitation in refugee-hosting areas has not yet commenced due to operational constraints <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178654-1d2a0a33-5408-4d44-962f-daf3b0692ffa.pdf" target="_blank">P178654 – ISR</a>. The agricultural livelihoods project has activated its Crisis Response Window to support 47,444 farmers facing acute food insecurity driven by displacement and flooding, and is actively engaging 11,811 returnees and 830 refugees <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P169120-7aa9496d-b33a-4d6f-9215-13a79cf81b04.pdf" target="_blank">P169120 – ISR</a>. The social safety net project targets 7,000 refugee households and 10,500 host community households in Maban and Pariang, and has documented tensions between refugee and host communities over equitable benefit distribution <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177663-9151675f-6e7a-4881-a0dd-256fab61878e.pdf" target="_blank">P177663 – ISR</a>.

## 4. Acute Economic Fragility, Oil Revenue Dependency, and Fiscal Collapse

South Sudan's economy is near-totally dependent on oil, which constitutes over 90 percent of government revenues and is routed entirely through Sudan's conflict-affected pipeline to Port Sudan. A pipeline rupture in February 2024 caused severe economic contraction, with hyperinflation averaging approximately 143 percent in FY2024/25 and poverty rising to an estimated 92 percent. While oil exports partially resumed in 2025 and the IMF reached a staff-level agreement for a Staff-Monitored Program, economic recovery remains contingent on security improvements that are now deteriorating. Government troops are reportedly largely unpaid, with elite loyalty undermined by reduced fiscal revenues. This macroeconomic environment creates compounding risks across the portfolio. The PFM project documents 9–10 months of salary arrears for civil servants, directly undermining the institutional capacity the project seeks to strengthen, and the Ministry of Finance has experienced frequent ministerial turnover and failure to meet statutory budget deadlines <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176761-bf79f177-54c7-42fd-a88d-6f811275abe1.pdf" target="_blank">P176761 – ISR</a>. The health project's government co-financing commitment of US$10 million was overdue as of October 2024, with the Ministry of Health recording only 31 percent budget execution against a 50 percent target; health worker incentive payments are delayed, contributing to Boma Health Worker attrition <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181385-31c255fc-69d1-4cc6-88c2-e37e28acd22f.pdf" target="_blank">P181385 – Aide Memoire</a>. The civil service reform project has failed to deposit required counterpart funding of US$3 million, reflecting systemic budget execution constraints rather than administrative delay <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P500500-59292fc4-855f-47ed-84e5-0ca6c3bf985d.pdf" target="_blank">P500500 – Aide Memoire</a>. The financial sector project maintains a persistent High macroeconomic risk rating, with currency instability and fiscal fragility directly undermining the viability of banking institutions and the central bank strengthening activities the project supports <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P179278-6eaa8a2f-4164-43d4-a658-b3edad29f602.pdf" target="_blank">P179278 – ISR</a>. The social safety net project denominates cash transfers in US dollars specifically to mitigate foreign exchange fluctuations, but acknowledges that the government's low fiscal commitment to social sectors poses a fundamental sustainability risk <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088dd19cd&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P177663 – PAD</a>. The livestock project explicitly identifies that 70 percent of oil exports were disrupted by the Sudan conflict, causing a fourth consecutive year of economic contraction <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-f81886be-210a-4bc0-9a92-5cf656fbbc43.pdf" target="_blank">P500553 – PAD</a>.

## 5. Conflict-Related Sexual Violence, Political Repression, and Shrinking Civic Space

The UN Commission on Human Rights in South Sudan has found that conflict-related sexual violence (CRSV) remains a "defining and persistent feature" of the crisis, with "widespread and systematic patterns of rape" perpetrated by all armed forces and groups, functioning as a "strategic instrument of conflict deployed to terrorise civilian populations, drive displacement, and fracture social cohesion." Simultaneously, the National Security Service has escalated arrests, detentions, and harassment of civil society actors, journalists, and political opponents under the 2024 NSS Act, with at least 114 documented cases of censorship and arbitrary arrests between July 2022 and July 2025. First Vice President Riek Machar remains under house arrest on politically motivated charges. These twin dynamics—systematic sexual violence and shrinking civic space—create compounding risks for projects that depend on community participation, civil society engagement, and protection of vulnerable populations. Every project in the portfolio identifies GBV as a high risk. The community resilience project notes that 65 percent of women and girls in South Sudan report lifetime experience of physical or sexual assault, and its GBV referral pathways depend on health and psychosocial support services that are now being destroyed by conflict <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-e8d67f4f-bc76-49af-9b6c-6099c748075b.pdf" target="_blank">P177093 – PAD</a>. The livestock project has prepared a SEAH Action Plan but acknowledges that escalation of CRSV to "systematic weapon of war" levels will overwhelm these mitigation mechanisms and create additional barriers to women's participation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-f81886be-210a-4bc0-9a92-5cf656fbbc43.pdf" target="_blank">P500553 – PAD</a>. The women's empowerment project documents that sector strategies and policies for GBV services remain unstable, institutional capacity for MGCSW is persistently weak, and the government lacks coherent policy frameworks to respond to the scale of sexual violence—with the health system collapse and USAID funding cuts further eliminating entry points for survivor support <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176900-a3e2718f-c6ca-487d-abc8-86c1b83e75d6.pdf" target="_blank">P176900 – ISR</a>. On civic space, the PFM project's community monitoring groups, budget literacy workshops, and media partnerships—designed to support transparency—operate in an environment where civil society participants face arrest and harassment <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088d01007&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P176761 – PAD</a>. The agricultural livelihoods project's participatory planning mechanisms and the social safety net project's grievance redress systems both assume a civic space that is actively shrinking, with the GRM for the civil service reform project not yet operationalized despite being a project requirement [P169120 | Aide Memoire; P500500 | Aide Memoire].
