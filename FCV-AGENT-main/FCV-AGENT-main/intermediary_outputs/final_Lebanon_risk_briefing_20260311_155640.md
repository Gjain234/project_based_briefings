# Lebanon Portfolio FCV Risk Briefing

## 1. Active Armed Conflict and the Collapse of Reconstruction Momentum

Lebanon's portfolio faces its most acute threat from the renewed Israel-Hezbollah armed conflict that erupted on 2 March 2026, when Hezbollah fired rockets and drones into northern Israel following the killing of Iranian Supreme Leader Khamenei, triggering large-scale Israeli military operations across southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and Beirut's southern suburbs—killing at least 294 people and injuring over 1,000 in the first eight days. The conflict is assessed as more asymmetric and unpredictable than the 2024 round, with no active diplomatic mediation capable of containing escalation. Every active project in the portfolio was designed with explicit awareness of this threat. The Lebanon Emergency Assistance Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> identifies southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and Beirut's southern suburbs as primary implementation zones and acknowledges that geopolitical risks may impede implementation and restrict World Bank supervision visits—these risks have now fully materialized. The Greater Beirut Water Supply Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a> explicitly states that "no works will commence until conflict has ended and sites are safe," and as of March 2026, all civil works remain suspended. The Lebanon Solid Waste and UPOPs Reduction Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a> operates in Union of Municipalities of Tyre and Abbasiyeh in South Lebanon, where Israeli ground incursions have been announced. The Lebanon Fiscal Management Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-5067fa60-78e4-4f5e-b6c1-5a428d852b01.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – PAD</a> relies on staff presence in Beirut ministries that are now subject to airstrikes. The Lebanon Social Recovery Project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-6dadd285-c9a6-442f-8faa-3a5cdb52d0ab.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – PAD</a> targets Beirut, Mount Lebanon, and the Bekaa Valley, and acknowledges that "broader escalation of tensions and/or conflict events in region could rapidly and unpredictably augment national context." Implementation evidence confirms these risks are materializing across the portfolio. The LEAP reconstruction project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a> shows high political and governance risk and high macroeconomic instability, with the UN Special Coordinator explicitly noting that "a major World Bank loan was set to jump-start reconstruction but progress has now collapsed." The Beqaa Valley water and sanitation project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a> documents that contractors suspended all sewage network works in the Beqaa due to escalating military activities beginning September 2024, and a subsequent aide memoire confirms that the project is now operating on virtual missions only, with work prohibited near areas of active shelling and UXO clearance required before any resumption. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a> records that Component 2 activities in southern Lebanon have been put on hold due to security escalation, with data collection in Nabatieh and Tyre service zones shifted entirely to virtual tools. The social recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-bde1794c-f97c-48b9-a56e-1949644a584e.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – Aide Memoire</a> documents sporadic armed strikes on South Lebanon and Beirut's southern suburbs as a direct implementation constraint. The development-to-humanitarian aid pivot now risks entrenching Lebanon in crisis-response mode rather than enabling the structural reforms the portfolio was designed to support.

## 2. Mass Internal Displacement and Acute Humanitarian Crisis

Lebanon's displacement crisis has reached catastrophic proportions, with over 667,000 people registered on the government's displacement platform as of 10 March 2026—an increase of over 100,000 in a single day—and approximately 120,000 sheltering in collective sites. Many of the displaced were uprooted for the second time since the 2024 hostilities, fleeing from southern Lebanon, Beirut's suburbs, and the Bekaa Valley. Over 4.1 million people—more than 70% of Lebanon's population—were already in need of humanitarian assistance before the March 2026 attacks. This displacement crisis directly undermines the targeting, beneficiary identification, and service delivery mechanisms of every project in the portfolio. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> was designed for 1.2 million affected people in priority conflict-affected districts, including 900,000+ displaced during the 2023–24 conflict, but the March 2026 escalation has created a new displacement wave that renders the original prioritization criteria and geographic targeting obsolete. The water supply project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a> explicitly identifies displacement from southern Lebanon to Beirut as a factor expected to "further strain BMLWE's capacity to meet growing water demand," and the project's land acquisition component for reservoirs and transmission pipelines faces compounded difficulty as housing alternatives for Project Affected Persons become unavailable amid mass displacement. The Lebanon Social Safety Net project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-07e5fa4b-e782-4ffd-ab74-21191c4b8c78.pdf" target="_blank">P178075 – PAD</a> relies on the DAEM registration platform and WFP beneficiary verification for cash transfer delivery, but displacement of registered beneficiaries from their registered locations makes distribution through money transfer agents operationally infeasible. The COVID-19 and Health System Resilience project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9c1bfedd-5761-415c-9838-dc89cfe988b8.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – PAD</a> targets 4.7 million people including 1.5 million Syrian and Palestinian refugees in high-concentration areas such as Akkar and Arsal, but displacement disrupts geographic targeting and strains the public hospital infrastructure the project depends on. Implementation evidence confirms these impacts are materializing. The social recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a> documents that the project's vulnerability to service delivery disruption in conflict-affected areas is directly connected to the displacement crisis, with challenges accessing GBV survivors, mental health cases, and displaced persons. The social recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-bde1794c-f97c-48b9-a56e-1949644a584e.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – Aide Memoire</a> records that NGO partners are losing core funding from US and other development partners precisely when humanitarian demand has surged, reducing civil society capacity to respond to the acute crisis. The health project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178587-8133ed2c-dff3-4ee7-a615-063ca255128b.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – ISR</a> documents that the project is tracking refugee and host community pressures on the health system, with hospital utilization by displaced populations straining already overstretched facilities. The Beqaa water project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a> records that security issues in Nabatieh and Tyre—primary displacement source areas—have forced the project to rely on virtual and remote tools rather than field-based implementation.

## 3. Sectarian Political Fragmentation, Institutional Paralysis, and Democratic Backsliding

Lebanon's confessional political system, based on power-sharing among 18 officially recognized ethno-religious groups, has generated a compounding governance crisis that directly constrains every project in the portfolio. Tensions between the reform-oriented government of President Aoun and Prime Minister Salam on one side, and the Hezbollah-Amal bloc and Parliament Speaker Nabih Berri on the other, have persisted over disarmament, electoral law, and fiscal policy. Senior Hezbollah officials have walked out of cabinet sessions over disarmament discussions. On 9 March 2026, parliament voted 76 to 41 to extend its term by two years—Lebanon's fourth such extension in a decade—postponing elections originally scheduled for May 2026 and raising long-term concerns about institutional legitimacy. The fiscal management project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-5067fa60-78e4-4f5e-b6c1-5a428d852b01.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – PAD</a> was explicitly designed to "operate despite ongoing political deadlock" through implementation at the technical level, but the PAD acknowledges that "high governance and political risks inherent to Lebanon due to confessional governance system, political uncertainty, and ongoing political deadlock" limit the government's ability to take expedient policy actions. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> requires coordination across the Ministries of Energy and Water, Education, Social Affairs, Public Health, and Environment, with regular inter-ministerial coordination meetings—a mechanism directly undermined by cabinet walkouts and sectarian fragmentation. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a> requires a Project Steering Committee including nine ministries and agencies, and the PAD identifies institutional capacity as a substantial risk given the Ministry of Environment's 30% staff loss since 2019. The Beirut Critical Environmental Recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-829b3a34-aa4e-4b31-a4d1-5d469e9f50b4.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – PAD</a> requires a Project Coordination Committee including the Ministry of Environment, Beirut Governorate, Municipality of Beirut, and CDR—all institutions whose coordination capacity is compromised by sectarian paralysis. Implementation evidence confirms these risks are materializing across the portfolio. The fiscal management project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a> documents that the IBRD loan has not yet achieved effectiveness due to pending parliamentary ratification, with the ISR explicitly citing political and governance instability as the binding constraint. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-f112aa75-2e9c-4c03-ac0e-46311eb3089b.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – ISR</a> records that the project has been unable to obtain necessary approvals from Beirut Governorate and Municipality for waste management infrastructure, and that Parliament has not ratified the follow-on LEAP financing. The Beirut environmental recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176635-f112aa75-2e9c-4c03-ac0e-46311eb3089b.pdf" target="_blank">P176635 – ISR</a> documents that contaminated rubble management activities at the Bakalian site cannot proceed because they are pending transfer to the LEAP, which itself awaits parliamentary ratification. The agricultural project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a> shows zero implementation progress across all indicators—zero farmers reached, zero SME loans approved, zero rural infrastructure completed—with the ISR attributing this directly to political instability and governance fragility. The social recovery project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P506127-d9f2a824-f45b-42d8-baa2-906f33700549.pdf" target="_blank">P506127 – ISR</a> documents persistent high stakeholder tensions and moderate institutional capacity constraints, with local NGO sub-grantees facing governance weaknesses rooted in the same confessional system dynamics.

## 4. Economic Crisis, IMF Reform Stalling, and Financial Sector Fragility

Lebanon's economic collapse is the deepest structural constraint on portfolio sustainability. Real GDP has declined more than 38% since 2019, contracting 6.6% in 2024 alone. Public debt is estimated at over 357% of GDP. The banking sector carries over $72 billion in accumulated losses, and depositors continue to face monthly withdrawal restrictions of $400–500. Parliament passed a bank secrecy amendment in April 2025—a key IMF requirement—but only limited progress has been made on other prior actions, with the bank resolution framework and depositor compensation plan remaining incomplete. The March 2026 conflict escalation has halted nascent recovery momentum entirely. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> identifies macroeconomic risk as high, noting ongoing economic crisis with 7.1% contraction expected in 2024, triple-digit inflation, and Lebanese pound depreciation of 98%, with project contracts denominated in US dollars as a mitigation measure. The water supply project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a> identifies a structural currency mismatch between revenues in Lebanese pounds and costs in US dollars, with approximately $1 billion per year needed for eight hours of electricity service, and notes that the Cash Waterfall Mechanism required to ensure EDL's access to foreign exchange was to be operationalized by June 2025—a deadline now rendered moot by renewed conflict. The social safety net project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IDU-07e5fa4b-e782-4ffd-ab74-21191c4b8c78.pdf" target="_blank">P178075 – PAD</a> identifies macroeconomic risk as high, with severe financial crisis, capital flight, currency collapse, multiple exchange rates, and banking system failures, and relies on USD-denominated cash transfers as a mitigation measure. The Beqaa water project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08452b660&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P147854 – PAD</a> requires government counterpart funding of $5 million and assumes tariff increases and 90% cost recovery by 2022—projections that have been overtaken by economic collapse. Implementation evidence confirms these risks are materializing across the portfolio. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a> documents high macroeconomic instability, with the UN Special Coordinator's March 2026 statement that "progress has now come to a screeching halt" directly validating the risk. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a> records that 52% of landowners in the Zahle sewer network project have not collected compensation, with the evidence explicitly stating that "devaluation of the LBP decreased substantially the compensation amount in real terms," making payments economically negligible. The agricultural project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180334-4899e0cd-2b9b-432c-88d6-4a6b7c577697.pdf" target="_blank">P180334 – ISR</a> shows a persistent high macroeconomic risk rating, with the ISR noting that the same political obstacles preventing IMF prior actions are constraining project implementation capacity. The health project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178587-8133ed2c-dff3-4ee7-a615-063ca255128b.pdf" target="_blank">P178587 – ISR</a> documents a persistent high macroeconomic risk rating affecting disbursements and operational costs, with currency instability and fiscal constraints limiting the government's capacity to maintain health service delivery. The fiscal management project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P181155-3aec5db1-a5d2-44c4-b63f-102a196768dd.pdf" target="_blank">P181155 – ISR</a> documents high macroeconomic risk and high fiduciary risk, with the project's core objective of restoring domestic revenue mobilization directly undermined by the political paralysis that has stalled IMF reform implementation.

## 5. Israeli Continued Occupation, UNIFIL Mandate Erosion, and Sovereignty Violations in Southern Lebanon

A distinct and compounding risk layer for the portfolio's southern Lebanon operations is the pattern of Israeli military presence and violations that predates and now intensifies the March 2026 escalation. Since the November 2024 ceasefire, Israel has maintained military presence at five positions and two buffer zones inside Lebanese territory near the Blue Line, conducted near-daily airstrikes, and recorded over 10,000 violations of Lebanese airspace between November 2024 and end-February 2026. Human Rights Watch in December 2025 accused Israeli forces of targeting reconstruction equipment. In February 2026, the IDF dropped glyphosate herbicide near the Blue Line in what UNIFIL sources characterized as a pattern to keep border areas depopulated. Compounding this, the UN Security Council in August 2025 adopted a resolution granting UNIFIL its final mandate renewal, requesting cessation of operations by end of 2026 and full closure by 2027—removing the only systematic documentation of violations and monitoring mechanism in southern Lebanon. Three Ghanaian peacekeepers were injured on 6 March 2026. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-92339148-e75a-4dca-8bc2-580a6104a46a.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – PAD</a> explicitly includes southern Lebanon as a project location and notes that Israeli forces have targeted reconstruction equipment, making it impossible to implement reconstruction activities in occupied areas. The water supply project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-46a4ccd6-8f02-40fa-b05f-21af84f07850.pdf" target="_blank">P504170 – PAD</a> identifies unexploded ordnance near worksites as an exacerbated risk due to Middle East conflict, and notes that as of November 2024, implementation was severely challenged despite no reported damage to infrastructure. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-1e05d46a-942a-40a9-b99e-73b57a786469.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – PAD</a> operates in Union of Municipalities of Tyre, Municipality of Abbasiyeh, and Union of Municipalities of Sahel el Zahrani—all within the UNIFIL operational area and directly exposed to Israeli military presence. The Beqaa water project <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08452b660&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P147854 – PAD</a> targets Zahle, West Beqaa, Baalbeck, Hermel, and Rachaya districts, and the PAD notes that security incidents in the Beqaa region affect project implementation. Implementation evidence confirms these risks are materializing. The Beqaa water project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P147854-903aa505-bae1-44ca-bf98-11a99cbf798e.pdf" target="_blank">P147854 – Aide Memoire</a> documents that the ESIA for the Nabatieh Sanitary Landfill was delayed due to security issues in early 2025, with Nabatieh located directly in the zone of Israeli military operations and ceasefire violations. The solid waste project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172770-d2ffb9b1-6857-4db5-a132-6f8948899097.pdf" target="_blank">P172770 – Aide Memoire</a> records that implementation delays in Nabatieh and Tyre service zones due to unstable security conditions have forced the project to shift to virtual data collection, with in-person field work rendered unsafe by the conflict environment. The LEAP project <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509428-8183afa5-2d83-4fd5-9788-2230e5216342.pdf" target="_blank">P509428 – ISR</a> documents high stakeholder risk and high institutional capacity risk, with the impending UNIFIL closure by 2027 removing the only systematic monitoring mechanism in southern Lebanon and signaling to all stakeholders that international commitment to Lebanon's stability is ending—further delegitimizing the government institutions the project depends on for implementation.
