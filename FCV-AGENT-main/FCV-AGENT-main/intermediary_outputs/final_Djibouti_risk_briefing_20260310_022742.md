# Djibouti FCV Portfolio Briefing

## 1. Democratic Backsliding and Indefinite Presidential Rule

Djibouti's October 2025 constitutional revision removing presidential age limits—enabling President Guelleh to seek a sixth term in April 2026—represents a fundamental deterioration in institutional checks and governance predictability. This political consolidation creates direct exposure across the portfolio. The TVET and skills development project relies on a multi-ministry steering committee under the Economic Development Council, but the PAD's mitigation strategy of a "high-level steering committee" provides no insulation against executive capture in an increasingly centralized political environment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The public finance management reform project faces analogous vulnerability: its governance structure depends on a PFM Reform Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Finance, and the PAD explicitly identifies vested interests as a high political risk while relying on "government commitment demonstrated through past successes"—an assumption that weakens as political attention shifts toward consolidating power ahead of 2026 <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The corridor development project similarly flags that "ability to influence private sector finance mobilization depends on political will," with mitigation contingent on "commitment at highest state level"—a fragile foundation under indefinite executive rule <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing: the PFM project's ISR documents a persistent "High" political and governance risk rating, with the constitutional revision directly undermining the institutional predictability required for reform implementation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>. The TVET project's Aide Memoire records stalled steering committee activity and governance coordination failures, with inter-ministerial processes increasingly dependent on executive discretion rather than formalized procedures <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>. The digital connectivity project's ISR similarly flags a sustained "High" political and governance risk, with the concentration of power correlating with weakened institutional autonomy across regulatory bodies <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-c15be353-62d2-4f55-928e-a77385dc1393.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – ISR</a>.

## 2. Severe Youth Unemployment and Economic Inequality

With youth unemployment exceeding 75% and endemic corruption constraining economic mobility, Djibouti's structural economic fragility creates pervasive implementation risk across the portfolio. The TVET project's core theory of change—training 23,775 graduates for labor market absorption—is directly undermined by this context: the PAD acknowledges macroeconomic risk as "substantial" but documents no labor market demand assessment or private sector absorption capacity analysis, leaving trained cohorts potentially without viable employment pathways <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The agri-food development project similarly targets youth as direct beneficiaries but provides no youth employment targets or mechanisms to convert technical assistance into actual income opportunities within a structurally constrained economy <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>. The corridor project identifies "road transport service providers and local traders" as beneficiaries but includes no youth employment targets or analysis of how infrastructure improvements translate to jobs for the 75%+ unemployed youth population <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. These risks are actively materializing. The TVET project's ISR records that the online gig work component—designed specifically to address youth unemployment—has achieved zero results, with implementation failures suggesting the project underestimated structural barriers including limited digital infrastructure and insufficient formal employment pathways <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – ISR</a>. The corridor project's Aide Memoire documents systematic labor violations affecting 150 daily workers, with non-compliant contracts and lack of social security registration directly enabled by workers' minimal bargaining power in a 75%+ unemployment environment <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The food security project's ISR records that drought-driven reallocation of resources exacerbates economic inequality and food access disparities, disproportionately affecting vulnerable youth populations already facing acute economic pressure <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – ISR</a>.

## 3. Ethnic Tensions and Political Exclusion

The structural dominance of the Issa Somali ethnic group (approximately 60% of the population) in governance, combined with the systematic political marginalization of the Afar minority (approximately 35%), creates persistent risks of exclusionary implementation, elite capture, and contested resource allocation across the portfolio. The TVET project targets beneficiaries across six regions but includes no explicit ethnic representation safeguards in its steering committee or implementation units, creating vulnerability to political interference if beneficiary selection is perceived as favoring dominant ethnic constituencies <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The emergency response project explicitly targets vulnerable groups including refugees, migrants, and internally displaced persons across nationally diverse locations, but the PAD does not address how ethnic-based political exclusion may result in unequal resource allocation during emergency response <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. The education project targets 29,214 refugees across five regions and refugee villages, with integration into the national education system potentially becoming politically contested if ethnic tensions intensify around resource allocation to non-Djiboutian populations <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. These dynamics are manifesting in implementation. The corridor project's ISR records persistent "Substantial" stakeholder engagement risk, with ethnic marginalization of the Afar minority creating trust deficits and coordination failures that impede inclusive implementation cooperation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>. The social protection project's Aide Memoire documents that youth training programs lack pre-defined eligibility criteria and transparent selection procedures, creating a direct mechanism through which ethnic favoritism could operate—reproducing structural inequalities at the project level <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf" target="_blank">P508560 – Aide Memoire</a>. The urban upgrading project's ISR records that refugee-led CSO participation has achieved zero implementation, with refugees predominantly from Somalia and Ethiopia facing compounded barriers that mirror the country's ethnic-based exclusionary governance dynamics <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>.

## 4. Collapse of Independent Civil Society and Media Freedom

Freedom House classifies Djibouti as "Not Free," with journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested for criticizing the government and virtually no independent civil society remaining. This creates a structural implementation gap across the entire portfolio, as projects designed with stakeholder engagement, grievance redress, and community feedback mechanisms are operating in an environment where these functions cannot be independently performed. The PFM reform project's Component 3 explicitly targets "civil society engagement" and aims to "improve transparency and citizen engagement," yet the PAD does not acknowledge that the country context makes meaningful independent civil society participation structurally impossible <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The statistics project relies on "systematic citizen engagement interventions" as a mitigation strategy for political risk, but this approach depends on civil society that does not exist in practice <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087c0f9e4&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P171777 – PAD</a>. The TVET project's M&E system relies on "independent academic evaluation" and "confidential reporting," but both mechanisms are vulnerable to government pressure or self-censorship in an environment where independent verification is structurally compromised <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. These risks are pervasively realized. The corridor project's Aide Memoire documents systematic non-compliance with labor and social protection standards affecting 150 workers, with no independent unions, labor advocates, or media scrutiny to expose violations—a direct consequence of collapsed civil society oversight <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – Aide Memoire</a>. The GBV prevention project's Aide Memoire records stalled NGO contract negotiations, absent accounting systems, and delayed financial reporting, with the collapse of independent organizational capacity creating structural barriers to civil society service delivery partnerships <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178332-285d7898-9c2d-4514-b7ba-c25e1c82a9dd.pdf" target="_blank">P178332 – Aide Memoire</a>. The urban upgrading project's ISR documents that refugee-led CSO sub-projects have achieved zero implementation, consistent with the hostile environment for autonomous civil society that extends to refugee communities <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>.

## 5. FRUD-armé Insurgent Activity and Terrorism Threat

The FRUD-armé splinter group's October 2023 raid on an army post—killing seven soldiers—demonstrates active domestic insurgent capacity, compounded by Djibouti's proximity to al-Shabaab in Somalia. This security threat creates direct operational risks for projects with field presence in remote and border-proximate regions. The TVET project operates across six regions including Ali Sabieh and Obock, where 3,165 refugee beneficiaries are concentrated in camps geographically proximate to documented insurgent activity; the PAD's security risk mitigation is limited to "institutional protocols and safe learning environments" without any specific threat assessment for FRUD-armé <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The corridor project's infrastructure along RN1, RN5, and RN19 and the border crossing at Guelileh faces acknowledged "security/fragility (substantial)" risk from regional instability, but mitigation relies only on "regular security risk assessments" without specific protocols for domestic insurgent threats to road rehabilitation sites or border operations <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. The agri-food project operates in the Northern Zone (Tadjourah and Obock regions) with climate-proof rural infrastructure including boreholes and rural roads, yet the PAD asserts "no security risks identified for project implementation" despite FRUD-armé's demonstrated capability to conduct armed raids in proximate areas <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-9320408e-8d34-46ff-a14e-0221bdaeab77.pdf" target="_blank">P178836 – PAD</a>. The statistics project's field operations—including population census and household surveys requiring enumerators to access communities across national territory—face direct security risks from active insurgent threats, with the PAD acknowledging "road safety concerns during census activities" in conflict-affected areas <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087c0f9e4&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P171777 – PAD</a>. Implementation evidence confirms these concerns are not hypothetical: the statistics project's Aide Memoire documents security concerns in refugee settlements as a concrete implementation barrier for field enumeration teams, with insurgent activity creating genuine operational hazards <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171777-8dfe1d10-65dd-4d7a-96a0-701ceb6e77ed.pdf" target="_blank">P171777 – Aide Memoire</a>.

## 6. Escalating Djibouti-Somaliland Diplomatic Crisis

Djibouti's assertive military alignment with Mogadishu, the unprecedented mutual recall of ambassadors with Somaliland, and evidence of coordination with anti-Somaliland militias represent a rapidly deteriorating regional security environment with direct portfolio implications. The emergency response project serves 32,000 refugees and asylum-seekers primarily from Somalia, and the Somaliland crisis could destabilize this population, trigger new displacement waves, or create security restrictions on humanitarian access to refugee areas <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. The corridor project's cross-border operations at the Guelileh One-Stop Border Post depend on bilateral customs cooperation with Ethiopia, but the PAD does not assess how Djibouti's military alignment with Mogadishu and diplomatic crisis with Somaliland could create instability at the border crossing or complicate bilateral coordination <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. The refugee and host community integration project targets 12,860 refugees primarily from Somalia operating in Ali Sabieh region settlements; escalating regional tensions could force movement restrictions, security threats, or displacement of these beneficiaries, directly undermining social cohesion objectives <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf" target="_blank">P506969 – PAD</a>. These risks are beginning to materialize in implementation. The corridor project's ISR explicitly identifies the Djibouti-Somaliland diplomatic crisis as directly threatening cross-border coordination capacity essential for corridor project implementation, with deteriorating regional relations reducing political will for sustained bilateral cooperation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>. The TVET project's Aide Memoire records stalled bilateral labor mobility negotiations with Gulf states, with Djibouti's regional positioning and diplomatic isolation potentially affecting its negotiating leverage with Kuwait and Qatar <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – Aide Memoire</a>.

## 7. Refugee and Asylum-Seeker Hosting Amid Resource Constraints

Djibouti hosts approximately 32,000 displaced persons—23,000 refugees and 8,000 asylum-seekers—with 85% residing in refugee villages, alongside 150,000 non-registered migrants, placing acute strain on already fragile public services amid recurrent droughts and limited fiscal resources. This hosting burden creates structural implementation constraints across the portfolio's most vulnerable beneficiary groups. The emergency response project's implementing institutions—SEGRC and MEFI—are identified as having "limited capacity due to lack of funding and staff," creating a structural mismatch between the project's refugee and migrant targeting and the baseline institutional capacity available to serve them <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf" target="_blank">P509082 – PAD</a>. The TVET project directly targets 3,165 refugees for skills training but does not address sustainability of training delivery if humanitarian conditions deteriorate or if competing priorities (food security, shelter) undermine training participation and completion rates <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf" target="_blank">P175483 – PAD</a>. The food security project explicitly targets emergency food stocks for refugees but specifies no allocation percentages or distribution protocols between refugee villages and host communities, creating risk of competing claims during resource-scarce crisis periods <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – PAD</a>. These risks are extensively realized across the portfolio. The health project's ISR records refugee immunization rates stuck at 12% against an 80% target, with vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5%—concrete manifestations of insufficient institutional capacity to maintain basic preventive health interventions for refugee populations <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf" target="_blank">P178033 – ISR</a>. The food security project's ISR documents that the June 2025 drought declaration triggered resource reallocation away from preparedness toward emergency response, with the project achieving only 50% of food stock targets and zero water infrastructure rehabilitation—directly compounding the humanitarian strain on refugee-hosting capacity <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf" target="_blank">P178988 – ISR</a>. The urban upgrading project's ISR records zero implementation of refugee law recommendations, zero refugee-led CSO sub-projects, and stalled property rights documentation in refugee villages, demonstrating systemic institutional failure to operationalize refugee integration commitments <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>.

## 8. Presidential Succession Uncertainty and Health Concerns

Despite the constitutional revision clearing a formal path to a sixth presidential term, recurring health concerns and speculation about whether President Guelleh may step down before the April 2026 elections create significant political unpredictability that compounds governance fragility across the portfolio. Projects with multi-year implementation horizons and governance structures dependent on ministerial continuity are particularly exposed. The urban upgrading project's governance structure—a Steering Committee chaired by the Minister of Economy and Finance—relies on government counterpart financing and sustained institutional commitment, but the PAD does not address how succession uncertainty or potential presidential transition may affect government focus or create political disruptions during implementation <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – PAD</a>. The PFM reform project's governance depends on a Steering Committee co-chaired by Ministers of Finance and Budget; succession dynamics could disrupt ministerial leadership and the sustained political priority required for complex institutional reforms <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The education project explicitly identifies "ministerial reshuffles" as a substantial governance risk, with project coordination relying on the Secretary General as project lead—a structure vulnerable to leadership transitions during a politically uncertain period <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P166059 – PAD</a>. These risks are materializing in implementation. The PFM project's ISR documents a persistent "High" political and governance risk rating, with succession uncertainty explicitly identified as creating institutional unpredictability that affects governance continuity and policy implementation <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>. The corridor project's ISR similarly records that succession uncertainty and health concerns create political unpredictability that directly sustains "Substantial" governance risk, with unclear leadership transitions undermining institutional continuity and the sustained political commitment necessary for multi-year project cycles <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf" target="_blank">P174300 – ISR</a>.

## 9. International Community Tolerance of Democratic Backsliding

The international community's strategic prioritization of Djibouti's geopolitical stability—hosting eight foreign military bases and serving as a critical Horn of Africa logistics hub—has created a permissive environment for governance deterioration without meaningful external accountability pressure. This dynamic amplifies every other risk in the portfolio by removing the external incentives that would otherwise constrain institutional dysfunction. The digital connectivity project's PAD identifies "substantial" political risk requiring "Bank-executed technical assistance to clarify procedures," but if development partner relations strain due to governance concerns, the government may resist external technical assistance or limit Bank access to implementation processes [P174461 | PAD]. The PFM reform project similarly depends on "continued sector policy dialogue" and technical assistance from development partners, with no contingency planning for reduced donor engagement if governance deteriorates further <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – PAD</a>. The corridor project's political risk mitigation relies on "involving donor agencies and stakeholders to emphasize project importance"—an approach that weakens if development partners reduce engagement due to democratic backsliding concerns <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P174300 – PAD</a>. These dynamics are actively shaping implementation outcomes. The digital connectivity project's Aide Memoire records that the ARMD regulatory authority has failed to appoint board members since 2022 and has not adopted the Digital Code Book Two, with the permissive international environment enabling the government to deprioritize institutional commitments without facing development partner sanctions <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-9e33bd55-6107-428e-bde8-2dc02b8b55cf.pdf" target="_blank">P174461 – Aide Memoire</a>. The PFM project's ISR documents that governance structures required for PFM reform remain unestablished, with the international community's tolerance of democratic backsliding reducing external pressure for genuine institutional effectiveness <a href="https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf" target="_blank">P180696 – ISR</a>. The urban upgrading project's ISR records that the Land Directorate unilaterally stalled land regularization since October 2023 without apparent consequences, reflecting the broader pattern where limited external accountability enables domestic agencies to deprioritize donor-supported initiatives <a href="http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes" target="_blank">P162901 – ISR</a>.
