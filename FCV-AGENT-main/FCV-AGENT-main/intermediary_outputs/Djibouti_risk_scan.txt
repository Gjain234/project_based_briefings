**Risk Overview:** Djibouti has evolved into a hyper-congested strategic garrison, where eight foreign powers are compressed into a territory the size of Vermont, creating an environment of high-stakes deconfliction and systemic friction. ([Horn Review](https://hornreview.org/2026/01/21/djibouti-at-the-center-of-growing-global-security-threats/)) In October 2025, Djibouti adopted a partial constitutional revision that removed the presidential age limit, clearing the way for President Ismail Omar Guelleh—in power since 1999—to seek a sixth term in 2026. ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/news/voices/djiboutis-new-constitution-between-longevity-power-and-beginnings-socio-political-instability)) Domestically, the country has high rates of inequality, poverty, and endemic corruption ([International IDEA](https://www.idea.int/democracytracker/country/djibouti)), while youth unemployment exceeds 75%. ([Library of Congress](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11303)) Regionally, Djibouti is evolving from a strategic asset into a congested proxy theatre where foreign military saturation invites inevitable friction, having effectively auctioned its sovereignty to seven major powers. ([Horn Review](https://hornreview.org/2026/01/03/djiboutis-ports-strategic-alignments-and-emerging-regional-fissures/)) The growing tension between Somaliland and Djibouti has reached a critical juncture, marked by Djibouti's unexpected and assertive political and military alignment with Mogadishu, culminating in the unprecedented recall of ambassadors from each other's capitals. ([SomTribune](https://www.somtribune.net/2026/01/04/djiboutis-counterproductive-alignment-with-somalia-implications-on-itself-and-the-region/)) An April 2026 presidential election looms amid succession uncertainty and limited democratic space.

**Institutional fragility, political tensions, and social unrest:** Djibouti's October 2025 constitutional revision removed the presidential age limit and tightened eligibility requirements, adopting a reform amid a tightly controlled political environment. ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/news/voices/djiboutis-new-constitution-between-longevity-power-and-beginnings-socio-political-instability)) Opposition figures have described the move as deepening political stagnation, and Freedom House continues to classify Djibouti as Not Free, citing restrictions on political competition and media freedom. ([Addis Standard](https://addisstandard.com/crumbling-of-rentier-fortress-ethiopias-maritime-pivot-decline-of-djiboutis-monopoly/)) There is virtually no independent civil society left in the country, and the few voices that identify as such are only present on social media. ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/news/voices/djiboutis-new-constitution-between-longevity-power-and-beginnings-socio-political-instability)) High youth unemployment and rising debt compound domestic pressure. ([Addis Standard](https://addisstandard.com/crumbling-of-rentier-fortress-ethiopias-maritime-pivot-decline-of-djiboutis-monopoly/)) Ethnic tensions are one of the primary drivers of Djibouti's politics ([International IDEA](https://www.idea.int/democracytracker/country/djibouti)), with Guelleh and most other governing party officials members of the Issa Somali ethnic group, comprising approximately 60% of the population, while the Afar minority comprises approximately 35%. ([International IDEA](https://www.idea.int/democracytracker/country/djibouti)) Despite Guelleh's clear path to holding the presidency, Djibouti must navigate considerable political instability, and Guelleh has faced recurring health concerns, which fueled speculation that he may step down prior to the 2026 polls. ([Africa Center](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/en-elections-2026/djibouti/))

**Conflict and violence dynamics:** In October 2023, the splinter group FRUD-armé launched its first attack since January 2021, a raid on an army post in the Tadjourah region, leaving seven soldiers dead, four injured and six missing. ([International Crisis Group](https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/horn-africa/djibouti)) The National Assembly passed a law in October 2023 classifying FRUD-armé as a terrorist group. ([International Crisis Group](https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/horn-africa/djibouti)) Given Djibouti's proximity to prominent terrorist organizations such as al-Shabaab that control territory in neighboring Somalia, Djiboutian government officials prioritize counterterrorism efforts. ([U.S. State Department](https://www.state.gov/reports/country-reports-on-terrorism-2022/djibouti)) Djibouti has been a steady contributor to regional security efforts in Somalia, maintaining 1,800 troops as part of the African Union's peace force to help combat al-Shabaab, and funds a separate 300-strong unit to help secure Somalia's president and key infrastructure. ([Africa Center](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/en-elections-2026/djibouti/)) Tension with Somaliland has reached a critical juncture, marked by Djibouti's assertive military alignment with Mogadishu and the unprecedented recall of ambassadors. ([SomTribune](https://www.somtribune.net/2026/01/04/djiboutis-counterproductive-alignment-with-somalia-implications-on-itself-and-the-region/)) President Guelleh appears to have turned to a "wag the dog" strategy, manufacturing a foreign crisis to rally a disgruntled populace by coordinating with regional intelligence to provide logistical corridors for anti-Somaliland militias. ([Addis Standard](https://addisstandard.com/crumbling-of-rentier-fortress-ethiopias-maritime-pivot-decline-of-djiboutis-monopoly/))

**Displacement and refugee dynamics:** Djibouti hosts approximately 32,000 people forced to flee, including over 23,000 refugees and 8,000 asylum-seekers, primarily from Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen (as of January 2026). ([UNHCR](https://www.unhcr.org/where-we-work/countries/djibouti)) Around 85% reside in refugee villages such as Ali Addeh, Holl-Holl, and Markazi, with the remainder in urban areas, and women and children make up 67% of the displaced population. ([UNHCR](https://www.unhcr.org/where-we-work/countries/djibouti)) Despite recurrent droughts and limited resources, Djibouti has maintained an open-door policy and has been recognized as a regional leader in implementing the Comprehensive Refugee Response Framework, with the government taking steps to include refugees in national systems such as education, health care, and employment. ([UNHCR](https://www.unhcr.org/where-we-work/countries/djibouti))

**Sensitivities between government and development actors:** There has been no official reaction from the international community or regional organizations to the October 2025 constitutional amendments, though the Djiboutian League for Human Rights stated authorities are preparing "a lifetime presidency for Ismaïl Omar Guelleh." ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/news/voices/djiboutis-new-constitution-between-longevity-power-and-beginnings-socio-political-instability)) ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/news/voices/djiboutis-new-constitution-between-longevity-power-and-beginnings-socio-political-instability)) For many international partners, the stability of the Djibouti government has been viewed as a source of predictability in a volatile region, with limited external pressure for political reform, which has reinforced the stability of the current leadership. ([The Conversation](https://theconversation.com/djiboutis-democracy-takes-another-knock-as-ageing-president-engineers-yet-another-term-271009)) Journalists and activists who air criticism of Guelleh or the Union for a Presidential Majority are regularly harassed or arrested. ([Freedom House](https://freedomhouse.org/country/djibouti/freedom-world/2025)) Djibouti's alignment with Egypt and support for Somalia—moves perceived as undermining regional multipolarity—risk straining relations with development partners prioritizing democratic governance and regional stability.
