# FCV Portfolio Briefing: Djibouti

## 1. Constitutional Revision Enabling Indefinite Presidential Rule

In October 2025, Djibouti removed the presidential age limit through constitutional revision, enabling President Ismail Omar Guelleh—in power since 1999—to seek a sixth term in April 2026. This fundamental governance deterioration directly undermines institutional checks and democratic norms that are foundational to project implementation. The constitutional manipulation signals weakening rule-of-law predictability and creates political uncertainty that affects government counterpart reliability across the portfolio.

Project evidence demonstrates this risk is materializing across multiple initiatives. The PFM reform project (P180696) shows persistent "High" political and governance risk, with administrative delays in formalizing governance decrees despite ministerial approval—suggesting institutional prioritization toward consolidating executive power over routine development functions. The education project (P166059) identifies ministerial reshuffles as a "substantial" governance risk, with project coordination dependent on sustained political commitment that becomes fragile under constitutional instability. The corridor project (P174300) reports "Substantial" political and governance risk, with project governance structures vulnerable to political interference as executive power concentrates. Across the portfolio, institutional continuity and policy consistency—prerequisites for multi-year project success—are compromised by the governance environment that prioritizes regime consolidation over institutional development.

## 2. Severe Youth Unemployment and Economic Inequality

Youth unemployment in Djibouti exceeds 75%, compounded by high rates of inequality, poverty, and endemic corruption. This structural economic crisis creates domestic pressure and limits the absorptive capacity of development projects designed to address employment and livelihood outcomes. The TVET project (P175483) explicitly targets youth employment but faces fundamental constraints: with labor market absorption capacity severely limited and private sector demand minimal, training graduates cannot transition to formal employment. The refugee employment component reports zero achievement on employment outcomes (stuck at 5%), reflecting the broader structural inability of the economy to absorb even targeted training cohorts.

The economic inequality risk is materializing across social protection and livelihood initiatives. The emergency food security project (P178988) reports critical shortfalls in food stock acquisition (50% of target) and zero achievement on water infrastructure, directly undermining livelihood restoration for vulnerable populations. The slum upgrading project (P162901) identifies "substantial" risk from "non-transparent beneficiary selection," with youth beneficiary engagement threatened by economic desperation that reduces participation in project activities. The skills development initiative (P178836) acknowledges that youth unemployment dynamics create implementation friction, as beneficiaries facing severe economic pressure may prioritize immediate income generation over training participation. The portfolio evidence indicates that supply-side training interventions cannot overcome demand-side constraints in an economy with structural unemployment and limited job creation capacity.

## 3. Ethnic Tensions and Political Exclusion

Ethnic tensions are a primary driver of Djibouti's politics, with the Issa Somali ethnic group (approximately 60% of population) dominating governance while the Afar minority (approximately 35%) remains politically marginalized. This structural imbalance fuels grievances and limits inclusive political participation, creating institutional fragmentation that undermines project implementation across diverse constituencies.

Project evidence shows this risk is affecting beneficiary targeting and institutional coordination. The refugee integration project (P506969) identifies "substantial" risk from "tensions between host communities and refugees," with the project's dual beneficiary approach (12,860 refugees and 15,900 host residents) vulnerable to ethnic-based resource allocation disputes. The education project (P166059) notes that refugee targeting (29,214 refugees, 43% Somali origin) could become politically contested if ethnic tensions escalate, particularly given the Issa-Afar political imbalance. The CERP project (P509082) identifies ethnic tensions as affecting "equitable targeting and beneficiary reach," with marginalized groups potentially excluded from emergency response. The slum upgrading project (P162901) reports implementation challenges in "processus participatif" (participatory processes) that may reflect underlying ethnic divisions in beneficiary selection. Across the portfolio, ethnic marginalization of the Afar minority creates institutional legitimacy deficits that complicate inclusive governance and equitable benefit distribution, particularly in projects requiring broad stakeholder participation.

## 4. Collapse of Independent Civil Society and Media Freedom

Djibouti has virtually no independent civil society remaining, with only marginal voices present on social media. Freedom House classifies the country as Not Free, citing severe restrictions on political competition and media freedom, with journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested for criticizing the government. This institutional hollowing removes accountability mechanisms and transparency structures essential for project governance, stakeholder engagement, and institutional oversight.

The governance impact is evident across the portfolio. The PFM reform project (P180696) identifies "substantial" political risk requiring "citizen access to digital services" and "civil society engagement" components, but the PAD does not acknowledge that independent civil society is virtually non-existent. The project assumes civil society can meaningfully participate in oversight and engagement, but the country context shows "only marginal voices present on social media" and "journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested." This creates a fundamental implementation gap where project safeguards depend on institutional checks that do not exist. The education project (P166059) establishes a grievance mechanism "pour toutes parties prenantes" (for all stakeholders), but effectiveness is undermined by the absence of independent civil society to validate or amplify concerns. The GBV prevention project (P178332) reports critical gaps in psychosocial support capacity and NGO contract negotiations, reflecting the broader institutional hollowing where civil society service providers have been marginalized or constrained. The corridor project (P174300) documents systemic non-compliance with labor and social protection standards affecting 150 daily workers, enabled by the absence of independent unions, labor advocates, or media scrutiny that might otherwise expose and correct violations. Across the portfolio, the absence of independent oversight mechanisms creates permissive conditions for implementation gaps and governance failures to persist without external accountability or transparency.

## 5. FRUD-armé Insurgent Activity and Terrorism Threat

The splinter group FRUD-armé launched a raid on an army post in October 2023, killing seven soldiers and injuring four. Classified as a terrorist group by the National Assembly, FRUD-armé represents an ongoing domestic security threat amid Djibouti's proximity to al-Shabaab and other terrorist organizations in Somalia. This active insurgent presence creates direct operational risks to project implementation in rural and remote areas.

Project evidence indicates security risks are materializing in implementation. The TVET project (P175483) operates across six regions including Ali Sabieh and Obock, geographically proximate to areas of potential FRUD-armé activity. The PAD identifies "security (moderate)" risk focused narrowly on "refugee protection framework adequacy" but does not address operational security for training facilities or beneficiary access in conflict-adjacent zones. The refugee concentration in Ali-Addeh and Holl-Holl camps (Ali Sabieh region) places 3,165 direct beneficiaries in a region with documented insurgent activity. The corridor project (P174300) acknowledges "security/fragility (substantial)" risk from "regional instability" but does not specifically address FRUD-armé threat to road rehabilitation sites or border operations. The statistics project (P171777) explicitly identifies "security risks from regional instability" and notes the project operates in "conflict-affected areas," with field enumeration teams vulnerable to insurgent activity during census and household survey operations. The portfolio evidence indicates that while projects acknowledge regional security concerns, the specific threat from FRUD-armé's demonstrated capacity to conduct armed raids creates tangible operational risks that may disrupt implementation, staff safety, and beneficiary access in rural and remote zones.
