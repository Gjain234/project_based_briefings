# Ethiopia Portfolio FCV Briefing

## 1. Active Armed Conflict in Amhara and Oromia Regions

Ethiopia's two most populous regions remain engulfed in active armed conflict that is directly degrading portfolio performance. In Amhara, the Fano insurgency—involving multiple factions, some of which signed a partial peace agreement in December 2025 but many of which continue armed resistance with significant territorial control—has closed over 4,000 schools and displaced more than 4 million children from education. In Oromia, violence persists despite a December 2024 peace agreement with one OLA faction: over 25 civilians were killed in East Arsi Zone in October 2025 and 32 in a single day in August, with intercommunal violence between Oromo and Somali communities displacing over 288,000 people since July 2025. These conflicts are not background context—they are materializing as direct implementation failures across the portfolio. The rural roads project [P176303 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-a04969dd-32f6-453f-b2d3-310f5643586b.pdf) explicitly identifies "internal conflicts affecting project sites, staff safety, material transportation" as a substantial security risk and requires conflict-sensitive site selection across all 13 regions including Amhara. The PSNP [P172479 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-33203b57-3884-48f8-a304-7fc05ebf92c8.pdf) confirms that insecurity in Amhara and Oromia is preventing public works monitoring and follow-up, with unexploded ordnance in hotspot woredas blocking implementation entirely [P172479 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-33203b57-3884-48f8-a304-7fc05ebf92c8.pdf). The health project [P175167 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175167-992833b3-d61e-459e-9188-466f35c2618c.pdf) reports that only 2 of 262 conflict-affected health facilities have been restored, with data quality assessments impossible due to security constraints. The WASH project [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes) explicitly documents that internal conflict in Amhara is impeding staff movement and project implementation, with assets damaged and financial records destroyed in three Amhara woredas [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes). The energy access project [P171742 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171742-a6dffcd4-0989-414f-875d-c153cf49eb19.pdf) has seen its implementation rating decline to Moderately Unsatisfactory despite 80% of contracts under implementation, with mini-grid sites requiring security-driven replacements [P171742 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171742-45fbd416-22c6-4fe2-a001-21a06b2bddd9.pdf). Forward-looking risks are severe: with multiple Fano factions maintaining popular support in rural areas and OLA violence continuing despite partial agreements, there is no near-term prospect of stabilization sufficient to restore normal project operations in either region.

## 2. Mass Internal Displacement and Deteriorating Humanitarian Conditions

Ethiopia hosts between 3.3 and 4.7 million internally displaced persons—one of the world's largest IDP crises—with living conditions described as dire and the humanitarian funding environment shrinking critically. The WFP has been forced to suspend treatment for 650,000 malnourished women and children, and 3.6 million vulnerable people face loss of food assistance due to funding gaps driven in part by the Trump administration's foreign aid freeze. Over 21 million people require humanitarian assistance. This crisis is not peripheral to the portfolio—it is the operating context for the majority of projects. The PSNP [P172479 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087f8ffa9&stream=Yes) explicitly targets 4.23 million IDPs and acknowledges "ongoing internal displacements due to conflicts," yet ISR evidence confirms that IDP-specific mechanisms are failing: account opening for financial inclusion has stalled in 23 Tigray woredas [P172479 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-33203b57-3884-48f8-a304-7fc05ebf92c8.pdf), five contested woredas have been excluded from program coverage entirely [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf), and the EDRMC lacks institutional capacity to confirm beneficiary numbers for shock response disbursement [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf). The TVET project [P177881 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177881-02907b3f-0a5c-484e-b00e-136cb8695aad.pdf) has achieved only 0.76% IDP enrollment against a 35% target, with the PAD's commitment that "at least 15% of beneficiaries be IDPs" proving undeliverable in practice. The health project [P175167 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175167-992833b3-d61e-459e-9188-466f35c2618c.pdf) reports IDP clinics remain non-operational due to Ministry of Finance counter-funding delays, directly leaving displaced populations without healthcare. The WASH project [P167794 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086d2b493&stream=Yes) identifies IDPs as a vulnerable group requiring targeted approaches but faces implementation gaps in newly established regions with only 26–51% staffing levels [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes). The humanitarian funding crisis is also materializing as a macroeconomic constraint: the rural roads project [P176303 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176303-7b125857-00f3-4f1b-a25e-a0ad429dba62.pdf) maintains a "High" macroeconomic risk rating, and the PSNP [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf) faces currency depreciation eroding the real value of government matching fund contributions. The forward outlook is deeply concerning: with humanitarian funding declining and displacement continuing at current rates, the portfolio's ability to reach intended beneficiaries and demonstrate results is fundamentally compromised.

## 3. Political Crisis in Tigray and Fragile Peace Process in Northern Regions

Tigray faces a compounding political and security crisis that threatens to unravel the fragile 2022 Pretoria Agreement. The TPLF's deregistration from the June 2026 elections—with the National Election Board recognizing a rival party while excluding the TPLF—has been labeled a "clear declaration of war" by Tigrayan leaders. Federal authority is rapidly shrinking in Mekelle and other key cities, tensions between the TPLF and Tigray Defense Forces are escalating, and Eritrean troops continue to commit sexual violence in occupied areas of Tigray while Tigrayans are being forcibly displaced from Western Tigray. The pending referendum on Western Tigray's status remains unresolved. These dynamics are materializing across the portfolio in ways that go beyond access constraints. The WASH project [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes) documents asset loss and inability to verify financial records in Tigray, with auditors unable to access the region; the project cannot assess asset status or damages in Amhara and Afar [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes). The PSNP [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf) has excluded five contested woredas from program coverage, and the Early Warning System dashboard remains inaccessible to prioritization committee institutions [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf). The urban recovery project [P169943 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P169943-fc642666-20d5-4d3e-950f-b6a65b97177a.pdf) reports that recovery grant distribution to IDPs in Mekelle is delayed, with implementation modalities requiring adaptation to the fragile post-conflict environment [P169943 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P169943-fc642666-20d5-4d3e-950f-b6a65b97177a.pdf). The digital project [P171034 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08847bca4&stream=Yes) explicitly excludes Tigray from coverage due to the ongoing conflict and telecommunications blackout, meaning approximately 5–6 million Tigrayans are excluded from a project designed to increase digital inclusion for "all citizens of Ethiopia." The TVET project [P177881 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-7e6589de-f796-4cc4-8c29-ff61d3c2e295.pdf) targets Tigray for Centers of Excellence and IDP inclusion, but the PAD's acknowledgment that "peace agreement reached November 2022 but volatility continues" understates the current crisis severity. With the TPLF's electoral exclusion creating conditions for renewed armed conflict within a 0–3 month horizon, projects dependent on federal-regional coordination in Tigray face acute governance disruption risk.

## 4. Humanitarian Funding Crisis and Macroeconomic Deterioration

Ethiopia's humanitarian funding crisis has reached a critical threshold that is directly constraining portfolio implementation capacity. The combination of the Trump administration's foreign aid freeze, reduced international donor support, and Ethiopia's own fiscal constraints—characterized by high inflation, foreign exchange shortages, and currency depreciation since July 2024—is creating a resource environment in which both government counterpart funding commitments and complementary humanitarian services are failing simultaneously. The rural roads project [P176303 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P176303-7b125857-00f3-4f1b-a25e-a0ad429dba62.pdf) maintains a "High" macroeconomic risk rating with persistent concerns about government counterpart funding availability (US$107.3 million required), and the WASH project [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes) reports delayed government matching fund contributions (US$50 million required) with the government unable to meet its obligations. The digital project [P171034 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171034-2b31c1cd-3768-4621-a1dd-4599e54b1019.pdf) documents customs duty issues doubling contract prices, Birr depreciation reducing purchasing power, and the government unilaterally reallocating US$30 million in project funds away from digital job creation. The PSNP [P172479 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P172479-521e2e7f-369f-4f5b-b998-4d81919b9b17.pdf) faces macroeconomic instability with high ETB/USD conversion rates eroding the real value of safety net transfers, while the humanitarian funding crisis is manifesting as a direct implementation constraint: WFP's suspension of operations in Amhara has disrupted humanitarian access for over 500,000 people in areas where multiple projects operate. The TVET project [P177881 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177881-02907b3f-0a5c-484e-b00e-136cb8695aad.pdf) reports that Ministry of Finance counter-funding delays are preventing the establishment of mobile health teams and operationalization of IDP clinics [P175167 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175167-992833b3-d61e-459e-9188-466f35c2618c.pdf). The livestock project [P159382 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P159382-d6896db2-88d8-43da-9163-01d9c113b5ec.pdf) maintains a "High" political and governance risk rating with institutional capacity constraints directly linked to the broader resource environment. Forward-looking risks are compounded by the June 2026 election cycle, which is likely to divert government attention and resources from development commitments, and by the severe cost-of-living crisis that is fueling insurgency recruitment and reducing household economic resilience across project beneficiary populations.

## 5. Cross-Border Spillover from Sudan and Regional Instability, and Legitimacy Concerns Surrounding the June 2026 Elections

Two interconnected forward-looking risks are converging to create a deteriorating operating environment for the portfolio. First, the Sudan conflict has driven over 163,000 individuals into Ethiopia, with more than 50,000 new refugee arrivals in 2025 alone, while South Sudanese opposition forces have conducted cross-border incursions in Gambella region. Ethiopia hosts over one million refugees amid growing political instability and funding shortfalls that undermine protection capacity. This is materializing directly: the urban safety net project [P169943 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P169943-fc642666-20d5-4d3e-950f-b6a65b97177a.pdf) reports that refugee program dropout in Pugnido (Gambella) required re-targeting of 3,175 beneficiaries due to insecurity and forced relocation; the project cannot establish reliable refugee targeting data for Addis Ababa's urban refugee population [P169943 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P169943-fc642666-20d5-4d3e-950f-b6a65b97177a.pdf); and refugee livelihood participation stands at only 6% against a 30% target [P178047 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178047-b0d0fe8e-7b17-4207-831a-5f4c80491177.pdf). The pastoral livelihoods project [P180076 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180076-b359290e-2e82-42ba-a499-e9b275ae75f5.pdf) confirms that Gambella and Benishangul Gumuz are inaccessible to World Bank supervision due to cross-border security threats. Second, the June 2026 elections carry high risk of triggering broader instability: insecurity may prevent voting in several regions, the TPLF's exclusion threatens Tigrayan participation, major opposition parties may boycott as they did in 2021, and the government is conducting systematic crackdowns on independent media (44 journalists detained in 2025) and civil society. These dynamics are already materializing as governance fragility across the portfolio: the TVET project [P177881 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177881-02907b3f-0a5c-484e-b00e-136cb8695aad.pdf) maintains a "Substantial" political and governance risk rating with the Project Steering Committee meeting only minimally [P177881 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177881-a2d54917-aedd-4d6b-bdb7-01bbf1399378.pdf); the digital project [P171034 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171034-3cd75dbd-57b9-4dc3-9518-8a20ac754002.pdf) shows persistent "Substantial" governance risk with Ethio Telecom resisting regulatory reform [P171034 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171034-2b31c1cd-3768-4621-a1dd-4599e54b1019.pdf); and the WASH project [P167794 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P167794-f8ff51f0-6541-4d6a-944c-966e7779455e.pdf) documents woreda-level leadership reshuffling and staff turnover driven by political instability [P167794 – Aide Memoire](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef997b&stream=Yes). The combination of cross-border refugee pressure, pre-election political repression, and the severe cost-of-living crisis creates conditions in which project governance structures, community engagement mechanisms, and independent monitoring capacity are all simultaneously under threat.
