# FCV Portfolio Briefing: Djibouti

## 1. Democratic Backsliding and Institutional Erosion

Djibouti's governance landscape has undergone fundamental deterioration through constitutional manipulation that removes institutional checks on executive power. In October 2025, the government eliminated the presidential age limit, enabling President Ismail Omar Guelleh—in power since 1999—to pursue indefinite re-election. This constitutional revision represents more than procedural change; it signals the systematic dismantling of institutional constraints that normally provide governance predictability and rule-of-law protections essential for development implementation. [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) [P180696 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf) [P174461 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-c15be353-62d2-4f55-928e-a77385dc1393.pdf)

Projects across the portfolio face direct implementation consequences from this institutional weakening. Government counterpart reliability has deteriorated as political consolidation supersedes institutional development priorities. The removal of democratic constraints creates uncertainty around policy continuity and administrative capacity, particularly as the government focuses institutional attention on consolidating power ahead of April 2026 elections rather than sustaining development commitments. [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) [P180696 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf) The persistent "High" political and governance risk ratings across multiple projects reflect this fundamental governance fragility, where constitutional manipulation has undermined the institutional predictability required for multi-year project execution.

Evidence of this risk materializing is substantial. Project governance structures remain incomplete, with steering committees inactive and regulatory board appointments delayed since 2022. [P174461 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-c15be353-62d2-4f55-928e-a77385dc1393.pdf) Administrative processes that should be routine—such as publishing ministerial decrees required for project operationalization—face unexplained delays, suggesting institutional prioritization toward political consolidation over development implementation. [P180696 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-78d5caca-0d39-443d-8d7b-742a76e39ce8.pdf) The governance environment has become sufficiently unstable that project teams report difficulty maintaining government engagement and securing timely institutional decisions.

## 2. Collapse of Accountability Mechanisms and Civil Society

Djibouti has virtually eliminated independent civil society and severely restricted media freedom, creating a governance environment where institutional accountability mechanisms have been systematically dismantled. Freedom House classifies the country as "Not Free," with journalists and activists regularly harassed or arrested for criticizing the government. This institutional hollowing directly undermines project implementation by removing the transparency, stakeholder engagement, and external oversight mechanisms that normally support development effectiveness. [P174461 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-9e33bd55-6107-428e-bde8-2dc02b8b55cf.pdf) [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) [P174300 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf)

The absence of independent oversight manifests across the portfolio as weak project governance, compromised safeguards implementation, and limited accountability for institutional performance. Projects targeting vulnerable populations—refugees, persons with disabilities, youth—face particular implementation challenges because civil society organizations that typically advocate for vulnerable group inclusion and monitor equitable service delivery have been marginalized or eliminated. [P175483 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf) [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) Without functioning civil society intermediaries, projects struggle to conduct authentic community consultation, establish credible grievance mechanisms, or maintain transparent stakeholder engagement.

Realized risks demonstrate that this governance constraint is actively impeding project delivery. Environmental and social safeguards implementation lags significantly, with grievance redress mechanisms non-functional and stakeholder mobilization documentation sparse. [P174461 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-9e33bd55-6107-428e-bde8-2dc02b8b55cf.pdf) [P178988 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-79f711cb-4676-4798-95e4-844f5162676d.pdf) Labor compliance violations on construction sites persist without independent monitoring or media scrutiny to expose and pressure remediation. [P174300 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-1e1324d5-1aa7-4175-a5a6-411c6205fe3f.pdf) The project targeting gender-based violence prevention reports critical gaps in psychosocial support capacity and delayed safeguards monitoring—gaps that would normally be flagged by independent civil society oversight but persist unchecked in the current governance environment. ## 3. Severe Youth Unemployment and Economic Inequality Creating Domestic Pressure

Djibouti faces a critical youth employment crisis with unemployment exceeding 75%, compounded by endemic corruption, high poverty rates, and severe economic inequality. This structural economic fragility creates significant domestic pressure, particularly among a young population with few legitimate economic prospects. The economic desperation underlying this risk directly affects project implementation by constraining human capital availability, reducing institutional capacity, and creating vulnerability to exploitation among beneficiary populations. [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf) [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) [P508560 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf)

The economic inequality and limited opportunity context directly undermines project effectiveness in addressing employment and livelihood outcomes. Skills training initiatives targeting youth employment face structural barriers: the labor market lacks sufficient formal sector jobs to absorb trained workers, and refugees competing for the same limited opportunities face compounded barriers. [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf) The project targeting refugee employment outcomes has stalled at 5% achievement against an 80% target, reflecting the reality that training alone cannot overcome the absence of viable employment pathways in Djibouti's constrained economy. [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf)

Evidence of this risk materializing is pervasive across implementation. Projects struggle to identify viable income-generating activities in remote rural areas where market access is severely limited. [P508560 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf) [P180999 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf) Informal traders and vulnerable populations accept exploitative working conditions—non-compliant contracts, lack of social security registration—because youth unemployment creates minimal bargaining power. ## 4. Refugee Hosting Amid Severe Resource Constraints

Djibouti maintains an open-door policy toward displaced populations while hosting approximately 32,000 refugees and asylum-seekers (predominantly from Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen) amid recurrent droughts and severely limited resources. Eighty-five percent of this refugee population resides in remote villages with fragile infrastructure and minimal public service capacity. This humanitarian hosting burden creates acute strain on already-fragile public services and infrastructure, directly undermining the government's capacity to deliver development services to both refugee and host populations. [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) [P162901 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes) [P171777 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171777-8dfe1d10-65dd-4d7a-96a0-701ceb6e77ed.pdf)

The resource constraint dynamics directly manifest in project implementation gaps targeting refugee populations. Health service delivery to refugees has deteriorated significantly, with immunization rates for refugee children at 50% against an 80% target, and vitamin A supplementation declining from 10% to 5%. [P178033 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178033-d78154cd-d774-432b-a705-9636c8a8a3b0.pdf) Education enrollment for refugee students has collapsed from 596 to 384 between 2021-2025, reflecting the strain on education infrastructure serving both host and refugee populations. [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) The project targeting refugee skills development and employment reports zero enrollment for refugees in online gig work training and minimal progress on labor mobility agreements—implementation failures rooted in the institutional fragility and resource scarcity that characterize the refugee hosting environment. [P175483 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf)

Realized risks demonstrate that resource constraints are actively limiting service delivery to vulnerable populations. Data collection challenges on refugee households in census and household survey operations reflect the logistical complexity and institutional strain of managing large displaced populations in resource-constrained settings. [P171777 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0884c1bc2&stream=Yes) The project targeting refugee property rights documentation reports stalled land regularization since October 2023, with pending restructuring plans in refugee villages remaining unapproved. ## 5. Ethnic Tensions and Political Exclusion Creating Institutional Fragmentation

Djibouti's politics are structured around ethnic divisions, with the Issa Somali ethnic group (approximately 60% of population) dominating governance while the Afar minority (approximately 35%) remains systematically marginalized from political participation and resource allocation. This structural ethnic imbalance fuels grievances, undermines institutional legitimacy, and creates political fragmentation that directly impairs project implementation capacity. Inclusive institutional coordination and equitable service delivery—both essential for development effectiveness—are compromised when governance structures systematically exclude significant population segments. [P174300 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174300-6fc17b17-9f69-4b50-938f-fac14f07b6b3.pdf) [P162901 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes) [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf)

The ethnic exclusion dynamics create particular vulnerability in projects targeting vulnerable populations or requiring broad stakeholder participation. School management committees function at only 62% capacity, suggesting weak community governance structures that may reflect ethnic power imbalances in local decision-making. [P166059 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P166059-1c484167-6c9c-4c60-bb6c-a5a8d9a85a29.pdf) The project targeting refugee and host community integration reports persistent tensions between refugee beneficiaries and host populations, with no documented coordination mechanisms to manage inter-group friction—a critical gap given that refugee populations (predominantly Somali) may interact with ethnically divided host communities where Afar minorities face political marginalization. [P178988 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178988-40bca9d9-77f1-4080-b0cc-d5ba5676b10e.pdf) Projects targeting vulnerable populations including persons with disabilities report implementation gaps in transparent beneficiary selection, creating risk that opaque program access could reproduce existing ethnic inequities in resource allocation. [P508560 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf) [P180999 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P178992-7c9d64c3-b649-4740-bf35-769304d92e5f.pdf)

Evidence of this risk materializing includes weak institutional capacity to serve marginalized populations equitably. The project targeting education reports fragmentation in the education system, with private Arabic schools (often serving minority communities) operating outside government data collection systems, suggesting institutional distrust rooted in ethnic and community-based exclusion dynamics.
