Human capital (basic education, skills/TVET, and refugee inclusion) is a core FCV-relevant cluster, but it is exposed to heightened insecurity and social fragmentation risks—particularly if sectarian violence in Djibouti City constrains access to centralized training and administration, or if insecurity in outlying regions constrains school access and verification. The PADs anticipate delivery constraints in refugee-hosting areas (e.g., limited internet access affecting ICT training) and cross-ministry coordination failures that can stall training reforms [P175483 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=IBArchive-38b35d89-2c5c-4636-ab7e-0ba662b95a5f.pdf), alongside the operational fragility of results-based processes that depend on formal ministerial decisions and the concentration of key training functions in the capital [P166059 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087ef2534&stream=Yes). These risks are already materializing in implementation: refugee outcomes are not being delivered as intended (refugee TVET enrollment and work-based learning participation remain at zero) and refugee outcome data are not yet available for monitoring [P175483 – Implementation Status and Results Report](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-ac50c771-2198-4714-8f62-c0065e446f6c.pdf). Governance and safeguards execution is also showing strain, with an inactive steering committee and delayed operationalization of agreed GBV prevention/response measures [P175483 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P175483-22796ae4-2f59-4fa6-af78-41747fb3e346.pdf), which is particularly concerning in a context of constrained civic space and elevated election-period fragility.

Social protection, basic services, and local resilience (safety nets, social registry, and community-driven investments in underserved/refugee-hosting regions) form a second cluster, with high exposure to political tensions, constrained accountability space, and rising social cohesion risks where benefit targeting is contested. The PAD flags substantial social risks (elite capture, social conflict, and exclusion errors in targeting cash/nutrition and registry activities) [P506969 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf), and highlights political/governance and fiduciary vulnerabilities, including line-ministry interference and demanding coordination requirements across implementing entities [P506969 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-87c444de-4797-4bf9-b654-4932a7fb0112.pdf). While no project-specific ISR evidence is provided here for these operations, systemic realized governance bottlenecks already affecting donor-funded reforms signal practical execution risks for safety-net scale-up and registry strengthening (e.g., delayed operationalization of PFM reform governance structures affecting related projects) [P180696 – Implementation Status and Results Report](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-38a76556-6182-48de-9bfe-ad27e73ddd21.pdf). In an environment of mounting political uncertainty and constrained oversight, these cross-cutting coordination and accountability weaknesses increase the likelihood that social assistance delivery and community investments become slower, more contested, or less equitable than designed.

Food security and crisis-response stabilization (strategic food reserves and emergency activation mechanisms) is a third cluster that directly intersects with FCV pathways, as price spikes and shortages can amplify unrest amid Djibouti City tensions and pre-election fragility. The PAD explicitly frames benefits around preventing demonstrations and broader instability linked to supply shocks (“food riots”) and emphasizes the need to manage regional conflict-related supply disruption risks and stakeholder engagement limitations during preparation [P178988 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-ab0d00ef-27b4-4e55-b4d9-d19643e5cfb2.pdf). In parallel, the crisis-response design recognizes displacement pressures that strain public services and complicate beneficiary reach during activations [P509082 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf), and flags high institutional capacity risks where key coordinating entities lack staff/funding and PIU systems are untested for rapid activation [P509082 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P509082-335ace4f-041e-4d8b-8b24-01c7157e4078.pdf). Realized public-sector decision and coordination delays—evidenced by slow formalization/operationalization of governance arrangements in the PFM reform space—underline that, in practice, time-critical actions (procurement, releases, and multi-agency coordination) could be delayed when speed is most needed [P180696 – Implementation Status and Results Report](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-38a76556-6182-48de-9bfe-ad27e73ddd21.pdf). This is particularly salient given the country’s exposure to regional insecurity and domestic political tension that can crowd out administrative bandwidth.

Infrastructure, urban development, and housing/land (transport corridors, affordable housing finance, resettlement/tenure, and city systems under migration pressure) is a fourth cluster, highly sensitive to both insecurity and governance constraints. The corridor PAD highlights fragility-driven implementation conditionality for cross-border collaboration, procurement risks affected by the regional security context, and private sector concerns around political/payment risk in long-term contracting [P174300 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088bdcd4b&stream=Yes); housing finance operations similarly depend on sustained high-level coordination across multiple institutions and are shaped by refugee/migrant-driven slum expansion pressures that complicate targeting and affordability outcomes [P176772 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-38023395-68a8-4eb6-8dd3-05bbe5a8c9ed.pdf). Urban upgrading/land operations face additional PAD-flagged risks of excluding non-citizens/refugees from planning and tenure tools and reliance on government financing for off-site connectivity—both vulnerable under political volatility and fiscal/administrative bottlenecks [P162901 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b086ac6df2&stream=Yes). Implementation is already encountering a concrete governance/administrative blockage in land regularization, where case files have been stalled due to internal data-migration prioritization, slowing delivery of land certificates and undermining tenure-related results [P162901 – Implementation Status and Results Report](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0870d5571&stream=Yes). In the current risk environment (urban social tensions, constrained civic space, and election-related uncertainty), such bottlenecks can more readily translate into heightened contestation around resettlement, beneficiary selection, and infrastructure siting.

Governance, public financial management, statistics, and the enabling regulatory environment (including digital sector rulemaking) is the fifth cluster and a binding constraint across the portfolio, especially under rising political tensions linked to constitutional changes, constrained democratic space, and the 2026 electoral cycle. PADs flag weak fiduciary/oversight environments and limited non-state participation, alongside risks to the autonomy and credibility of core state functions such as statistics production and dissemination [P171777 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087c0f9e4&stream=Yes), and warn that reform progress can be derailed by limited consensus and potential cabinet changes that undermine continuity [P180696 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-28d37d68-d0ab-4355-acca-fed683f4b387.pdf). These risks have already materialized as delays in signing/publishing required legal instruments that have postponed project effectiveness and slowed reform execution, and as delays in operationalizing PFM reform governance structures that are affecting related donor-funded initiatives [P180696 – Implementation Status and Results Report](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P180696-38a76556-6182-48de-9bfe-ad27e73ddd21.pdf). Governance frictions are also evident in the data and coordination space (efforts to “reboot” fragmented aid coordination) [P171777 – Implementation Status and Results Report](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P171777-4b12f487-05cf-4472-bbf3-012c7cb41ce1.pdf), and in sector rulemaking where the non-adoption of the Code du Numérique continues to delay dependent activities and weakens the telecom regulatory framework in practice [P174461 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P174461-a6aeb6a6-887c-41f9-8eb7-fb31c9e7d652.pdf). Collectively, these realized delays reinforce that, in the current FCV context, implementation risk is less about technical design and more about the speed, predictability, and inclusiveness of state decision-making and inter-institutional coordination.
