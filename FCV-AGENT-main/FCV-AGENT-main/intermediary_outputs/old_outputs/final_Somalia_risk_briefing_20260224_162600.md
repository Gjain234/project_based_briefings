Severe drought-linked food insecurity remains an acute country risk that can quickly overwhelm nascent resilience gains and force an operational pivot from systems-building to crisis response. Projects supporting household resilience and service delivery are exposed because the operating context is characterized by recurrent climate and security shocks that “erode resilience” and constrain delivery capacity in climate-vulnerable areas [P507443 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-00f4f571-1823-453b-9c9d-8aeb7c1ee72e.pdf). This risk is already materializing: the urban recovery portfolio reports that a major drought shock triggered activation of emergency response mechanisms and reoriented implementation toward drought response, crowding out planned investments [P170922 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087783da4&stream=Yes). Looking ahead, continued drought conditions and rising needs can further reduce beneficiary ability to participate in economic inclusion/livelihood activities and can raise costs and complexity for implementation in drought-affected districts [P507443 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-00f4f571-1823-453b-9c9d-8aeb7c1ee72e.pdf).  

Large-scale displacement is a structural FCV risk that disrupts targeting, strains urban services, and complicates monitoring and continuity of benefits as populations move between rural areas, IDP settlements, and cities. Several operations are intentionally directed to high in-migration districts, making them inherently sensitive to rapid shifts in settlement patterns and beneficiary profiles [P507443 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-00f4f571-1823-453b-9c9d-8aeb7c1ee72e.pdf). This risk is clearly materializing in implementation: an urban program reports drought-induced internal displacement increasing the share of IDP beneficiaries in Mogadishu, Baidoa, and Garowe and intensifying pressure on municipal services and delivery capacity [P170922 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b087783da4&stream=Yes), with further reporting of displacement-related secondary movements reshaping demand in project locations [P170922 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P170922-8ebda4aa-5070-41fa-b865-72e4172bb0b8.pdf). Forward-looking, continued displacement will heighten risks of exclusion/elite capture, weaken community engagement, and increase the likelihood of grievances and site-level disruptions as competition over land and services intensifies [P170922 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b08735b2ef&stream=Yes).  

Disputes over electoral direction and constitutional reforms are a high political risk that can deepen polarization, stall decision-making, and reduce the predictability of intergovernmental cooperation needed for national programs. The portfolio is susceptible because multiple projects rely on sustained federal–member state coordination to implement reforms and harmonized systems, in a context where the federal and member state levels “continue to disagree on core constitutional provisions” and resource distribution [P507443 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-00f4f571-1823-453b-9c9d-8aeb7c1ee72e.pdf). The risk is materializing in reform delivery: fiscal governance operations report election-linked dynamics slowing or stalling approvals required from federal and member state parliaments for harmonized tax reforms [P173731 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P173731-b8cf0b3f-7b60-4926-bbe4-93675685b15a.pdf), and political tensions delaying adoption of an intergovernmental policy instrument on functional and revenue assignment [P173731 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P173731-2e907ab5-ba94-464c-ad53-4736db5c7867.pdf). Going forward, prolonged reform contestation increases the likelihood of further delays in approvals, weakened ownership, and stop-start implementation as political attention shifts toward contested reforms and electoral timelines [P173731 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0879e0e01&stream=Yes).  

Federal–member state confrontation is a distinct risk from broader polarization, with direct implications for funds flows, implementation permissions, and the ability to operate consistently across jurisdictions—particularly where member-state participation is a condition for execution. Projects are exposed because their implementation models depend on functional cooperation and formal agreements between the center and states; where these are absent, activities and disbursements can stall [P177627 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-63dd48bb-2de2-45f6-884c-8c9a581a5515.pdf). This risk is already materializing in multiple ways: a government systems operation reports two-year withholding of transfers to Jubaland, directly undermining predictability of intergovernmental fiscal arrangements [P173731 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P173731-b8cf0b3f-7b60-4926-bbe4-93675685b15a.pdf), while an infrastructure/services operation reports implementation blocked pending finalization of a required federal–Puntland subsidiary agreement [P177627 – ISR](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P177627-fbbfa6e1-4f42-4b2f-985f-98a91aaca38e.pdf). Forward-looking, continued confrontation raises the probability of further interruptions in transfers, delayed workplan approvals, and uneven geographic coverage as projects avoid or are unable to engage in politically contested jurisdictions [P173731 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b0879e0e01&stream=Yes).  

Persistent insecurity driven by insurgent activity and ongoing conflict dynamics remains a pervasive risk that constrains access, raises costs, and can abruptly halt civil works, supervision, and beneficiary-facing delivery. The portfolio is structurally susceptible because “security risks and extremist-instigated violence often make parts of the country inaccessible,” affecting both implementation and supervision [P176898 – PAD](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088ddf55e&stream=Yes). This risk is materializing across operations: infrastructure programs report inability to complete works due to insecurity and resulting implementation delays [P173315 – ISR](http://wbdocsservices.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&RENDITION=Y&TYPE=T&I4_DOCID=090224b088a396a8&stream=Yes), and the urban portfolio reports a terror attack near a project site in Kismayo, disrupting field conditions and elevating duty-of-care requirements [P170922 – Aide Memoire](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=P170922-8ebda4aa-5070-41fa-b865-72e4172bb0b8.pdf). Looking ahead, continued attack risk and kinetic operations will likely increase reliance on remote monitoring and security management plans, slow procurement and mobilization, and reduce hands-on oversight—raising execution and safeguard risks even where projects remain nominally active [P507443 – PAD](https://spfilesapi.worldbank.org/services?I4_SERVICE=FILE_URLS2&SITE_KEY=DRPSINT&I4_DOCID=BOSIB-00f4f571-1823-453b-9c9d-8aeb7c1ee72e.pdf).
