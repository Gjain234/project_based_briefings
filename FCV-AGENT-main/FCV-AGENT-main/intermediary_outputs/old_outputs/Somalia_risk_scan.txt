**Risk Overview:** Somalia faces a convergence of acute crises at a critical political juncture. Nearly 6.5 million people in Somalia are facing severe hunger as worsening drought, conflict and global aid cuts intensify the country's humanitarian crisis, with 6.5 million people projected to face crisis or worse levels of food insecurity by the end of March ([Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/feb/24/un-data-shows-65-million-people-risk-severe-hunger-drought/)). Simultaneously, efforts to amend the country's provisional constitution, establish a new electoral system and centralize executive power within the presidency are deepening mistrust between federal member states—especially Puntland and Jubaland—and the Federal Government of Somalia at a time when the security threat posed by al Shabaab requires a united front ([Africa Center](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/en-elections-2026/somalia/)). Since January 2025, al Shabaab has re-entered large swaths of territory in central Somalia which Somali security forces previously captured, enabling it to relink its support zones in central and southern Somalia for the first time since 2022 ([Critical Threats](https://www.criticalthreats.org/analysis/al-shabaabs-area-of-operations)). Unresolved governance disputes, persistent terrorist pressure, and humanitarian collapse create a trifecta of state fragility with direct implications for development programming and allied engagement.

**Institutional fragility, political tensions, and social unrest:** High-level talks between Somalia's federal government and the opposition-aligned Somali Future Council resumed in late February 2026 amid continuing disagreements over the country's electoral direction, as the government's term nears its final months ([Hiiraan Online](https://www.hiiraan.com/news4/2026/Feb/204489/federal_government_opposition_resume_talks_as_election_dispute_deepens.aspx)). President Hassan Sheikh favors advancing toward a one-person, one-vote system, while opposition leaders propose returning to the indirect system used in previous cycles but expanding the number of delegates, with opposition leaders proposing returns to the indirect model while the president's camp argues Somalia must move forward with preparations for direct elections ([Hiiraan Online](https://www.hiiraan.com/news4/2026/Feb/204489/federal_government_opposition_resume_talks_as_election_dispute_deepens.aspx)). Fighting between federal troops and Jubaland forces in December 2024 and mid-2025 killed dozens and forced hundreds of government soldiers to flee into Kenya, with Jubaland President Ahmed Madobe traveling to Garowe in June 2025 to cement an alliance with Puntland leader Said Abdullahi Deni against Mohamud's reforms ([Hiiraan Online](https://www.hiiraan.com/news4/2025/Sept/203062/icg_somalia_s_2026_election_reforms_trigger_standoff_raising_fears_of_renewed_unrest.aspx)). In January 2026, the FGS announced cancellation of all bilateral security and defense agreements with the UAE, citing national sovereignty concerns ([Africa Center](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/en-elections-2026/somalia/)).

**Conflict and violence dynamics:** On February 10, Somali Danab Special Forces, in coordination with Jubaland State security forces, conducted a targeted operation against Al-Shabaab militants in areas around Jamaame in Lower Juba region, resulting in 14 militants killed ([Defcon Level](https://www.defconlevel.com/alert/2026-02-10-somali-forces-eliminate-14-al-shabaab-militants-in-lower-juba-amid-us-airstrikes-against-isis-and-al-shabaab)) ([Defcon Level](https://www.defconlevel.com/alert/2026-02-10-somali-forces-eliminate-14-al-shabaab-militants-in-lower-juba-amid-us-airstrikes-against-isis-and-al-shabaab)). U.S. Africa Command conducted airstrikes targeting al-Shabaab on Feb. 15, 2026, and Feb. 9, 2026 in the vicinity of southern Somalia, approximately 51 km northeast of Kismayo ([AFRICOM](https://www.africom.mil/pressrelease/36223/us-forces-conduct-strike-targeting-al-shabaab)). However, al-Shabaab has ramped up its operations, launching around 50 percent more attacks per month in 2025 compared to its 2024 average in the Hiraan and Middle Shabelle regions ([Soufan Center](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2025-april-2/)). Continued fragmentation within the federal system creates space for Al-Shabaab to maneuver both militarily and ideologically ([African Security Analysis](https://www.africansecurityanalysis.org/reports/al-shabaab-resurgence-in-somalia-strategic-shifts-and-implications)).

**Displacement and refugee dynamics:** As of 31 December 2025, Somalia is hosting 46,668 refugees and asylum-seekers, with women and children representing 65% of the total population ([UNHCR](https://data.unhcr.org/en/country/som)). Conflict and drought have displaced nearly a quarter of a million people in 2025, raising the total to almost 4 million ([WFP](https://www.wfp.org/emergencies/somalia-emergency)) ([WFP](https://www.wfp.org/emergencies/somalia-emergency)). Drought and conflict displaced about 278,000 people between July and December, disrupting agricultural production, market access and aid delivery ([Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/feb/24/un-data-shows-65-million-people-risk-severe-hunger-drought/)). The 2025 Humanitarian Response Plan has received only 24% of its required funding ([The New Humanitarian](https://www.thenewhumanitarian.org/opinion/2026/02/19/somalia-facing-disastrous-drought-not-time-weaponise-aid)).

**Sensitivities between government and development actors:** The World Food Programme is sounding the alarm that its lifesaving emergency food and nutrition assistance in Somalia is at imminent risk of stopping without new immediate funding commitments, with resources expected to be depleted within weeks ([WFP USA](https://wfpusa.org/news/wfp-warns-of-catastrophic-shortfalls-in-somalia/)). Politics is blocking basic operational logic, with humanitarian teams in northern Somalia inhibited from traveling between Garowe and Las Anod ([The New Humanitarian](https://www.thenewhumanitarian.org/opinion/2026/02/19/somalia-facing-disastrous-drought-not-time-weaponise-aid)). Development partnerships face operational constraints as political tensions restrict humanitarian access and federal cohesion fractures ahead of elections, necessitating urgent international diplomatic intervention on both governance and security fronts.
